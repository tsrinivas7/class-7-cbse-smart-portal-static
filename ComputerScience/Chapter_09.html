Short Revision Notes

  • India is a Union of States with one Union Government and several State governments
  • The Union Government is more powerful than State Governments
  • There are 28 States in the Indian Union, each with a Legislature
  • The State Legislature consists of either one (unicameral) or two (bicameral) houses
  • The lower house is the Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha), the upper house is the Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad)
  • Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs) are directly elected by adult citizens through universal adult franchise
  • The number of MLAs ranges from 60 to 500, with smaller states having fewer members
  • The party winning the majority of seats in the Vidhan Sabha forms the government
  • The party without majority becomes the opposition
  • The tenure of the Vidhan Sabha is five years, but it can be dissolved earlier by the Governor on the advice of the Chief Minister
  • The Governor can dissolve the Assembly during a constitutional emergency under Article 356
  • A motion of no confidence can lead to the dissolution of the Assembly
  • The Speaker presides over the Vidhan Sabha and maintains order
  • The Chief Minister is appointed by the Governor from the majority party in the Legislative Assembly
  • The Chief Minister appoints other ministers on their advice
  • The Legislative Council is partly elected and partly nominated, with members serving for six years
  • One-third of the members retire every two years
  • The Governor appoints members of the Legislative Council, including those nominated for special knowledge in arts, science, literature, etc.
  • The Legislative Council has no power to reject bills originated in the Vidhan Sabha
  • The Legislative Council can return a bill for recommendations within three months, but cannot suggest changes after the second stage
  • Money Bills can only be introduced in the Vidhan Sabha with prior consent from the Governor
  • The Vidhan Sabha has more power in passing ordinary bills than the Legislative Council
  • The State Legislature makes laws on subjects in the State List (e.g., police, agriculture, land revenue) and on subjects in the Concurrent List (with Parliament's law prevailing in case of conflict)
  • The State Legislature passes the state budget and can pass ordinances when the Assembly is not in session
  • The Governor is the constitutional head of the state, appointed by the President for five years
  • The Governor can dissolve the Assembly, promulgate ordinances, and grant pardons
  • The Governor can act independently in discretionary powers, such as appointing a Chief Minister in a hung assembly
  • The Governor is accountable to the President and acts as an agent of the Central Government
  • The Chief Minister is responsible to the Legislative Assembly and can ask ministers to resign
  • The Chief Minister allocates portfolios to ministers and can remove them at any time
  • The Vidhan Parishad is not present in all states, with some states having only a unicameral legislature
  • The real head of a democratic country is the people, represented through elected bodies
  • The presiding officer of the Legislative Assembly is the Speaker
  • A majority means more than half of the total seats in a body
  • The legislative process involves three readings for ordinary bills: introduction, second reading, and third reading
  • The degree of a polynomial is the highest power of any variable in the expression
  • Like terms have the same algebraic factors; unlike terms have different algebraic factors
  • A monomial has one term, a binomial has two terms, a trinomial has three terms, and a polynomial has one or more terms
  • The coefficient of a term is the numerical factor of that term
  • The value of an algebraic expression depends on the values of the variables involved
  • Algebraic expressions can be used to represent formulas and rules in mathematics and real-life situations
