## INTRODUCTION
Our Earth is known as blue planet. The reason is that it appears as a blue ball when seen from space. Have you ever thought what element contributes to its blue colour? It is WATER!! Now the question arises: where this water comes from and where it goes? Let’s read about the journey of water in the water cycle to get the answer.

## THE WATER CYCLE
Water continually changes its form. The heat of the sun evaporates water and changes it into water vapour. The water vapour being warm, rises high and gradually cools down and condenses to form clouds. This causes rain, snowfall and hailstorm. # DISTRIBUTION OF WATERBODIES

Our Earth is also called the Watery Planet. It is because about 71% of the Earth's surface is covered with water while only 29% is the land area. The water is found in oceans, seas, lakes, rivers, etc. The domain of water is called the hydrosphere.

Though about two-thirds of the Earth is covered with water yet the water fit for human consumption is only 3%. 97% of the water on the Earth is saline water. It is found in oceans and seas, and is not suitable for human consumption.

Out of the 3% of freshwater, 2% is in the form of ice caps and glaciers and only 1% is available in the form of surface water. This is found in rivers, lakes and as ground water or in the gaseous form as water vapour in the atmosphere.

Freshwater is the most valuable natural resource. It must be used judiciously without being wasted and polluted.

## Insight
The Dead Sea is the lowest point on Earth at 431 meters below sea level. It is one of the saltiest water body in the world. No aquatic living form is found here. Because of high salt content in water, you cannot get drowned here.

## Key Question
Why is the amount of freshwater available to us is very less?

## MOVEMENT OF OCEAN WATER

Oceans are very large waterbodies surrounding the continents. There are five main oceans on the surface of the Earth. These are the Pacific Ocean, the Atlantic Ocean, the Indian Ocean, the Arctic Ocean, and the Antarctic Ocean. These oceans have a moderating effect on the climate of coastal areas of the continents. The ocean water is never still. Its movement can be categorised as waves, tides and currents. These movements are caused due to difference in temperature, salinity and prevailing winds.

## Key Question
Why is ocean water not fit for human consumption?

## Waves

When surface water of the oceans rises and falls alternately, they are called waves. These are caused by the action of wind which pushes the water in an up and down movement. The waves travel in the direction of the wind. The shape and height of the wave depends on the speed of the wind. A light breeze produces gentle waves while a strong wind causes huge waves which cause tremendous destruction.

## Insight
The tallest wave ever measured was 524 m at Lituya Bay, Alaska.

# CASE STUDY — INDIAN TSUNAMI OF 2004

## Impact

The term 'Tsunami' comes from the Japanese words 'tsu' meaning harbour and 'nami' meaning waves. It means huge tidal waves. These waves are also known as the harbour waves. A tsunami can be caused due to underwater earthquakes, volcanic eruptions and underwater massive explosions.

On 26 December, 2004, a deadly tsunami occurred in the Indian Ocean due to an earthquake called the Sumatra-Andaman Earthquake. Its epicentre was off the west coast of Sumatra in Indonesia.

The earthquake which measured 9.0 magnitude on the Richter Scale caused a series of tsunami waves as high as 30 metres causing devastation and destruction on the coasts of countries bordering the Indian Ocean.

The tsunami affected the coastal areas across South and Southeast Asia. India, Indonesia, Sri Lanka and Thailand were the countries where destruction was maximum. Coastal areas of Malaysia, Myanmar, Bangladesh, Maldives and East African countries of Somalia, Kenya, Tanzania and Madagascar also suffered serious damage. In India, the coastal areas of Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Puducherry and Andaman and Nicobar Islands were the worst affected.

The Indira Point, which was the Southernmost point of India in Andaman and Nicobar Islands was submerged.

## Impact

The tsunami wreaked havoc on the communities living in the coastal areas. More than 1.8 lakh lives were lost, about 42,000 people were reported missing. The damage to property was unaccountable. Houses were flattened, trees and electric poles uprooted, automobiles submerged and agricultural land flooded with saline water. Thousands of people in the affected region lost their livelihood. Devastation caused due to the waves was beyond imagination.

## Relief

Relief poured in from all quarters — local, state, national and international. Providing food, water, first aid, medical aid, clothes and shelter was an uphill task. The government was able to mobilise resources and respond almost immediately. Though, the rehabilitation work is almost complete, yet healing the emotional scars and providing emotional support was not an easy task. Life for many would never be the same.

# TIDES

Periodic rise and fall of ocean water at regular intervals, twice a day, is called a tide. Tides are experienced in the oceans and seas. When the level of seawater rises, it is a high tide and when the level falls, it is a low tide. Gravitational attraction of the moon and sun causes these tides.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing the gravitational pull of the moon and sun on the Earth, resulting in high tides. Labels: Earth, Moon, Sun. Arrows show the direction of gravitational pull. [IMAGE_END]

## Spring Tide and Neap Tide

As the moon is closer to the Earth, it exerts larger gravitational pull causing high tides.

The height of tides depends upon the relative position of the moon and the Earth with respect to the Sun. During full moon and new moon days, the sun, the moon and the Earth are in the same line resulting in extra high tides. These are called spring tides.

When the moon is in the first and last quarter, the sun and moon are at right angles and the water gets drawn in diagonally opposite directions. This results in low tides which are called neap tides.

## Uses

Tides are useful for navigation, fishing and trade. High tides help the ships to enter or leave the harbours easily. Tides help in fishing. During high tides more fish come near the shore helping the fishermen to get plentiful catch. Tides also help to generate electricity. As the seawater gushes in during a high tide, its energy can be tapped and used to generate electricity. India is yet to start utilising tidal energy.

# OCEAN CURRENTS

The continuous movement of ocean water in the form of streams in a definite direction is called an ocean current. The factors which give rise to ocean currents and modify their courses are:

- Action of planetary winds
- Variation in temperature and density of ocean water
- Rotation of the Earth
- Shapes of coastlines

Ocean currents may be warm or cold. Those currents which originate near the Equator and move towards the poles are warm currents. The cold currents move from the poles to the tropics or lower latitudes. Warm currents are less dense than cold ocean currents.

The general circulation of ocean currents is clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and anti-clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere due to the rotation of the Earth. Ocean currents have great influence on the climate and economic activities of an area.

## Effects of Ocean Currents

- Warm currents raise the temperature over land surface while cold currents lower the temperature of the coastal areas.
- In places where warm and cold currents meet, dense fog is produced which reduces the visibility making it risky for the ships to sail.
- Warm currents keep the seas and the oceans free from icebergs, providing safe and easy sailing in the cold regions.
- Turbulent ocean water further poses more challenges.
- The areas where the warm and cold ocean currents meet, are also the best fishing areas.

## Revision Flow Chart
### Movement of Ocean Water
- **Waves**
  - Alternative rise and fall of the surface water in the ocean and seas due to wind action.
- **Tides**
  - Rise and fall of ocean water at regular intervals, twice daily.
- **Currents**
  - The continuous movement of ocean water in the form of streams in a definite direction.
  - **Types of Current**
    - **Warm Currents**
      - Move from the Equator to the poles.
      - Raise the temperature of the areas where they flow.
    - **Cold Currents**
      - Move from the poles to the Equator.
      - Lower the temperature of the areas where they flow.

### Types of Tide
- **Spring (High) Tide**
  - Occurs on Full Moon and New Moon days.
  - Sun, moon and the Earth fall in the same line.
- **Neap (Low) Tide**
  - Occurs in the 1st and last quarter of the Moon cycle.
  - Sun and Moon are at right angles.
