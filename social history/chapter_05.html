Chapter 5: Tribal Societies in Medieval India

Who Were the Tribals?

Since ancient times, tribes have been a distinct part of Indian society. They lived in groups called clans, led by tribal chiefs. Unlike the Hindu society, tribal communities did not have a caste system. Instead, they valued socio-economic equality.

  • Ownership: Land, cattle, and property were owned collectively by the community.
  • Lifestyle: They survived through hunting, food gathering, and shifting cultivation.
  • Values: Their societies were based on sharing and mutual dependence.

Interaction with Mainstream Society

During the medieval period, many tribal groups interacted with the expanding kingdoms of India.

Absorption into Hindu Society

As kingdoms expanded, rulers took over tribal territories.
* Status Changes: Tribal chiefs were often given the status of Kshatriyas (warriors/rulers), while ordinary tribals were given the status of Shudras.
* Integration: Over time, many tribes joined the Hindu caste-based society and became part of the Jatis (sub-castes).
* Conversion: Some tribes, especially in Punjab and Sindh, converted to Islam because it did not have a caste hierarchy.

Resistance to Change

Not all tribes agreed to become part of the Hindu society. Being independent-minded, many fought back.
* The Cheros: Forest tribes from eastern Uttar Pradesh and Bihar who built forts to resist the Mughals but were eventually defeated.
* General Trend: While some accepted new roles, others fiercely protected their independence.

Economic Life and Trade

Most tribal groups were pastoral nomads, moving from place to place with their cattle.

  • Exchange: They traded animal products (milk, ghee, wool) for grains, utensils, and textiles from settled farmers and craftsmen.
  • Shifting Cultivation: Also known as jhoom cultivation, they moved to new lands when the soil lost its fertility.
  • The Banjaras: These were important nomadic traders.
    • They formed caravans called tanda.
    • They transported food grains from villages to cities and towns.
    • They supplied food to large Mughal armies during military campaigns.
    • Sultan Ala-ud-din Khalji famously used them to move grains to Delhi markets.

Notable Tribal Kingdoms

The Gonds (Central India)

The Gonds lived in the forests of Chhattisgarh and Central India.
* Power: They became rich due to precious metals and forest products. They were skilled in training elephants.
* Kingdom: They established the Garh-Katanga kingdom in the 15th century.
* Administration: The tribe was divided into clans headed by Rajas. These clans formed larger units called garhs, chaurasi (84 villages), and barhots (12 villages).
* Rani Durgawati: A famous Gond queen who ruled after her husband died. She bravely resisted the Mughal army but was defeated in 1565 CE. She chose death over surrender.

The Ahoms (North-Eastern India)

The Ahoms migrated from Burma (Myanmar) to the Brahmaputra Valley in the 13th century.
* Rule: They established the Ahom Kingdom (1228–1826 CE) and ruled for nearly 600 years.
* Resistance: They fought the rulers of Bengal and even the Mughals.
* Society: Divided into clans called Khels, which distributed land among peasants.
* Culture: They adopted the Vaishnav faith but continued to worship their own tribal gods. They encouraged literature and wrote history books called Buranjis.

Other Important Tribal Groups

India had a wide variety of tribal populations across different regions:
* North-West: Balochis (large and powerful), Gaddis (shepherds in Western Himalayas).
* West/Central: Bhils (western and central India), Kolis (Maharashtra, Karnataka, Gujarat).
* South: Koragas, Maravars.
* East/North-East: Nagas, Santhals, Mundas (originally hunters, later farmers).
* Central: Langahs and Arghuns (Multan and Sind region).

Key Takeaways

  • Tribal societies were initially egalitarian without a caste system.
  • Interaction with mainstream society led to both absorption (into castes) and resistance (fighting for independence).
  • Economically, tribes played a vital role in agriculture, trade, and transportation (especially the Banjaras).
  • Powerful tribal kingdoms like the Gonds and Ahoms proved that tribes could establish strong states and resist empires.

Key Terms

Ahoms — A tribal group from North Eastern India who established a kingdom in the Brahmaputra Valley.
Banjaras — Nomadic traders who transported grains and vegetables between rural areas and urban markets.
Barhots — Administrative units consisting of 12 villages within Gond society.
Bhuiyans — Old political system of landlords suppressed by the Ahoms.
Buranji — Detailed historical reports written initially in the Ahom language and later in Assamese.
Chaurasi — Administrative divisions within Gond kingdoms comprising 84 villages.
Clans — Groups within tribal societies led by chiefs, forming the basis of community organization.
Distinct — Separate or different, used to describe how tribes formed a unique group in Indian society.
Garh-Katanga — A large Gond kingdom set up in the 15th century in Central India.
Garhs — Smaller kingdoms formed by clans within the Gond tribal structure.
Gonds — A major tribal group living in the forests of Central India and Chhattisgarh.
Jatis — Sub-castes into which many tribes were incorporated as Hindu society became more complex.
Khels — Clans within Ahom society that distributed land among peasants.
Kshatriyas — Status given to tribal chiefs when absorbed into the Hindu caste hierarchy.
Mansabdar — An administrative post or rank given to tribal chiefs under Mughal rule.
Mutual — Something held in common, describing the sharing basis of tribal communities.
Nomads — People who move from place to place rather than settling permanently.
Pastoral nomads — Tribal groups who moved with cattle and sold animal products for goods.
Resistance — Use of force or violence to oppose someone, often used by tribes against absorption.
Shifting cultivation — Farming practice where tribals moved to new lands when soil fertility decreased.
Shudras — Status given to ordinary tribals when absorbed into the Hindu caste hierarchy.
Tanda — Caravans along which Banjaras moved with their animals and goods.
Vaishnav faith — A religious belief adopted gradually by the Ahoms in the Brahmaputra Valley.
Varna system — The four-fold division of Hindu society into Brahmanas, Kshatriyas, Vaishyas, and Shudras.

Key Questions from the Text

Q1: Who are the tribals?

Answer:
Tribals are a distinct group in Indian society since ancient times. They lived in clans led by tribal chiefs. Key characteristics include:
* Each tribe had its own laws, rules, and traditions.
* They survived by hunting, food gathering, and shifting cultivation.
* Unlike Hindu society, they had no caste system.
* There was socio-economic equality in the community.
* Land, cattle, and properties were owned collectively.
* Their communities were based on sharing and mutual dependence.

Q2: Why did Ala-ud-din Khalji use the Banjaras to transport grains from the rural areas to Delhi markets?

Answer:
Ala-ud-din Khalji used the Banjaras because they were expert nomadic traders who could efficiently transport food supplies from villages to cities. They bought grains and vegetables from farmers and sold them in urban markets. This service was crucial for supplying food to the large Mughal armies during military campaigns and ensuring food availability in the capital city of Delhi.

5. Very Short Answer Type Questions.

(a) What were the four varnas?
Answer: The four varnas were Brahmanas, Kshatriyas, Vaishyas, and Shudras.

(b) Define Jatis.
Answer: Jatis were groups within the Hindu caste-based society that people with new skills were incorporated into as the economy and society became more complex.

(c) What was the basis of tribal communities?
Answer: Tribal communities were based on sharing and mutual dependence, with collective ownership of land, cattle, and other movable properties.

(d) Who were the Cheros?
Answer: The Cheros were forest tribes located in eastern Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand, and western Bihar who fought against the Mughals to maintain their independence.

(e) Who were the banjaras?
Answer: The Banjaras were a nomadic tribal group involved in commercial exchanges who transported grains and vegetables from farmers to urban markets and army camps.

6. Short Answer Type Questions.

(a) In the Medieval period, what was the basis of the division in Hindu society?
Answer: The division in Hindu society was based on the Varna system, which categorized people into Brahmanas, Kshatriyas, Vaishyas, and Shudras. Later, this transformed into a rigid caste system with Jatis.

(b) What led to the formation of Jatis?
Answer: As the economy and society became more complex, people with new skills were incorporated into the Hindu society. Many tribes joined the Jatis belonging to the lower caste hierarchy.

(c) Who were given the status of Kshatriyas?
Answer: Generally, tribal chiefs were given the status of Kshatriyas when their groups were absorbed into the rest of Indian society.

(d) Highlight the occupations practised by tribal societies in India.
Answer: Tribal societies practised hunting, food gathering, shifting cultivation (jhoom), pastoral nomadism (moving with cattle), and various crafts. Some became farmers, while others worked as pedlars, entertainers, or soldiers.

(e) How did the Gonds assert their independence?
Answer: The Gonds asserted their independence by setting up a large kingdom named Garh-Katanga in the 15th century. They utilized rich resources like precious metals and elephants, organized themselves into clans (garhs), and resisted invasions, notably under Queen Rani Durgawati.

7. Long Answer Type Questions.

(a) How did the tribal groups interact with the society during the medieval period?
Answer: Tribal groups interacted with Indian society in several ways:
1. Absorption: Many tribes were absorbed into Hindu society. Chiefs became Kshatriyas and ordinary members became Shudras.
2. Conversion: Some tribes, especially in Punjab and Sindh, converted to Islam as it lacked a caste system.
3. Economic Exchange: Groups like Banjaras traded animal products for grains and textiles with settled communities.
4. Resistance: Some tribes, like the Cheros and Gonds, resisted absorption and fought wars to maintain independence.
5. State Building: Tribes like the Ahoms and Gonds established powerful independent kingdoms.

(b) What were the features of pastoral nomads?
Answer:
1. They moved from one place to another with their cattle.
2. They sold animal products like milk, ghee, and wool.
3. They exchanged these products for grains, utensils, and textiles produced by settled farmers.
4. Most practised 'shifting cultivation' (jhoom), moving to new lands when soil fertility decreased.
5. They often traveled in caravans known as 'tanda'.

(c) How did Ahoms manage to resist the Mughals?
Answer:
1. Strong Administration: They established the Ahom Kingdom (1228-1826 CE) and ruled for nearly 600 years.
2. Military Strength: They fought rulers of Bengal and entered Mughal territory.
3. Social Organization: Society was divided into clans called 'Khels' which distributed land and took community decisions.
4. Population Management: Population checks allowed shifting people from crowded to less populated areas.
5. Cultural Balance: They adopted Vaishnav faith but kept worshiping their own tribal Gods, maintaining harmony.

(d) How Banjaras were important for the economy?
Answer:
1. Food Supply: They transported food grains from villages to cities and towns.
2. Army Support: They transported food supplies to large Mughal armies during military campaigns.
3. Market Linkage: They connected rural producers (farmers) with urban consumers (markets).
4. Trade Network: They facilitated trade by buying grains/vegetables and selling them in urban markets, acting as vital links in the supply chain.

(e) The Government of India has taken several welfare measures for the betterment of the tribal people. What was the need for such special attention? Should not we leave these people to live in their natural surroundings without any disturbance? (HOTS)
Answer:
* Need for Special Attention: Tribal people have historically faced exploitation, displacement due to development projects, and lack of access to education and healthcare. Special attention is needed to protect their rights, preserve their culture, and ensure their socio-economic development.
* Should we leave them undisturbed? While respecting their way of life is important, leaving them completely isolated may not be beneficial. They need access to modern healthcare, education, and legal protection to survive in the changing world. The goal should be balanced development that protects their identity while improving their quality of life, rather than total isolation which might lead to vulnerability.

8. Assertion and Reason Based Questions.

Assertion (A): Most of the tribal groups were pastoral nomads. They moved from one place to another with their cattle.
Reason (R): The tribal groups did not like to settle down.

Answer:
* Assertion is True.
* Reason is False.
* Explanation: While most tribal groups were pastoral nomads, the reason that they "did not like to settle down" is not entirely accurate. Many tribal groups eventually settled down, became farmers, or were absorbed into the caste system (like the Gonds and Ahoms). Their movement was often due to economic reasons like grazing and shifting cultivation, not necessarily a dislike for settling.
