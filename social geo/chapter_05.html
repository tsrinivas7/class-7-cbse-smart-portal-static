Chapter 5: Water

Introduction

Our Earth is known as the Blue Planet or Watery Planet. When seen from space, it appears blue because of the vast amount of water covering its surface. About 71% of the Earth's surface is covered with water, while only 29% is land.

The Water Cycle

Water is constantly moving and changing its form in a continuous process called the Hydrological Cycle or Water Cycle.
* Evaporation: Heat from the sun turns water into water vapour.
* Condensation: Water vapour rises, cools down, and forms clouds.
* Precipitation: Water falls back to Earth as rain, snow, or hail.
* Collection: Water collects in rivers, lakes, and oceans, completing the cycle.

Distribution of Waterbodies

The domain of water is called the Hydrosphere. Although most of the Earth is covered in water, very little is suitable for human use.
* Saline Water: 97% of Earth's water is salty and found in oceans and seas. It cannot be consumed by humans.
* Freshwater: Only 3% is freshwater.
* 2% is locked in ice caps and glaciers.
* Only 1% is available as surface water (rivers, lakes) or groundwater.
* Dead Sea Fact: The Dead Sea is the lowest point on Earth (431 meters below sea level). It is extremely salty, so no aquatic life exists there, and people float easily due to the high salt content.

Movement of Ocean Water

Ocean water is never still. It moves in three main ways: Waves, Tides, and Currents. These movements are caused by wind, temperature differences, salinity, and the Earth's rotation.

1. Waves

  • Waves are the alternate rise and fall of surface water.
  • They are caused by the action of wind pushing water up and down.
  • Strong winds create huge, destructive waves.
  • Fact: The tallest wave ever measured was 524 meters at Lituya Bay, Alaska.

2. Tides

  • Tides are the periodic rise and fall of ocean water twice a day.
  • Cause: Gravitational attraction of the Moon and the Sun.
  • Types of Tides:
    • Spring Tide: Occurs during Full Moon and New Moon days when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are in a straight line. These result in extra high tides.
    • Neap Tide: Occurs during the first and last quarters of the Moon when the Sun and Moon are at right angles. These result in lower tides.
  • Uses: Tides help ships enter harbours (navigation), assist fishermen, and can be used to generate electricity.

3. Ocean Currents

  • Ocean currents are continuous streams of ocean water moving in a definite direction.
  • Factors: Planetary winds, temperature/density variations, Earth's rotation, and coastline shapes.
  • Types:
    • Warm Currents: Originate near the Equator and move towards the poles. They raise temperatures.
    • Cold Currents: Move from the poles to the tropics. They lower temperatures.
  • Effects:
    • Influence local climate (warm currents warm land; cold currents cool coastal areas).
    • Dense fog forms where warm and cold currents meet, making sailing risky.
    • Areas where currents meet are excellent fishing grounds.
    • Warm currents keep seas free from icebergs in cold regions.
  • Circulation: Clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and anti-clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere.

4. Tsunamis

  • Origin: The word comes from Japanese (tsu = harbour, nami = waves).
  • Causes: Underwater earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or massive explosions.
  • Case Study: Indian Ocean Tsunami (2004):
    • Date: 26 December 2004.
    • Cause: A 9.0 magnitude earthquake off the west coast of Sumatra, Indonesia.
    • Impact: Waves up to 30 metres high devastated coastal areas across South and Southeast Asia.
    • Affected Countries: India, Indonesia, Sri Lanka, Thailand, Malaysia, Myanmar, Bangladesh, Maldives, and East African nations.
    • Damage in India: Coastal areas of Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Puducherry, and Andaman & Nicobar Islands were worst affected. Indira Point was submerged. Over 1.8 lakh lives were lost.
    • Relief: Immediate government response provided food, shelter, and medical aid, though emotional recovery took longer.

Conservation of Water

Freshwater is the most valuable natural resource. Since only 1% is readily available, we must use it judiciously. We should avoid wasting and polluting water to ensure it remains available for future generations.

EXERCISE 5: VERY SHORT ANSWER TYPE QUESTIONS

Question 5(a): What do you mean by hydrosphere?
Answer: The domain of water is called the hydrosphere. It includes oceans, seas, lakes, rivers, ice caps, glaciers, groundwater, and atmospheric water vapour.

Question 5(b): Suggest any two ways to conserve water.
Answer: 1. Use water judiciously without wasting it. 2. Prevent pollution of water sources. (Other examples: Fix leaking taps, rainwater harvesting).

Question 5(c): Name the three movements of the ocean water.
Answer: The three movements are Waves, Tides, and Currents.

Question 5(d): On what factors does the shape and height of a wave depend?
Answer: The shape and height of a wave depend on the speed of the wind.

Question 5(e): What is tide?
Answer: A tide is the periodic rise and fall of ocean water at regular intervals, twice a day.

EXERCISE 6: SHORT ANSWER TYPE QUESTIONS

Question 6(a): Is water a renewable resource? How?
Answer: Yes, water is a renewable resource. It undergoes the hydrological cycle where it evaporates, forms clouds, precipitates as rain/snow, and returns to the earth, continuously recycling itself.

Question 6(b): Mention the factors that give rise to ocean currents.
Answer: The factors are: Action of planetary winds, Variation in temperature and density of ocean water, Rotation of the Earth, and Shapes of coastlines.

Question 6(c): Why is it important to use water judiciously?
Answer: It is important because although 71% of Earth is covered with water, only 3% is freshwater, and only 1% is easily available for human consumption. Freshwater is a valuable natural resource that must not be wasted or polluted.

Question 6(d): Distinguish between:
(i) Waves and Tides
Waves are caused by wind pushing water up and down. Tides are caused by gravitational pull of the moon and sun causing rise and fall at regular intervals.
(ii) Warm and Cold Ocean Currents
Warm currents move from the Equator to the poles and raise temperatures. Cold currents move from the poles to the Equator and lower temperatures.

Question 6(e): According to Hindu mythology the Moon is responsible for water-related phenomena. Tides are also caused due to it. Does the sun also play any role in the occurrence of tides?
Answer: Yes, the sun also plays a role. The height of tides depends upon the relative position of the moon and the Earth with respect to the Sun. During full moon and new moon days, when the sun, moon, and Earth are in a line, extra high tides (spring tides) occur.

EXERCISE 7: LONG ANSWER TYPE QUESTIONS

Question 7(a): What are the different types of tides? Explain them in detail with their diagrams.
Answer: There are two main types:
1. Spring Tide: Occurs on Full Moon and New Moon days. The sun, moon, and Earth fall in the same line, resulting in extra high tides.
2. Neap Tide: Occurs in the 1st and last quarter of the Moon cycle. The sun and moon are at right angles, resulting in low tides.
(Diagrams would show the alignment of Earth, Moon, and Sun for both cases).

Question 7(b): How are ocean currents different from waves?
Answer: Waves are the alternative rise and fall of surface water due to wind action. Ocean currents are the continuous movement of ocean water in the form of streams in a definite direction.

Question 7(c): Mention the important uses of tides.
Answer: 1. Navigation: High tides help ships enter or leave harbours easily. 2. Fishing: More fish come near the shore during high tides. 3. Electricity Generation: Energy from gushing seawater can be tapped to generate electricity.

Question 7(d): Warm and cold ocean currents shape the climate of the nearby areas. Do they also affect navigation? Find out the effects of these ocean currents on the movement of ships.
Answer: Yes, they affect navigation. Where warm and cold currents meet, dense fog is produced, reducing visibility and making sailing risky. Warm currents keep seas free from icebergs, providing safe sailing in cold regions. Turbulent ocean water poses challenges.

Question 7(e): Highlight the impacts of Tsunami.
Answer: Impacts include loss of life (over 1.8 lakh lives lost in 2004), people reported missing, destruction of property (houses flattened, trees uprooted), flooding of agricultural land with saline water, loss of livelihood, and long-term emotional scars for survivors.

EXERCISE 8: ASSERTION AND REASON BASED QUESTIONS

Question 8:
Assertion (A): Giant waves travel at a great speed and cause widespread damage.
Reason (R): Giant waves like the Tsunami are caused by earthquakes or volcanic eruptions.
Answer: (a) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A.
Explanation: Tsunamis are indeed caused by seismic events like earthquakes, which generate the giant waves that travel fast and cause damage.
