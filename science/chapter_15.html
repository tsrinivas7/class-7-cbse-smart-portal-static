Chapter Summary

15 Light

One of the most important senses that we human beings have is to 'see' the world around us. Light enables us to 'see'. For thousands of years, people have been curious about light, its properties and behavior. The earliest experiments on light involved the study of light using mirrors and lenses.

  • Light allows us to see objects around us.
  • Study of light involves mirrors, lenses, and prisms.
  • We will learn about rectilinear propagation, reflection, mirrors, lenses, and colours.

RECTILINEAR PROPAGATION OF LIGHT

Generally, light travels in a straight line in a uniform transparent medium or in vacuum. This mode of propagation of light is called rectilinear propagation.

Rectilinear — Consisting or bound by straight lines.

  • Light travels in a straight line in a uniform transparent medium or vacuum.
  • Shadows are formed when an object blocks light travelling in a straight line.
  • Activity C-6.2 verifies this by showing light is seen through a straight tube but not a bent one.
  • You cannot see a friend hiding behind a chair because light travels in straight lines and is blocked.

REFLECTION OF LIGHT

You can see an object when light falls on it. When light falls on a surface, it bounces off the surface. This is called reflection.

reflection — When light falls on a surface, it bounces off the surface.
regular surface — A smooth surface.
irregular surface — A rough and wavy surface.
regular reflection — Reflection by a regular surface where a parallel beam of light reflects in only one direction.
irregular or diffused reflection — Reflection by a rough surface where a parallel beam of light reflects in all directions.

  • We see objects because light reflected by them reaches our eyes.
  • Regular surfaces reflect parallel beams in one direction (smooth surfaces).
  • Irregular surfaces reflect parallel beams in all directions (rough surfaces).
  • Still water and polished metal act as regular reflecting surfaces.

PLANE MIRRORS

Any smooth polished surface that can reflect the rays of light and form a clear image is called a mirror.

mirror — A highly polished reflecting surface that can reflect the rays of light into the same medium.
lateral inversion — The property where the left side of the object appears as the right side in the image.
virtual image — An image that cannot be touched or formed on a screen.
upright image — An image that is not upside down compared to the object.
image characteristics — Properties of an image produced by a reflecting surface.

  • A plane mirror produces an upright, virtual image of the same size as the object.
  • The distance of the image behind the mirror equals the distance of the object in front.
  • The image is laterally inverted (left becomes right).
  • The image moves away as the object moves away from the mirror.

SPHERICAL MIRRORS

As the name suggests, a spherical mirror is a part of a sphere. The polished curved surface of spherical mirrors also reflects light, but the images formed by them are different from those formed by a plane mirror.

spherical mirror — A part of a sphere.
Concave mirrors — A spherical mirror in which the reflecting surface is the inner surface of the sphere.
convex mirrors — A spherical mirror in which the reflecting surface is the outer surface of the sphere.

  • Spherical mirrors are parts of a hollow sphere cut through.
  • There are two types: Concave mirrors (inner surface) and Convex mirrors (outer surface).
  • Images formed by spherical mirrors differ from plane mirrors.

Concave Mirror (or Converging Mirror)

A concave mirror is a spherical mirror in which the reflecting surface is the inner surface of the sphere.

Concave mirror — A spherical mirror in which the reflecting surface is the inner surface of the sphere.
enlarged Image — An image that is bigger than the object.

  • Can form real, inverted images on a screen when the object is far.
  • Can form virtual, upright, enlarged images when the object is very close.
  • The point where sunlight focuses is called the focus or focal point.
  • Used in dentist's mirrors, shaving mirrors, and solar power projects.

Convex Mirror (or Diverging Mirror)

A convex mirror is a spherical mirror in which the reflecting surface is the outer surface of the sphere.

Convex mirror — A spherical mirror in which the reflecting surface is the outer surface of the sphere.
diminished Image — An image that is smaller than the object.

  • Always forms virtual, upright, and diminished images regardless of object position.
  • Provides a wide field of view.
  • Used as rear view mirrors and wing mirrors in vehicles.
  • Used in supermarkets and parking lots for security.

Uses of Spherical Mirrors

Unlike plane mirrors, curved mirrors form all sorts of images. These can be put to use in a variety of applications.

  • Concave mirrors are used by dentists and for shaving to get enlarged images.
  • Concave mirrors focus sunlight in solar power projects.
  • Convex mirrors are used as rear view mirrors in buses and cars.
  • Convex mirrors help view surroundings in supermarkets and parking lots.

SPHERICAL LENSES

A lens is a curved and polished piece of glass or any other transparent material. Just like mirrors, lenses can also change the direction of propagation of light. Convex and concave lenses are the two common types of lenses.

lens — A curved and polished piece of glass or any other transparent material.
Convex Lens (or Converging Lens) — A lens that bulges in the middle and is thinner at the edges.
Concave Lens (or Diverging Lens) — A lens that is thinner in the middle and smoothly increases in thickness at the edges.

  • Lenses change the direction of light propagation.
  • Convex lenses converge light; Concave lenses diverge light.
  • Common examples include magnifying glasses and spectacles.

Convex Lens (or Converging Lens)

A convex lens is a lens that bulges in the middle and is thinner at the edges. A common example is a magnifying glass.

focus or focal point — The point where a sharp image of the sun is formed on a screen.
Real image — An image that can be formed on a screen.
Virtual image — An image that cannot be formed on a screen (formed when object is very close).

  • Forms real, inverted images on a screen when the object is at a distance.
  • Forms virtual, upright, enlarged images when the object is very close.
  • Used in reading glasses (long sight), cameras, microscopes, and telescopes.
  • Our eyes also contain lenses.

Concave Lens (or Diverging Lens)

A concave lens is a lens that is thinner in the middle and smoothly increases in thickness at the edges. Concave lenses are present in spectacles of negative power.

  • Always forms a virtual, diminished, upright image regardless of object position.
  • Cannot form an image on a screen.
  • Used to correct short-sightedness in spectacles.

Uses of Lenses

Both convex and concave lenses are used in spectacles to correct eyesight.

  • Convex lenses are used as reading glasses for long-sightedness.
  • Concave lenses are used to correct short-sightedness.
  • Lenses are used in cameras, magnifying glasses, binoculars, microscopes, and telescopes.
  • Human eyes utilize lenses to focus light.

COLOURS

White light is composed of many colours. When these colours combine, they look white. One way of showing that white light is composed of different colours is to make white light pass through a glass prism. This splits up the white light into its constituent colours. This is called dispersion of light.

dispersion of light — Splitting up white light into its constituent colours.
spectrum — A band of colours seen after white light passes through a prism.
visible spectrum — The band of colours visible to the human eye.

  • White light is composed of many colours (VIBGYOR).
  • Prisms split white light into a spectrum (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red).
  • Objects absorb some colours and reflect others (e.g., green leaves reflect green).
  • The visible spectrum consists of seven colours.

Newton's Disc

Another method which can be used to show that white light is composed of different colours is to show that different colours, when combined, give us white light. This is what happens in a Newton's disc.

persistence of vision — Property of our eyes where an image remains for a fraction of a second.

  • Newton's disc is painted with constituent colours of white light (VIBGYOR) in equal proportions.
  • When rotated very fast, the disc appears white due to persistence of vision.

  • Isaac Newton proposed that white light is a mixture of several colours.

  • Light travels in a straight line (rectilinear propagation).

  • Shadows are formed due to rectilinear propagation of light.
  • Plane mirrors form virtual, upright images of the same size.
  • Concave mirrors can form both real and virtual images.
  • Convex mirrors always form virtual, diminished images.
  • Lenses are curved polished pieces of glass or transparent material.
  • Convex lenses can form real and virtual images; Concave lenses form only virtual images.
  • White light is composed of many colours (VIBGYOR).
  • Newton's disc demonstrates that combining colours gives white light.

Section 1: Introduction Questions (Page 224)

Question 1. Aman is hiding behind a door and trying to look around the open door with the help of a bent hollow tube. Do you think he will be successful?
Answer: No, Aman will not be successful. Light travels in a straight line (rectilinear propagation). It cannot bend around corners to pass through a bent tube to reach his eye.

Question 2. Where do you think the colours in the CD are coming from—is the CD itself coloured?
Answer: No, the CD itself is not coloured. The colours come from white sunlight reflecting off the CD's surface. The CD splits the white light into its constituent colours (dispersion).

Question 3. Nancy is trying to get the sun-light passing through the window to fall on a wall by the side of the window. What do you think she is using to do this?
Answer: She is using a mirror. A mirror reflects light, allowing her to change the direction of the sunlight falling through the window onto the wall.

Question 4. Why do the images in mirrors in amusement parks appear distorted?
Answer: The images appear distorted because the mirrors have curved surfaces (spherical mirrors), unlike plane mirrors. These curved surfaces alter the path of light rays differently than flat surfaces.

IV. Long answer type questions

1. What is the difference between the reflection from a regular surface and that from an irregular surface?
Answer:
* Regular Surface: Smooth and polished. Parallel incident rays reflect in only one direction (parallel reflected rays). Forms clear images (e.g., plane mirror).
* Irregular Surface: Rough and wavy. Parallel incident rays reflect in all directions (scattered rays). Does not form clear images (e.g., wall, paper).

2. You are given a plane mirror, convex mirror, concave mirror, convex lens and a concave lens. Give two methods by which you can form diminished images of an object and two methods by which you can form enlarged images.
Answer:
* Diminished Images:
1. Using a Convex Mirror (always forms diminished virtual images).
2. Using a Concave Lens (always forms diminished virtual images).
(Also, Concave mirror forms diminished images when object is far).
* Enlarged Images:
1. Using a Concave Mirror (when object is close to the mirror).
2. Using a Convex Lens (when object is close to the lens, acts as magnifying glass).

3. What is a Newton's disc? What is it used to demonstrate? Explain how.
Answer:
* Definition: A Newton's disc is a circular disc painted with the seven constituent colours of white light (VIBGYOR) in equal proportions.
* Demonstration: It demonstrates that white light is composed of seven colours.
* Explanation: When the disc is rotated very fast, the persistence of vision causes the images of the different colours to overlap in our eyes. The brain perceives this mixture as white light.

Rectilinear propagation of light explains a lot of phenomena associated with light. One such phenomenon is the formation of shadows.

This shadow is formed when the pencil blocks the light travelling in a straight line. This is possible because of rectilinear propagation of light. See Figure 15.1.

There are many instances in our daily lives where we come across rectilinear propagation of light. Can you see your friend if he/she is hiding behind a chair? REFLECTION OF LIGHT

You can see an object when light falls on it. When light falls on a surface, it bounces off the surface. This is called reflection.

Reflecting Surfaces

All types of surfaces reflect light. That is why we can see them. When light from the sun or any source falls on an object, we are able to see the object because the light reflected by the object reaches our eyes.

While talking of reflection, we refer to a smooth surface as a regular surface, and a rough and wavy surface as an irregular surface. A regular surface reflects a parallel beam of light incident upon it in only one direction. Reflection by a regular surface is referred to as regular reflection [Fig. 15.2(a)]. A rough surface reflects a parallel beam of light incident upon it in all directions. The small bumps and irregularities on a rough surface cause each of the light rays to reflect in different directions. This kind of reflection is called irregular or diffused reflection [Fig. 15.2(b)].

PLANE MIRRORS

Can you think of a plane reflecting surface? The mirror on the dressing table is a good example of a plane reflecting surface. Any smooth polished surface that can reflect the rays of light and form a clear image is called a mirror. Any highly polished metal surface, e.g., a stainless steel plate, or still water (Fig. 15.3), acts like a mirror.

If you look at yourself closely in a mirror, what do you observe about your image? Is it bigger or smaller than you? Place the palm of your hand on the flat surface of the mirror and study its reflected image. Is the image bigger, smaller, or of the same size? Now, move your hand slowly away from the mirror and observe the size of the image all the time. You will find that the size of the image neither increases nor decreases in size (Fig. 15.4). However, as you move your hand away from the mirror, the image also moves away. The image is at the same distance behind the mirror as the object (here your hand is the object) in front of it.

[IMAGE_START] Illustration of a boy standing in front of a vertical mirror. The boy is facing the mirror. Inside the mirror frame, there is a reflection of the boy facing the opposite direction (laterally inverted).
Caption below: "Fig 15.4 The image formed in a plane mirror is laterally inverted" [IMAGE_END]

Lift your right hand. The image in the mirror appears to lift its left hand. This is called lateral inversion.

Now, stand in front of the mirror and see if you can touch your image formed in the mirror. You cannot because the image seems to be behind the mirror. Such an image is called a virtual image. A virtual image cannot be touched or formed on a screen. Do you notice anything else about your image? Is the image upside down as compared to you? It is not. Such an image is called an upright image. Thus, a reflecting surface produces an image of certain properties. These are called image characteristics. To summarize, the image characteristics of a plane mirror are the following.

A plane mirror produces an upright, virtual image.
The image is of the same size as that of the object.
The distance of the image formed behind the mirror equals the distance of the object in front of the mirror.
The image in a plane mirror is laterally inverted.

One way of showing that white light is composed of different colours is to make white light pass through a glass prism. This splits up the white light into its constituent colours. See Fig (Fig 15.9). This is called dispersion of light.

[IMAGE_START] Figure 15.9 shows a diagram of light dispersion. A beam of white light enters a triangular glass prism from the left. Inside the prism, the light bends. Upon exiting the right side of the prism, the light spreads out into a fan-shaped spectrum of colors (rainbow), projecting onto a dark background. The caption reads: "Fig. 15.9 Dispersion of light through a prism". [IMAGE_END]

If you hold a screen in its path, after the white light passes through a prism, you will see a band of colours. This band of colours is called the spectrum. As it is visible to the human eye, it is called the visible spectrum (Fig. 15.10). When light falls on an object, it absorbs a few of these colours and reflects the rest. In case of a green leaf, it absorbs all the colours except green (which it reflects). That is why we see it as green.

Another method which can be used to show that white light is composed of different colours is to show that different colours, when combined, give us white light. This is what happens in a Newton's disc. Let us learn about it.

234 Light

Newton's Disc

Newton's disc is a coloured disc which is painted with the constituent colours of white light [i.e., violet, indigo, blue, green, yellow, orange, and red (VIBGYOR)] in equal proportions so that when it is rotated very fast, the disc appears white (Fig. 15.11). This is because of a property of our eyes called persistence of vision. This means that an image formed by the eye remains for a fraction of a second. When the disc is rotated at a high speed, the images of the different colours overlap in our eyes and the brain perceives it as white.

Key Words

Term Definition
Mirror A highly polished reflecting surface that can reflect the rays of light into the same medium is called a mirror.
Virtual image An image that cannot be formed on a screen is called a virtual image.
Real image An image that can be formed on a screen is called a real image.
Inverted image An image that is upside down as compared to the object is called an inverted image.
Erect image An image that is upright (the right way up) as compared to the object is called an erect image.
Convex mirror A curved mirror with a reflecting surface bulging outward at the middle is called a convex mirror.
Concave mirror A curved mirror with a reflecting surface curving inward at the middle is called a concave mirror.

Light | 235

Lens A curved and polished piece of glass or any other transparent material is called a lens.
Convex lens The type of lens that bulges out in the middle and is thinner around the edges is called a convex lens.
Concave lens The lens that is thinner in the middle (as compared to the edges) is called a concave lens.

Summary

  • Light travels in a straight line. This is called rectilinear propagation of light.
  • Shadows are formed as a result of rectilinear propagation of light.
  • We can see the reflected image of an object only if it is reflected by a highly polished surface.
  • The image formed by a plane mirror cannot be made to form on a screen.
  • A plane mirror forms a virtual, upright image. The image size remains the same as that of the object.
  • A convex mirror can only form virtual images, whereas a concave mirror can form both virtual and real images depending on the distance of the object from the mirror.
  • A lens is a curved and polished piece of glass or any other transparent material.
  • Common lenses are of two types: convex and concave.
  • A concave lens always forms a virtual image. A convex lens can form both real and virtual images depending on the distance of the object from the lens.
  • Light is composed of many colours. When these colours combine, they look white.
  • Newton's disc is a coloured disc painted with constituent colours of white light (VIBGYOR) in equal proportions so that when it is rotated very fast, the disc appears white.
