Respiration in Organisms

Respiratory System

oxygen — A gas required by cells to break down food and obtain energy.
inhalation or breathing in — The intake of air rich in oxygen.
enzymes — Substances involved in chemical reactions during respiration.
catalysts — Substances that speed up reactions.
exhalation or breathing out — The removal of air rich in carbon dioxide.
Breathing — The physical process of inhaling oxygen-rich air and exhaling carbon dioxide-rich air.
Respiration — The chemical process of releasing energy from the breakdown of food.

  • All living organisms require oxygen to break down food and obtain energy.
  • Breathing involves the physical exchange of gases (inhalation and exhalation).
  • Respiration involves chemical reactions inside cells to release energy.
  • Carbon dioxide produced during respiration is toxic and must be removed from the body.
  • Breathing is a physical process, whereas respiration is a chemical process involving enzymes.

Respiratory System in Animals

spiracles — Small holes in the body of insects through which air enters.
tracheal tubes — Tubes in insects that carry air from spiracles inside the body.
gills — Special organs used by aquatic animals like fish for respiration.
filaments — Thread-like structures making up gills, rich in blood vessels.
capillaries — Tiny blood vessels in gills that absorb oxygen from water.
lungs — Sac-like, spongy organs used by mammals and humans for respiration.

  • Unicellular animals absorb oxygen directly from water or air by diffusion.
  • Earthworms and leeches exchange gases through their moist and slimy skin.
  • Amphibians like frogs breathe through skin in water and lungs on land.
  • Insects breathe through spiracles connected to tracheal tubes.
  • Fish use gills containing filaments and capillaries to absorb dissolved oxygen from water.

Respiratory System in Humans

thorax — The chest area where lungs are present.
diaphragm — A dome-shaped muscle beneath the lungs that helps pump air.
nostrils — The pair of openings through which oxygen-rich air enters the nose.
nasal cavity — The space inside the nose where air passes before entering the larynx.
larynx — The voice box located after the nasal cavity.
trachea — The windpipe made of ring-like cartilage structures.
bronchi — Two tubes that the trachea divides into, leading to the lungs.
bronchioles — Thinner tubes branching from bronchi carrying air to alveoli.
alveoli — Tiny air bags in lungs where the actual exchange of gases takes place.

  • Lungs are protected by the ribcage and supported by ribs.
  • Tiny hairs and mucus in the nose prevent dust and foreign particles from entering.
  • The trachea is held together by cartilage rings to keep it open.
  • Air travels from the trachea to bronchi, then bronchioles, and finally reaches alveoli.
  • The diaphragm contracts to pull air into the lungs (inhalation) and relaxes to push air out (exhalation).

Exchange of Gases

haemoglobin — A compound in blood that combines with absorbed oxygen.
oxyhaemoglobin — The compound formed when oxygen combines with haemoglobin in the blood.

  • Absorbed oxygen in the alveoli combines with haemoglobin to form oxyhaemoglobin.
  • Blood carries oxyhaemoglobin to all cells of the body.
  • In cells, oxygen helps break down food (glucose) to generate energy.
  • Carbon dioxide, water, and heat are generated along with energy during food breakdown.
  • Only a very thin membrane separates the air in alveoli from the blood in capillaries.

Respiratory System in Plants

stomata — Tiny holes or openings on the underside of leaves for gas exchange.

  • Plants respire to break down manufactured food (starch/sugar) to release energy.
  • Gas exchange occurs through stomata present on leaves.
  • Roots buried in soil also require oxygen to survive and take up minerals.
  • Farmers plough soil to create tiny air spaces around soil particles for root respiration.
  • Respiration occurs throughout day and night, unlike photosynthesis which happens only during the day.

Aerobic and Anaerobic Respiration

aerobic — Respiration that takes place in the presence of oxygen.
anaerobic — Respiration that takes place in the absence of oxygen.
ethyl alcohol — An organic compound formed during anaerobic respiration.
combustion — Burning process similar to aerobic respiration but differs in energy release.

  • Aerobic respiration is the most efficient form of respiration.
  • In aerobic respiration, sugar reacts with oxygen to produce energy, carbon dioxide, and water.
  • In anaerobic respiration, sugar breaks down without oxygen to produce energy, carbon dioxide, and ethyl alcohol.
  • Humans perform anaerobic respiration in muscle cells during heavy exercise when oxygen supply is low.
  • Microorganisms like yeast, bacteria, and fungi can respire anaerobically.

2. Let's Discuss (Page 132)

Question 1: Why do fish die when taken out of water?

Answer:
According to the chapter, fish use special organs called gills for respiration. Gills are made up of thread-like structures called filaments which are richly supplied with tiny blood vessels called capillaries. When water enters the fish's mouth, it flows over the gills, allowing the blood to absorb oxygen from the water. When fish are taken out of water, the gills cannot function properly to extract oxygen from the air, causing the fish to die due to lack of oxygen.

Question 2: We should try and breathe through the nose and not through the mouth. Why?

Answer:
As stated in the text and handwritten notes, the nose contains tiny hairs and mucus that prevent dust and other foreign particles from entering the body. Breathing through the nose filters the air before it reaches the lungs, whereas breathing through the mouth does not offer this protection.

III. Short Answer Type Questions

1. Define respiration. Why is it called a chemical process?
Answer: Respiration is the process of releasing energy from the breakdown of food. It is called a chemical process because it involves a series of chemical reactions where enzymes and catalysts break down food molecules (oxidation) to release energy, producing carbon dioxide and water as byproducts.

2. How does the diaphragm help in pumping air?
Answer: The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle just beneath the lungs. When the diaphragm contracts, the chest cavity expands, and oxygen-rich air is taken into the lungs (inhalation). When the diaphragm relaxes, the chest cavity shrinks, and carbon dioxide-rich air is pumped out of the lungs (exhalation).

3. What is oxyhaemoglobin?
Answer: Oxyhaemoglobin is a compound formed when the oxygen absorbed in the alveoli combines with haemoglobin in the blood. Blood carries oxyhaemoglobin to all cells of the body.

4. Why do roots of plants need to respire?
Answer: Roots need to respire to produce the energy necessary to actively take up minerals from the soil. If roots cannot respire, they cannot survive.

IV. Long Answer Type Questions

1. Explain how a fish respires in water, with the help of gills.
Answer: Most aquatic animals like fish use special organs called gills for respiration. Gills are like slits made up of a large number of thread-like structures called filaments. These filaments are richly supplied with tiny blood vessels called capillaries. When water enters the fish's body through the mouth, it flows over the gills. The blood in the capillaries absorbs oxygen from the water and gives out carbon dioxide. This oxygen is then transported by the blood to the rest of the body.

2. Describe how exchange of gases takes place inside the human respiratory system.
Answer:
1. Oxygen-rich air enters through the nostrils into the nasal cavity, where dust is filtered by hairs and mucus.
2. Air passes through the larynx and enters the trachea (windpipe), supported by rings of cartilage.
3. The trachea divides into two bronchi, which break down into thinner tubes called bronchioles.
4. Bronchioles carry air into tiny air bags called alveoli.
5. Gas exchange actually takes place inside the alveoli. Oxygen is absorbed into the blood, and carbon dioxide is given out.
6. Carbon dioxide-rich air retraces its path back to the nose and is exhaled.

3. What is the difference between aerobic and anaerobic respiration? Write the equations to show both the processes.
Answer:
* Aerobic Respiration: Takes place in the presence of oxygen. It releases energy and forms carbon dioxide and water. It is the most efficient form.
* Equation: Sugar + Oxygen → Energy + Carbon dioxide + Water
* Anaerobic Respiration: Takes place in the absence of oxygen. It releases energy and forms carbon dioxide and ethyl alcohol.
* Equation: Sugar → Energy + Carbon dioxide + Ethyl alcohol

RESPIRATORY SYSTEM

All living organisms are made up of a number of cells. Each of these cells requires oxygen to break down food and obtain energy. The intake of air rich in oxygen is the first step. This is called inhalation or breathing in. The next step involves a series of chemical reactions, which help in the release of energy. Several enzymes and catalysts (substances that speed up reactions) are also involved in this process. When food is broken down, the reaction produces water and carbon dioxide. Carbon dioxide is toxic to the body, so it has to be moved out. The removal of air rich in carbon dioxide is called exhalation or breathing out. This step completes the cycle. The fundamental difference between breathing and respiration is the following.

Breathing is the process of intake of air rich in oxygen (inhalation or breathing in) and giving out air rich in carbon dioxide (exhalation or breathing out).

(Respiration is the process of releasing energy from the breakdown of food.)

Table 9.1 lists some more differences between breathing and respiration.

Table 9.1 Differences between breathing and respiration

Breathing Respiration
1. It is the process of inhalation and exhalation. It is a physical process. No chemical reaction takes place; only an exchange of gases is involved. 1. It is a series of chemical reactions that result in the release of energy by the oxidation of food molecules.
2. No enzymes or other catalysts are involved. 2. Enzymes and other catalysts are involved.
3. It takes place outside the cells. 3. It takes place inside the cells.
4. No energy is released during this process. 4. Energy is released during this process.

RESPIRATORY SYSTEM IN ANIMALS

The basic process of respiration is similar in all plants and animals, and results in the release of energy. In unicellular animals, oxygen is directly absorbed from the water or air and carbon dioxide is released by diffusion. About 21% of air is oxygen, but in water there is only about 0.5% of oxygen at normal temperatures. In higher animals, there are special organs that help in respiration:

Through skin In animals such as earthworms and leeches, exchange of gases takes place through the moist and slimy surface of the skin. In amphibians such as frogs, newts, and salamanders, the exchange of gases takes place through the skin (when in water), and through the lungs (when on land).

RESPIRATORY SYSTEM IN HUMANS

Humans, like many other land animals with backbones, breathe in oxygen-rich air and breathe out carbon dioxide-rich air with the help of lungs (Fig. 9.3). Lungs are present inside our chest (also called the thorax). They are protected by the ribcage. The ribs also support the lungs and help to pump air in and out when we breathe. Just beneath the lungs is a dome-shaped muscle called the diaphragm. When the diaphragm contracts, oxygen-rich air is taken into the lungs. When the diaphragm relaxes, carbon dioxide-rich air is pumped out of the lungs.

Passage of Air

As we inhale, the oxygen-rich air enters the nose through a pair of nostrils. Tiny hair and mucus present in the nose prevent dust and other foreign particles from entering our body. Air passes through the nasal cavity and enters the larynx. The wind pipe, called the trachea, is made up of several ring-like structures, held together by cartilage. Air enters the trachea, which then divides into two bronchi (singular: bronchus). The bronchi break down into several thinner tubes called bronchioles. These bronchioles carry the inhaled air into tiny air bags called alveoli.

The exchange of gases actually takes place inside these alveoli. Once the inhaled, oxygen-rich air reaches the alveoli, the oxygen is absorbed into the blood, and carbon dioxide is given out. The carbon dioxide-rich air now needs to be removed from the lungs. The air then retraces its journey back from the alveoli to the nose—through bronchioles, bronchi, trachea, larynx, and the nose. The carbon dioxide-rich air is then exhaled out of the nose.

RESPIRATORY SYSTEM IN PLANTS

In plants, food that is manufactured in the form of starch needs to be broken down to release energy. So, respiration is a vital function in plants. It is represented as:

$$ \text{sugar} + \text{oxygen} \longrightarrow \text{energy} + \text{carbon dioxide} + \text{water} $$

Plants respire through tiny holes or openings called stomata (Fig. 9.5) present on the underside of leaves. Stomata trap air (containing gases like oxygen and carbon dioxide) and the exchange of gases takes place inside plant cells.

Respiration occurs throughout day and night, whereas photosynthesis occurs only during the day when light is present.

Even roots buried in the soil require oxygen for the plant to survive. Plant roots typically take in oxygen that is available in the small spaces between soil particles, and give off carbon dioxide. If roots cannot respire, they cannot produce the energy necessary to actively take up minerals from the soil. For proper root respiration, farmers plough or till the soil so that tiny air spaces are created around the soil particles.

How do plants/animals live in water?

Aquatic plants and animals survive in water by utilizing dissolved oxygen. AEROBIC AND ANAEROBIC RESPIRATION

Respiration is primarily of two types: aerobic and anaerobic.

Aerobic Respiration

The process of respiration that takes place in the presence of oxygen is called aerobic respiration. This results in the release of energy, and in the formation of carbon dioxide and water.

Aerobic respiration is represented by the equation:
sugar + oxygen → energy + carbon dioxide + water

Aerobic respiration is the most efficient form of respiration. The reaction involved in this process is similar to that of combustion or burning. However, there are some differences between the two (Table 9.2).

Table 9.2 Differences between aerobic respiration and combustion

Aerobic respiration Combustion
1. Energy is given out gradually. 1. Energy is given out suddenly.
2. Energy is given out in the form that can be used by the organism. 2. Energy is given out in the form of heat or light.
3. Energy is stored inside the body in the form of ATP (adenosine triphosphate, energy-rich molecules), which can be used whenever needed. 3. Energy is not stored as ATP.

Anaerobic Respiration

The process of respiration that takes place in the absence of oxygen is called anaerobic ("an" means "without") respiration. This results in the release of energy, and in the formation of carbon dioxide and ethyl alcohol (an organic compound). It is represented by:
sugar → energy + carbon dioxide + ethyl alcohol

In humans, anaerobic respiration can be carried out only for a short period of time. It occurs in our muscle cells when there is not enough oxygen supply. This happens when we are doing heavy work or exercise, running fast, etc. However, many microorganisms, such as yeast, bacteria, and fungi, can only respire anaerobically.
