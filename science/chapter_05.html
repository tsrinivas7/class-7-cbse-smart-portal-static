🌍 Social Sciences & Geography

Water (Social Geography)

  • The Water Cycle: Water constantly moves between land, sea, and air through evaporation (sun heating water), condensation (forming clouds), and precipitation (rain/snow).
  • Distribution: Earth is called the "Blue Planet" because 71% is covered in water. However, only 3% is freshwater suitable for humans; most is saline ocean water.
  • Ocean Movements: Ocean water isn't still. It moves via waves (wind action), tides (moon/sun gravity), and currents (temperature, wind, earth rotation).
  • Tsunami: Huge waves caused by underwater earthquakes, like the 2004 Indian Ocean event.

Human Environment Interactions (Social Geography)

  • Amazon Basin: Located near the equator. Hot and wet climate supports thick rainforests ("Selvas") and diverse wildlife. People live in stilt houses to avoid floods.
  • Ganga-Brahmaputra Basin: Subtropical region with monsoon climate. Fertile plains support dense populations and agriculture (rice, wheat, jute). Home to the Sundarbans delta.

Tribal Societies (Social History)

  • Medieval Tribes: Groups like Gonds, Ahoms, and Banjaras lived independently or interacted with mainstream society.
  • Interaction: Some tribes were absorbed into the caste system, while others resisted (like the Cheros).
  • Notable Groups:
    • Banjaras: Nomadic traders who transported food grains.
    • Ahoms: Ruled Assam for 600 years and built strong kingdoms.
    • Gonds: Powerful in Central India, known for elephant training.

Gender Discrimination (Social Civics)

  • Gender vs. Sex: Sex is biological; gender is socially constructed roles and expectations.
  • Patriarchy: A system where men dominate, leading to discrimination against women (e.g., preference for sons, unequal pay).
  • Glass Ceiling: Invisible barriers preventing women from reaching top leadership positions.
  • Rights: The Constitution guarantees equality, and laws like the Pre-Natal Diagnostic Test Act protect women's rights.

The State Government (Social Civics)

  • Governor: The constitutional head of the state, appointed by the President.
  • Legislature: Makes laws.
    • Vidhan Sabha (Assembly): Lower house, members elected by people (MLAs).
    • Vidhan Parishad (Council): Upper house, partially nominated, permanent body.
  • Chief Minister: Leader of the majority party in the Assembly, heads the government.
  • Law Making: Bills go through readings in both houses before becoming Acts.

Understanding Media (Social Civics)

  • Role: Media informs the public and shapes opinions. In a democracy, free media is essential.
  • Types: Print (newspapers), Electronic (TV, radio), and Digital (Internet).
  • Advertising: Promotes products (commercial) or social causes (social).
  • RTI Act: Gives citizens the right to access government information.

🔬 Science

Wastewater Management (Science)

  • Sources: Households, industries, agriculture, and hospitals generate dirty water (wastewater/sewage).
  • Treatment Plant:
    1. Primary: Mechanical removal of solids (screens, grit chambers).
    2. Secondary: Biological breakdown using bacteria (aeration tanks).
    3. Tertiary: Chemical treatment (chlorine/UV light) to kill germs.
  • Sludge: Solid waste settled during treatment. Can be composted or incinerated.
  • Alternatives: Septic tanks, biogas plants, and vermi-composting toilets help manage waste locally.

Reproduction in Plants (Science)

  • Asexual: One parent involved. Methods include budding (yeast), fragmentation (algae), and spore formation (mosses).
  • Vegetative: New plants grow from roots, stems, or leaves (e.g., potato tubers, onion bulbs). Artificial methods include cutting, grafting, and layering.
  • Sexual: Involves flowers. Pollen (male) meets ovule (female) via pollination (wind, insects, water). Fertilization creates a zygote, which grows into a seed inside a fruit.
  • Seed Dispersal: Seeds spread by wind, water, animals, or explosion to reduce competition.

➗ Mathematics

Algebraic Expressions (Maths)

  • Basics: Uses variables (letters like x, y) and constants (numbers).
  • Terms: Parts of an expression separated by + or -.
  • Coefficients: The number multiplying the variable.
  • Operations: We can add or subtract only like terms (same variables).
  • Polynomials: Classified by terms: Monomial (1), Binomial (2), Trinomial (3).

Simple Linear Equations (Maths)

  • Definition: An equation with one variable raised to the power of 1.
  • Solving: Find the value of the variable that makes LHS = RHS.
  • Methods: Trial and error, balancing, or transposition (moving numbers across the equals sign and changing signs).

Lines and Angles (Maths)

  • Pairs: Complementary (sum to 90°), Supplementary (sum to 180°), Adjacent (share vertex/arm).
  • Linear Pair: Adjacent angles forming a straight line (sum to 180°).
  • Vertically Opposite: Equal angles formed when two lines intersect.
  • Transversal: A line crossing two or more lines. If lines are parallel, corresponding and alternate angles are equal.

Perimeter and Area (Maths)

  • Perimeter: Distance around a shape (sum of sides).
  • Area: Space enclosed by a shape.
    • Rectangle: Length × Breadth
    • Square: Side × Side
    • Triangle: ½ × Base × Height
    • Circle: πr² (Circumference = 2πr)
  • Applications: Calculating paths, fencing costs, and land areas.

Comparing Quantities (Maths)

  • Ratio: Comparison of two quantities (e.g., 2:1).
  • Percentage: Value per hundred (%). Used for discounts, marks, and growth.
  • Profit & Loss: Profit happens when Selling Price > Cost Price.
  • Simple Interest: Extra money paid on borrowed funds (SI = P×R×T / 100).

(a) Is water a renewable resource? How?
* Answer: Yes, water is a renewable resource. It is constantly recycled through the hydrological cycle (evaporation, condensation, precipitation) which replenishes water sources naturally.

(b) Mention the factors that give rise to ocean currents.
* Answer: The factors include:
1. Action of planetary winds.
2. Variation in temperature and density of ocean water.
3. Rotation of the Earth.
4. Shapes of coastlines.

(c) Why is it important to use water judiciously?
* Answer: Only 1% of water on Earth is available as surface freshwater for human consumption. Since freshwater is the most valuable natural resource and is limited, it must be used carefully without waste or pollution.

(d) Distinguish between:
* (i) Waves and Tides:
* Waves: Alternative rise and fall of surface water due to wind action. They travel in the direction of the wind.
* Tides: Periodic rise and fall of ocean water at regular intervals (twice daily) caused by gravitational pull of the moon and sun.
* (ii) Warm and Cold Ocean Currents:
* Warm Currents: Originate near the Equator, move towards poles, raise temperature of coastal areas, less dense.
* Cold Currents: Originate near Poles, move towards Equator, lower temperature of coastal areas, more dense.

7. Long Answer Type Questions

(a) What are the different types of tides? Explain them in detail with their diagrams.
* Answer:
1. Spring Tide: Occurs during Full Moon and New Moon days when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are in a straight line. This results in extra high tides.
2. Neap Tide: Occurs during the first and last quarter of the Moon when the Sun and Moon are at right angles. This results in low tides.

(b) How are ocean currents different from waves?
* Answer:
* Movement: Waves are up-and-down movements of surface water caused by wind. Currents are continuous horizontal movements of water in a definite direction (streams).
* Cause: Waves are caused primarily by wind. Currents are caused by winds, temperature differences, salinity, and Earth's rotation.
* Effect: Waves affect the immediate shoreline. Currents affect climate over large regions and navigation.

(c) Mention the important uses of tides.
* Answer:
1. Navigation: High tides help ships enter or leave harbours easily.
2. Fishing: More fish come near the shore during high tides, aiding fishermen.
3. Electricity Generation: Energy from seawater gushing in during high tides can be tapped to generate electricity.

(d) Do warm and cold ocean currents affect navigation? Find out the effects.
* Answer: Yes. Where warm and cold currents meet, dense fog is produced which reduces visibility, making it risky for ships to sail. Also, warm currents keep seas free from icebergs in cold regions, providing safe sailing.

(e) Highlight the impacts of Tsunami.
* Answer:
* Loss of Life: Over 1.8 lakh lives were lost in the 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami.
* Property Damage: Houses flattened, trees uprooted, automobiles submerged.
* Livelihood Loss: Agricultural land flooded with saline water; thousands lost livelihoods.
* Emotional Scars: Healing emotional trauma was difficult for survivors.

# Acids, Bases and Salts

## Acidic Substances

Substances containing acid (from Latin acer meaning sour) are called acidic substances.
Acids are of two types: mineral acids and organic acids.

### Mineral Acids

Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) and Nitric acid (HNO₃) are examples of mineral acids that are used in various industries. These acids are also found as chemical substances (minerals) existing in nature.
Organic Acid
Acids occur naturally in animal and plant materials.
Some examples of organic acids with their sources are given in Table 5.1.

### Strong and Weak Acids

Acids can be strong or weak. A strong acid is highly corrosive and can cause severe burns.
All strong acids are very corrosive, and for that reason, cannot be stored in metal containers. They are either stored in glass or ceramic containers or in wooden barrels made of wood. Hydrochloric acid, sulphuric acid, and nitric acid are examples of strong acids.

Weak acids, on the other hand, are not as destructive as strong acids.
Weak acids are mostly organic acids. Some of the organic acids are a part of our diet. Acetic acid, which is used to make vinegar, is an example of weak acid. It is also present in lemon juice and citric acid water. The fizzy drinks and soda water contain carbonic acid, which gives them a pleasant taste.
Acetic acid is not an organic acid; it is a mineral acid and a weak acid.

## Properties of Acids

Some of the characteristic properties of acids are:
- Acids are sour to taste
- Acids are corrosive in nature (e.g., lemon juice)
- Acids also have the ability to corrode metals such as iron and steel.
For this reason, acids are generally stored in glassware.

### BASIC SUBSTANCES

Substances containing a base are called basic substances. Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)_2) are examples of bases used in the laboratory (Fig 3.4). Corn starch, fresh egg white, etc., are other examples of bases.

Bases may have a strong irritating odour and should be used with caution as they can harm the skin and eyes.

# Strong and Weak Bases

As acids, bases can be strong or weak.
Strong bases are very corrosive and can burn the skin. These bases should be handled carefully. Caustic soda or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and caustic potash or potassium hydroxide (KOH) are examples of strong bases.

On the other hand, copper hydroxide [Cu(OH)_2], zinc hydroxide [Zn(OH)_2], and ammonium hydroxide (NH_4OH) are weak bases. Ammonium hydroxide is produced from dissolving ammonia (NH_3) in water.

## Properties of Bases

Some of the characteristic properties of bases are:
- **Bases are bitter to taste** - A bitter taste is characteristic of all bases.
- **Solutions of bases are slippery to touch** - If you apply a drop or two of a very dilute solution of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) on your fingers and then rub them, they will feel slippery.

## Fact File

Bases may or may not be soluble in water. Bases that can dissolve in water are called alkalis.
Some examples of soluble bases or alkalis are sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide, and calcium hydroxide.

### Uses of Bases

- **Calcium Hydroxide (Slaked Lime) [Ca(OH)_2]**
  - It is used to neutralize the acidity in soils.
  - It is an ingredient in whitewash and mortar.
  - It is a component of Bordeaux mixture for protecting agricultural crops from pests.
  - It is used in the preparation of dry mixes for painting and decorating.

- **Magnesium Hydroxide ( Milk of magnesia) [Mg(OH)_2]**
  - It is used as an antacid or laxative. It helps to correct excess acidity in the stomach.

### Sodium Hydroxide (Caustic soda) [NaOH]

It is used in the manufacture of paper and textiles.
- In homes, it is occasionally used to unblock drains.
- It is used in the manufacture of soaps (Fig. 5.5) and detergents.

# Indicators

How would you find out whether a substance is acidic, basic, or neutral? Tasting the substance is one way of doing it! But this is not a safe way. To overcome this, certain chemicals called indicators have been developed.

## The Most Common Indicator Used in the Laboratory: Litmus Paper

The most common indicator used in the laboratory is the litmus paper. It is extracted from lichens and is available either in the form of solutions or in the form of strips of paper known as litmus paper. Litmus paper turns blue under basic conditions (Fig. 5.6) and red under acidic conditions (Fig. 5.7).

The solutions that do not show a change in color by any of the indicators are neither acidic nor basic. These substances, as you already know, are called neutral substances. Some examples of neutral substances are water, sodium chloride, sugar, etc.

## Universal Indicators

Acids or bases can be either strong or weak. Scientists have made it very easy to know the strength of an acid or a base by giving them what has been given is known as pH number. It ranges from 1 to 14. pH 1 is considered strongly acidic, pH 14 strongly basic, and pH 7 neutral. You can measure pH of a solution by using a universal indicator.

## Natural Indicators

Indicators can be prepared easily from brightly coloured parts of plants such as flowers (china rose), roots (beetroot), stems (turmeric), and leaves (red cabbage).

Indicator solutions can be prepared by boiling coloured parts of the plant, such as petals in water for 10 minutes and straining out the plant part. This solution gives a different colour in acidic or basic conditions.

For example, red cabbage juice will change to deep red with acids, to purple with neutrals, and to green and yellow with bases. Onion juice also shows similar colour changes. You can directly put some rose petals in a dish containing vinegar or lemon juice and a few petals in soap solution and see the difference in colour.

### Activity

**Aim:** To see the effect of a natural indicator

#### Materials needed: Turmeric powder, alcohol, water, soap solution, and beaker

#### Method:
1. Mix 5g turmeric (of tumeric (haldi)) in 14 cup of alcohol in a small beaker. You can also mix it with water but turmeric dissolves better in alcohol.
2. Prepare a soap solution in another beaker by dissolving a small piece of washing soap in water.
3. Add a few drops of the tumeric–alcohol solution to the soap solution.

#### Observation:

#### Conclusion: Soap is a base, and the compound (called curcumin) that gives turmeric its yellow colour reacts with soap (a base) to form a red compound.

## Salts

Salts are general terms used for the substances that form when an acid and a base react with each other. This reaction is called neutralization reaction. The reaction between vinegar and baking soda is a neutralization reaction. Vinegar, as we already know, contains an acid and baking soda contains a base.

When vinegar reacts with baking soda, a salt is formed. Water and carbon dioxide gas are also the products of this reaction.

Similarly, common table salt is formed by the reaction of hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sodium chloride (NaOH).

hydrochloric acid + sodium chloride → sodium chloride + water

Salts can be acidic, basic, or neutral. Acidic salts are formed when a strong acid reacts with a weak base. Basic salts are formed when a strong base reacts with a weak acid.

When a strong acid reacts with a strong base, neutral salts are formed.

Strong Acid + Weak Base → Salt (acid), Strong Base + Weak Acid → Salt (basic)

# Properties of Salts

Some of the characteristic properties of salts are:

- Melting and boiling points: Salts are mostly solids, which melt as well as boil at high temperatures.
- Solubility in water: Salts are generally soluble in water. For example, sodium chloride, potassium sulphate, aluminium nitrate, ammonium carbonate, etc., are soluble salts while silver nitrate, copper chloride, copper carbonate, etc., are insoluble in water.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing the solubility of different salts in water: Sodium chloride (soluble), Potassium sulphate (soluble), Aluminium nitrate (soluble), Ammonium carbonate (soluble), Silver nitrate (insoluble), Copper chloride (insoluble), Copper carbonate (insoluble). Arrows indicate the direction of solubility.

[IMAGE_END]

- Power of crystallization: When a salt is dissolved in water, it forms crystals with water molecules present in them. This water is called the water of crystallization and such salts are called hydrated salts.
  - For example, copper sulphate crystal has five molecules of water for each copper sulphate molecule. This is written as CuSO₄·5H₂O. The water of crystallization gives the salt its characteristic colour to some crystals. For example, copper sulphate is blue in colour (Fig. 3.10). On heating, hydrated salts lose their water of crystallization and, as a result, the crystals lose their shape and convert themselves into a powdery substance.
- The hydrated salts that undergo dehydration on heating are called anhydrous salts.

When hydrated copper sulphate is heated, it gives out water molecules to form white powdery anhydrous copper sulphate. On addition of water, this substance can convert back to a hydrated copper sulphate solution again.
