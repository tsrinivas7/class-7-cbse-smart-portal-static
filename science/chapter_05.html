Chapter 05

Chapter Summary

5 Acids, Bases, and Salts

This chapter introduces different types of substances based on their chemical properties, specifically focusing on acidity, basicity, and the formation of salts. It covers how to identify these substances using indicators and explains their uses in daily life and industry.

ACIDIC SUBSTANCES

Substances containing an acid are called acidic substances. They are generally sour to taste and corrosive in nature.

Mineral Acid

Mineral acids are produced from chemical substances (minerals) existing in nature and are commonly used in laboratories.

  • Acidic substances — Substances containing an acid are called acidic substances.
  • Mineral acids — Examples include hydrochloric acid ($HCl$), sulphuric acid ($H_2SO_4$), and nitric acid ($HNO_3$). These are produced from minerals in nature.
  • Mineral acids are highly reactive towards metals and cannot be stored in metal containers; they are stored in glass or ceramic containers.
  • They are strong acids and can cause severe burns upon contact with human tissues.
  • They damage clothes, wood, and paper, requiring careful handling.

Organic Acid

Organic acids occur naturally in animal and plant materials and are mostly weak acids.

  • Organic acids — Acids that occur naturally in plant and animal materials are called organic acids.
  • Common examples include tartaric acid (grapes), acetic acid (vinegar), citric acid (lemons), and lactic acid (milk).
  • Most organic acids are weak acids and are often part of our diet.
  • Carbonic acid is found in fizzy drinks and soda water; it is a mineral acid but acts as a weak acid.
  • Organic acids give sour tastes to fruits like lemons, oranges, and apples.

Strong and Weak Acids

Acids are classified based on their strength and concentration, which determines their reactivity and safety.

  • Strong acid — A strong acid is highly corrosive and can cause severe burns. All strong acids are mineral acids.
  • Weak acids — Weak acids are not as destructive as strong acids and are mostly organic acids.
  • Dilute acids — Acids in which the amount of water is more are called dilute acids.
  • Concentrated acids — Acids in which the amount of water is less are called concentrated acids.
  • Vinegar is typically a 3–8% solution of acetic acid in water, making it a weak acid.

Properties of Acids

Acids have specific physical and chemical characteristics that distinguish them from other substances.

  • Corrosive — Tending to destroy slowly by chemical action.
  • Acids are sour to taste and are corrosive in nature. Along with sour taste, acids corrode metals such as iron and aluminium.
  • Acids are soluble in water either at room temperature or on heating to form a clear solution.
  • Mineral acids like nitric and sulphuric acids can burn human tissues and damage clothes.
  • Due to their corrosive nature, acids are generally stored in glassware rather than metal containers.

Uses of Acids

Different acids have specific applications in industries, households, and daily life.

  • Hydrochloric Acid (HCl) — Used in industries for heating applications, removing deposits from boilers, and cleaning sinks/sanitary ware.
  • Sulphuric Acid ($H_2SO_4$) — Called the 'King of chemicals'; used in car batteries, manufacture of paints, drugs, dyes, and fertilizers.
  • Nitric Acid ($HNO_3$) — Used by goldsmiths with hydrochloric acid to clean ornaments (aqua regia) and for producing fertilizers like ammonium nitrate.
  • Acetic Acid ($CH_3COOH$) — Main ingredient of vinegar; used to enhance food flavor, preserve pickles, and clean windows/floors.
  • Acids create an environment where most microorganisms cannot live, helping in food preservation (e.g., pickles, ketchups).

BASIC SUBSTANCES

Substances containing a base are called basic substances. They are bitter to taste and feel slippery to touch.

Strong and Weak Bases

Like acids, bases are categorized into strong and weak types based on their corrosiveness and solubility.

  • Basic substances — Substances containing a base are called basic substances.
  • Strong bases — Very corrosive and can burn the skin. Examples include caustic soda (sodium hydroxide, $NaOH$) and caustic potash (potassium hydroxide, $KOH$).
  • Weak bases — Less destructive. Examples include copper hydroxide ($Cu(OH)_2$), zinc hydroxide ($Zn(OH)_2$), and ammonium hydroxide ($NH_4OH$).
  • Ammonium hydroxide is produced by dissolving ammonia ($NH_3$) in water.
  • Bases may have a strong irritating odour and should be used with caution as they can harm skin and eyes.

Properties of Bases

Bases exhibit distinct sensory and chemical properties compared to acids.

  • Bases are bitter to taste. A bitter taste is characteristic of all bases.
  • Solutions of bases are slippery to touch. Rubbing fingers with a dilute solution of sodium hydroxide feels slippery.
  • Alkalis — Bases that can dissolve in water are called alkalis. Examples include sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide, and calcium hydroxide.
  • Some bases are soluble in water, while others may not be.
  • Fact File: HCl is present in the stomach, saliva is basic, and healthy blood is basic.

Uses of Bases

Bases play a crucial role in agriculture, household cleaning, and manufacturing.

  • Calcium Hydroxide (Slaked lime) — Used to neutralize acidity in soils, as an ingredient in whitewash/mortar, and in Bordeaux mixture for crop protection.
  • Magnesium Hydroxide (Milk of magnesia) — Used as an antacid or laxative to correct excess acidity in the stomach.
  • Sodium Hydroxide (Caustic soda) — Used in manufacturing paper, textiles, soaps, detergents, and unblocking drains.
  • Soaps are manufactured using sodium hydroxide.
  • Bases are essential components in many household cleaners.

INDICATORS

Indicators help identify whether a substance is acidic, basic, or neutral without tasting it.

Universal Indicators

Universal indicators allow scientists to determine the strength of an acid or a base using a pH scale.

  • Indicators — Substances that show a change in colour when brought in contact with acids and bases are called indicators.
  • Litmus — The most common indicator, extracted from lichens, available as red or blue paper/solution. Blue litmus turns red in acid; red litmus turns blue in base.
  • Neutral substances — Solutions that do not show a change in colour in any of the indicators are neither acidic nor basic (e.g., water, sugar).
  • pH number — Ranges from 1 to 14. pH 1 is strongly acidic, pH 14 is strongly basic, and pH 7 is neutral.
  • Universal Indicators — A mixture of indicators giving different colours for different pH values.
  • pH paper — Paper soaked with universal indicator used to measure pH by matching the colour to a chart.

Natural Indicators

Natural indicators can be prepared from plants and show colour changes in acidic and basic solutions.

  • Indicators can be prepared from brightly coloured parts of plants like flowers (china rose), roots (beetroot), stems (turmeric), and leaves (red cabbage).
  • Indicator solutions are made by boiling coloured plant parts in water and straining them.
  • Red cabbage juice changes to deep red with acids, purple with neutrals, and green/yellow with bases.
  • Turmeric turns red when mixed with soap solution (a base).
  • Onion juice also shows similar colour changes between acidic and basic mediums.

SALTS

Salts are formed when an acid and a base react with each other in a neutralization reaction.

Neutralization reactions

A neutralization reaction occurs when an acid reacts with a base to produce salt and water.

  • Salts — A general term used for substances formed when an acid and a base react with each other.
  • Neutralization reaction — The reaction in which an acid reacts with a base to form salt is called a neutralization reaction.
  • Reaction example: Hydrochloric acid + Sodium hydroxide $\rightarrow$ Sodium chloride + Water.
  • Acidic salts — Formed when a strong acid reacts with a weak base.
  • Basic salts — Formed when a strong base reacts with a weak acid.
  • Neutral salts — Formed when a strong acid reacts with a strong base.

Properties of Salts

Salts have unique physical properties regarding their state, solubility, and crystal structure.

  • Melting and boiling points — Salts are mostly solids that melt and boil at high temperatures.
  • Solubility in water — Salts are generally soluble in water (e.g., sodium chloride), though some are insoluble (e.g., silver chloride).
  • Water of crystallization — The water molecules present in solid crystals of salts is called the water of crystallization.
  • Hydrated salts — Salts with water of crystallization are called hydrated salts (e.g., Copper sulphate $CuSO_4 \cdot 5H_2O$).
  • Anhydrous salts — Salts which have lost their water of crystallization are called anhydrous salts (e.g., white powdery copper sulphate after heating).

Uses of Salts

Various salts are utilized in construction, agriculture, medicine, and industry.

  • Calcium carbonate ($CaCO_3$) — Used for flooring (marble), making lime/cement, and extracting iron.
  • Sodium carbonate ($Na_2CO_3 \cdot 10H_2O$) — Used in cleaning, detergents, water softening, and glass manufacture.
  • Sodium bicarbonate ($NaHCO_3$) — Used in bakery products and medicines.
  • Silver nitrate ($AgNO_3$) — Used in photography for developing films.
  • Ammonium nitrate ($NH_4NO_3$) — Used in fertilizers and explosives.
  • Potash alum ($K_2SO_4 \cdot Al_2(SO_4)_3 \cdot 24H_2O$) — Used in purification of water.
  • Sodium chloride (Common salt) — Used as seasoning for food and in manufacturing chlorine and sodium carbonate.

Applications of Neutralization

Neutralization reactions are applied frequently in everyday situations to solve problems caused by acidity or alkalinity.

  • Insect Sting — Bee stings inject acidic liquid, neutralized by calamine lotion (contains zinc carbonate, a base).
  • Indigestion — Caused by excess stomach acidity, treated with antacids like baking soda or magnesium hydroxide.
  • Soil Treatment — If soil is too acidic, bases like quicklime or slaked lime are added. If too alkaline, sulphur is added.
  • Factory Waste — Liquid waste containing acid is neutralized by adding a base like slaked lime before discharge to protect aquatic life.

2. Why acids or bases are sometimes added to soil?
Answer: Most plants grow best in neutral soil. If soil is too acidic, a base (like quicklime, slaked lime, or chalk) is added. If soil is too alkaline, sulphur is added. This helps plants grow properly.

3. What is a neutralization reaction? Give an example.
Answer: A neutralization reaction is the reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water.
Example: Hydrochloric acid + Sodium hydroxide $\rightarrow$ Sodium chloride + Water.

4. How can a hydrated salt be converted into anhydrous salt? Give an example.
Answer: By heating the hydrated salt. On heating, hydrated salts lose their water of crystallization and change to a powdery substance.
Example: When hydrated copper sulphate is heated, it loses water molecules to form white powdery anhydrous copper sulphate.

2. Explain the following:
a. Vinegar is used in packaged food items.
Answer: Microorganisms cannot live in an acidic environment. Vinegar creates an acidic environment that slows down their activities or kills them, acting as a preservative.
b. Slaked lime is added to factory waste.
Answer: Liquid waste from factories often contains acid which can kill aquatic life. Slaked lime (a base) is added to neutralize the acid before disposal.
c. Magnesium hydroxide is used as an antacid.
Answer: Excess acidity in the stomach leads to indigestion. Magnesium hydroxide (Milk of magnesia) is a base that neutralizes the excess acid in the stomach.

Answer: Strong acids and bases are highly corrosive. They can burn human tissues upon contact, damage clothes, and cause severe burns. Therefore, they must be handled with care.

3. What is water of crystallization? Explain with the help of an example.
Answer: Generally, salts are found as crystals with water molecules present in them. This water is called the water of crystallization. Such salts are called hydrated salts. This water gives the crystal its shape and sometimes its colour.
Example: Copper sulphate crystal ($CuSO_4 \cdot 5H_2O$) has five molecules of water for each copper sulphate molecule. It is blue due to this water. On heating, it loses this water to become white anhydrous powder.

Strong and Weak Acids

Acids can be strong or weak. A strong acid is highly corrosive and can cause severe burns. All strong acids are mineral acids. These acids are highly reactive towards metals and for that reason, cannot be stored in metal containers. They are either stored in glass or ceramic containers. The mineral acids also act against clothes and wood. Hydrochloric acid, sulphuric acid, and nitric acid are examples of strong acids.

Weak acids, on the other hand, are not as destructive as strong acids. Weak acids are mostly organic acids. Most of the organic acids are a part of our diet. Acetic acid, which is used to make vinegar, is an example of weak acids. Vinegar is typically a 3–8% solution of acetic acid in water. The fizzy drinks and soda water contain carbonic acid, which gives them a pleasant taste. Carbonic acid is not an organic acid. It is a mineral acid and a weak acid.

Properties of Acids

Some of the characteristic properties of acids are:

  • Acids are sour to taste and are corrosive in nature. Along with sour taste, acids also have the ability to corrode metals such as iron and aluminium. For this reason, acids are generally stored in glassware.
  • Mineral acids such as nitric and sulphuric acids can burn human tissues upon contact and damage clothes, paper, etc. Thus, one should handle acids with care.

Strong and Weak Bases

Like acids, bases can be strong or weak.

Strong bases are very corrosive and can burn the skin. These bases should be handled carefully. Caustic soda or sodium hydroxide ($NaOH$) and caustic potash or potassium hydroxide ($KOH$) are strong and corrosive bases.

On the other hand, copper hydroxide [$Cu(OH)_2$], zinc hydroxide [$Zn(OH)_2$], and ammonium hydroxide ($NH_4OH$) are weak bases. Ammonium hydroxide is produced from dissolving ammonia ($NH_3$) in water.

Properties of Bases

Some of the characteristic properties of bases are:

  • Bases are bitter to taste A bitter taste is characteristic of all bases.
  • Solutions of bases are slippery to touch If you apply a drop or two of a very dilute solution of sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide on your fingers and then rub them, they will feel slippery.
  • Bases may or may not be soluble in water Bases that can dissolve in water are called alkalis. Some examples of soluble bases or alkalis are sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide, and calcium hydroxide.

INDICATORS

How would you find out whether a substance is acidic, basic, or neutral? Tasting the substance is one way of doing it. But this is not a safe way. To overcome this, certain chemicals called indicators have been developed.

Indicators are substances that show a change in colour when brought in contact with acids and bases.

The most common indicator used in the laboratory is the litmus. It is extracted from lichens and is available either in the form of solutions or in the form of strips of paper known as litmus paper. Litmus paper is either red or blue in colour. It changes colour depending upon whether the compound is an acid or a base. Blue litmus paper turns red under acidic conditions (Fig. 5.6), and red litmus paper turns blue under basic conditions (Fig. 5.7).

Natural Indicators

SALTS

Salts is a general term used for the substances that are formed when an acid and a base react with each other. This reaction is called a neutralization reaction.

The reaction between vinegar and baking soda is a neutralization reaction. Vinegar, as we already know, contains an acid and baking soda contains a base.

When vinegar reacts with baking soda, a salt is formed. Water and carbon dioxide gas are also the products of this reaction.

Similarly, common table salt is formed by the reaction of hydrochloric acid (HCl) with sodium hydroxide (NaOH).

hydrochloric acid + sodium hydroxide $\longrightarrow$ sodium chloride + water
(acid) (base) (salt)

Get it Right
In common usage, salt refers to table salt. In chemistry, salt refers to the product of the reaction between an acid and a base.

Salts can be acidic, basic, or neutral. Acidic salts are formed when a strong acid reacts with a weak base. Basic salts are formed when a strong base reacts with a weak acid. When a strong acid reacts with a strong base, neutral salts are formed.

Strong Acid + Weak base $\rightarrow$ Acidic Salts
Strong base + Weak acid $\rightarrow$ Basic Salts
Strong acid + Strong base $\rightarrow$ Neutral Salts

Insect Sting
When a bee stings, it injects an acidic liquid into the skin. The sting is neutralized by rubbing calamine lotion on the skin. The lotion contains zinc carbonate (a base).

Indigestion
To digest food, the liquid in the stomach must be acidic. But too much acidity leads to indigestion. One takes antacids, which usually contain baking soda or magnesium hydroxide, to neutralize the excess acid.

Soil Treatment
Most plants grow best in neutral soil. If the soil is too acidic, or too alkaline, the plants do not grow properly or do not grow at all. Liquid waste from factories often contains acid. If it reaches a river, the acid will kill aquatic life. This is prevented by neutralizing the waste by adding a base such as slaked lime.

Properties of Salts

Some of the characteristic properties of salts are:

Melting and boiling points Salts are mostly solids, which melt as well as boil at high temperatures.

Solubility in water Salts are generally soluble in water. For example, sodium chloride, potassium sulphate, aluminium nitrate, ammonium carbonate, etc., are soluble salts while silver chloride, lead chloride, copper carbonate, etc., are insoluble in water.

Water of crystallization Generally, salts are found as crystals with water molecules present in them. This water is called the water of crystallization and such salts are called hydrated salts.

For example, copper sulphate crystal has five molecules of water for each copper sulphate molecule. This is written as $CuSO_4 \cdot 5H_2O$. This water of crystallization gives the crystal its shape. It also gives colour to some crystals. For example, copper sulphate is also called blue vitriol due to its blue colour (Fig. 5.10). On heating, hydrated salts lose their water of crystallization and, as a result, the crystals lose their shape and colour and change to a powdery substance.

[IMAGE_START] A small pile of bright blue crystalline powder. Below the image is the caption: "Copper sulphate ($CuSO_4$) Fig. 5.10 Blue vitriol" [IMAGE_END]

The hydrated salts that have lost their water of crystallization are called anhydrous salts.

When hydrated copper sulphate is heated, it gives out water molecules to form white powdery anhydrous copper sulphate. On addition of water, this substance can convert back to a hydrated copper sulphate solution again.

Water of crystallization The water molecules present in solid crystals of salts is called the water of crystallization.
Hydrated salts Salts with water of crystallization are called hydrated salts.
Anhydrous salts Salts which have lost their water of crystallization are called anhydrous salts.

Summary

  • Acidic substances have a sour taste, are corrosive in nature, and are soluble in water.
  • Depending on the amount of water, acids can be either dilute or concentrated.
  • A strong acid is highly corrosive.
  • Basic substances are bitter to taste and soapy to touch.
  • Bases that dissolve in water are called alkalis.
  • Bases form a part of many household cleaners.
  • Neutral substances are neither acidic nor basic.
  • Water is neither acidic nor basic.
  • Indicators are substances that show a change in colour when brought in contact with acids and bases.
  • Salts are formed when an acid reacts with a base.
