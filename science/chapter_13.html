13 Electric Current and Its Effects

Introduction

  • Electricity is a necessity in modern life, powering various devices like fans, mixers, bulbs, and irons.
  • Different electrical appliances work based on different effects produced by electric current flowing in a circuit.
  • This chapter studies electric circuits and the heating and magnetic effects of electric current.

ELECTRIC CIRCUIT DIAGRAM

Electric circuit — The unbroken path through which an electric current flows is called an electric circuit.
Circuit diagram — A drawing of an electrical circuit with standard symbols is called a circuit diagram.

  • An electric circuit consists of components represented by standard symbols in a diagram.
  • To draw a circuit diagram, there must be a source (cell/battery), wires without discontinuity, and a switch which can create a gap.
  • When the switch is ON, the circuit is closed and current flows; when OFF, it is open and current stops.
  • Cells can be connected in series (positive to negative) to increase voltage output.
  • A cell holder is a plastic box with metal contacts designed to hold one or more cells securely.

How to connect cells to make a battery?

  • Connecting cells together increases the voltage output (e.g., three 1.5 V cells make a 4.5 V battery).
  • Cells must be connected such that the positive terminal of one cell connects to the negative terminal of the next.
  • Identical cells should be used to ensure proper functioning.

Cell holder

  • Most battery-operated devices have a designated place for cells called a cell holder.
  • Cell holders are made of plastic with metal contacts and '+' and '-' signs inscribed inside.
  • They are designed to hold one or more cells safely within an electrical circuit.

HEATING EFFECT OF ELECTRIC CURRENT

Filament — A very thin wire made of special material (such as tungsten) inside an incandescent bulb through which current passes.
Heating effect of the electric current — The phenomenon where electric current passing through a conductor causes it to get heated.
Heating element — Tightly wound coils in appliances made of nichrome alloy that heat up when current passes through.
Nichrome — An alloy of nickel and chromium used to make heating elements in appliances.

  • When current passes through a filament in a bulb, it gets heated and glows due to the heating effect.
  • While heating effect can cause energy loss and damage, it is ingeniously used in appliances like irons, toasters, and ovens.
  • Many appliances contain heating elements made of nichrome which glow dull red when hot.
  • An activity demonstrates that a glowing bulb becomes warmer than when it is off, proving the heating effect.

Electric Fuse

Electric Fuse — A safety device which prevents excessive current from flowing in a circuit is called an electric fuse.

  • An electric fuse prevents large amounts of current from damaging appliances by cutting off the supply.
  • It works on the principle of the heating effect of electric current.
  • Fuses consist of a thin wire made of materials like lead, tin, or alloys that melt easily when heated.
  • If current exceeds the limit, the wire melts (fuse 'blows'), breaking the circuit and stopping current flow.
  • The number stamped on a cartridge fuse signifies the maximum current (in amperes) it can handle before melting.

Principle of an Electric Fuse

  • The amount of heating depends on the amount of current flowing through the wire.
  • Greater current produces more heat, causing the fuse wire to melt if it exceeds the design limit.
  • This breaks the circuit and protects the appliance from fire or damage.

Miniature circuit breaker (MCB)

Miniature circuit breaker (MCB) — An automatically operated electric switch that protects an electric circuit during an overload or short circuit.

  • An MCB protects circuits during overloads or short circuits and can be reset manually or automatically.
  • Unlike fuses, MCBs do not need replacement after a single use; they can be switched back on.
  • MCBs are commonly found on distribution boards or switchboards in homes.

MAGNETIC EFFECT OF ELECTRIC CURRENT

Electromagnet — A piece of soft iron with an insulated wire wound around it, that acts as a magnet when current flows in the wire, is called an electromagnet.
Solenoid — A long wire that has been wound many times in a tight coil around a hollow or solid core.

  • Electricity and magnetism are closely related; a current-carrying conductor behaves like a magnet.
  • Passing current through a wire deflects the needle of a magnetic compass placed near it.
  • An electromagnet loses its magnetic properties when the current is switched off.
  • The strength of an electromagnet increases with more turns of wire and higher current.
  • Wrapping wire around a soft iron core significantly increases the magnetic strength.

Electromagnet

  • An electromagnet acts like a magnet only when electric current is passed through it.
  • Commercially used electromagnets have insulated wire wound tightly many times around a cylindrical core.
  • A piece of soft iron in the core increases the strength of the electromagnet.

Solenoid

  • A solenoid is a long wire wound many times in a tight coil around a core, having a cylindrical shape.
  • It is often used as an electromagnet in various applications.

Know Your Scientist

  • Hans Christian Oersted was a physicist and chemist born in Denmark in 1777.
  • He discovered the magnetic effect of electric current by demonstrating that a magnetic needle deflects near a current-carrying wire.
  • The unit of magnetic field strength in the CGS system is named 'oersted' in his honour.

Uses of Electromagnets

  • Electromagnets are used in cranes to pick up cars in scrapyards and separate iron from garbage.
  • They are used in loudspeakers, telephones, and electric motors (fans, washing machines).
  • Electric bells utilize electromagnets to ring continuously.

Electric Bell

  • An electric bell contains an electromagnet that pulls a strip of iron, making a hammer hit the gong.
  • When the switch is pushed, current flows to the electromagnet, attracting the soft iron strip.
  • As the strip moves, it hits the gong and breaks the circuit at the interrupter screw.
  • The electromagnet demagnetizes, the strip returns, completing the circuit again, repeating the cycle quickly.

Electric buzzer

  • Modern electric buzzers and musical bells work on principles different from traditional electric bells.
  • Buzzers are used in various places where sound alerts are needed.

IV. Long Answer Type Questions

  1. Question: Draw two electric circuits showing two different situations when an electric current will not flow through the circuit. Explain the reason in each case.
    • Answer:
      • Situation 1 (Open Switch): Draw a circuit with a battery, bulb, and a switch drawn in the open position (gap in the wire).
        • Reason: The circuit is incomplete/discontinuous. Current cannot flow across the gap.
      • Situation 2 (Broken Wire/Dead Cell): Draw a circuit where the wire is broken in the middle OR draw a cell with no connection.
        • Reason: There is no unbroken path for the current to flow, or there is no source of energy to drive the current.
  2. Question: Explain the working of an electric fuse.
    • Answer: An electric fuse is a safety device used to prevent damage from excessive current. It consists of a thin wire made of a material that melts easily (like lead-tin alloy) placed inside a glass or ceramic cartridge. If too much current flows through the circuit, the heat produced melts the fuse wire. This breaks the circuit and stops the flow of current, protecting the appliances and preventing fires.
  3. Question: Draw a rough figure of a simple electromagnet. How can we find out if it is magnetic or not?
    • Answer:
      • Figure Description: Draw an iron nail. Wrap insulated copper wire tightly around the nail multiple times. Connect the ends of the wire to a battery cell.
      • Test: Bring the nail close to small iron objects like paper clips or safety pins. If the nail attracts them, it is acting as a magnet. Alternatively, bring a magnetic compass near it; if the needle deflects, it confirms the presence of a magnetic field.

* Answer: Yes, it would still deflect the magnetic compass needle.

In an incandescent bulb (Fig. 13.3), electric current passes through a very thin wire made of a special material (such as tungsten) called the filament. When the bulb is switched on, the filament gets heated. This is due to the heating effect of the electric current.

A disadvantage is an advantage!

Generally, the heating effect of electric current is a bother because it results in a loss of electrical energy and also results in the damage of electrical appliances. However, the same heating effect can be put to good use! Many electrical appliances that we use every day actually make use of the heating effect of electric current. This is a classic example of how a disadvantage can be ingeniously turned into an advantage. This happens very often in Science. When you find a disadvantage in a particular effect or situation, look to see how you can turn it into an advantage!

The heating effect of current is used in several appliances, which include electric toasters, electric irons (Fig. 13.4), hot plates, electric hairdryers, and electric ovens (Fig. 13.5).

Let us now learn how an electric fuse works.

Principle of an Electric Fuse

The electric fuse works on the principle of heating effect of electric current. The amount of heating caused depends on the amount of current flowing through the wire. The greater the current, the more is the heat produced.

An electric fuse consists of a thin wire usually placed inside a glass or ceramic cartridge. The wire is made of a material that melts easily when heated. Examples of material used for fuse wire are lead, tin, alloys of lead and tin, aluminium, zinc, etc.

It is designed such that only a certain maximum amount of current can flow through it. If the current exceeds this amount, the heating in the wire causes it to melt. We say that the fuse 'blows'. This breaks the circuit and stops the flow of current in the circuit.

Know Your Scientist

Hans Christian Oersted was born in Denmark in the year 1777. He was both, a physicist and a chemist. He is known for his discovery of the magnetic effect of electric current. He demonstrated that a magnetic needle gets deflected when it is brought near a current carrying wire. In the CGS system of units, the unit of magnetic field strength is given the name oersted in his honour.

Summary

  • Three conditions are required to make electric current flow in a circuit: a source to provide electric current, an unbroken path for electric current to flow, and a conducting wire.
  • For the sake of simplicity, scientists have developed standard symbols for drawing an electrical circuit. Such a drawing of an electric circuit is known as a circuit diagram.
  • When an electric current passes through an electric conductor or a wire, the conductor/wire gets heated up. This is called the heating effect of electric current.
  • Electric irons, room heaters, electric ovens, water heaters, etc., use the heating effect of electric current to work.
  • An electric fuse is a safety device. It prevents a large amount of current from flowing into any appliance or device.
  • An electric fuse consists of a thin wire made of a material that melts easily when heated.
  • A wire carrying current can behave like a magnet and deflects the needle of a compass.
  • An electromagnet consists of a piece of soft iron (called the core), with an insulated wire wound around it. The soft iron piece acts like a magnet when a current passes through the wire.
  • Electromagnets are used in cranes, electric motors, telephones, loudspeakers, electric bells, etc.
  • An electric bell consists of a battery, an electric switch, an electromagnet, an adjusting screw (interrupter), a soft iron strip, a hammer, and a gong.
