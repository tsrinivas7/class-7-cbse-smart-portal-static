TRANSPORT OF SUBSTANCES

  • Transport of substances — The process of moving essential materials within a living body to ensure organ systems work efficiently.
  • Food — Substance transported to all cells where it is broken down for energy release.
  • Oxygen — Required for the oxidation process of food.
  • Water and minerals — Required for proper functioning of the body.
  • Waste products — Generated as a result of life activities and need to be removed from cells and the body.

TRANSPORT IN ANIMALS

  • Amoeba — Unicellular animal where nutrients and oxygen are received directly through cell diffusion.
  • Paramoecium — Unicellular animal where nutrients and oxygen are received directly through cell diffusion.
  • Hydra — Water transports food and oxygen to all parts of the body and takes body wastes away.
  • circulatory system — Specialized system in multicellular organisms consisting of the heart, arteries, veins, and capillaries.
  • Blood — The medium for transport of all necessary materials to different parts of the body.
  • Diffusion — Movement of molecules or gases from a region of higher concentration to that of lower concentration.

TRANSPORT IN HUMANS

  • Blood — Fluid that flows in blood vessels forming a medium for transporting nutrients, gases, water, and waste products.
  • plasma — Straw-coloured liquid in which blood cells float.
  • red blood cells (RBCs) — Flat disc-like cells containing haemoglobin that help in transporting oxygen.
  • white blood cells (WBCs) — Larger cells that protect the body against disease-causing microorganisms.
  • blood platelets — Colourless, irregularly shaped cells smaller than RBCs that stop bleeding.
  • haemoglobin — Red pigment in RBCs that combines with oxygen to form oxyhaemoglobin.
  • oxyhaemoglobin — Compound formed when haemoglobin combines with oxygen to transport oxygen.

Blood Vessels

  • arteries — Thick-walled tubes that carry blood away from the heart under high pressure.
  • veins — Thin-walled tubes that carry blood to the heart, containing valves to prevent backflow.
  • capillaries — Thinnest blood vessels connecting arteries to veins where diffusion of gases occurs.

The Heart

  • four-chambered — Description of the muscular organ that pumps blood into blood vessels.
  • heart beats — Rhythmic contractions and relaxations of heart muscles.
  • pulse — Throbbing sensation felt where an artery comes close to the surface of the skin.
  • stethoscope — Instrument used to hear the heart beat.
  • sphygmomanometer — Instrument used to measure blood pressure.
  • right auricle — Chamber receiving deoxygenated blood from all parts of the body.
  • left auricle — Chamber with thinner walls pumping blood into the left ventricle.
  • right ventricle — Chamber pumping deoxygenated blood into the pulmonary artery.
  • left ventricle — Chamber with thicker walls pumping blood to the farthest parts of the body through the aorta.
  • pulmonary artery — Artery that takes deoxygenated blood to the lungs for oxygenation.
  • pulmonary vein — Vessel that brings back oxygen-rich blood from the lungs to the left auricle.
  • Aorta — Large vessel carrying blood to different parts of the body.
  • Tricuspid valve — Valve located between right auricle and right ventricle.
  • Bicuspid valve — Valve located between left auricle and left ventricle.
  • Valves — Structures that separate chambers so there is no mixing of blood and allow flow in one direction.
  • Septum — Wall separating the left and right sides of the heart.

TRANSPORT IN PLANTS

  • xylem — Tissue acting as a pipeline taking water and minerals from roots up the stem to leaves.
  • phloem — Tissue acting as a pipeline carrying prepared food to all parts of the plant.
  • translocation — Process by which food manufactured in leaves is sent to all parts of the plant.
  • Transpiration — Losing of water from the plant in the form of water vapour.
  • stomata — Openings present on the underside of leaves controlling water loss during transpiration.

Transpiration

  • Exchange of gases — Transpiration helps in opening stomata for absorbing carbon dioxide needed for photosynthesis.
  • Cooling effect — Evaporation of water vapour brings down the surface temperature of the plant.
  • Effect on mineral and water transport — Creates low pressure and pulling force to pull up water and minerals.

EXCRETORY SYSTEM

  • excretion — Process of removal of wastes from the cells of living organisms.
  • excretory system — Organ system responsible for the removal of wastes.
  • carbon dioxide — Waste formed during respiration, sent out with exhaled air.
  • urea — Nitrogenous waste formed inside the body, excreted in urine.
  • uric acid — Nitrogenous waste excreted along with urea.
  • nephrons — Functional units of the kidneys where absorption/filtration is carried out.
  • kidney — Pair of reddish, bean-shaped organs that filter waste substances from blood.
  • ureters — Two tubes connecting kidneys to the urinary bladder.
  • urinary bladder — Organ that stores urine.
  • urethra — Opening that removes urine from the body.
  • Dialysis — Method of removing toxic substances from the blood with the help of a machine.
  • artificial kidney — Another name for the dialysis machine.
  • kidney transplant — Procedure where a diseased kidney is replaced with a healthy kidney from a donor.

Excretion in Animals

  • Amoeba — Gets rid of body wastes by direct diffusion through cell membranes.
  • Paramecium — Gets rid of body wastes by direct diffusion through cell membranes.
  • Hydra — Gets rid of body wastes by direct diffusion through cell membranes.
  • Earthworms — Have tubular structures that help to get rid of body wastes.
  • Insects — Have tubular structures that help to get rid of body wastes.
  • Leeches — Have tubular structures that help to get rid of body wastes.

Excretion in Humans

  • Through exhaled air — Carbon dioxide formed after respiration is sent out of the body.
  • Through sweat — Skin acts as an excretory organ releasing urea, salts, and water to regulate body temperature.
  • Through the kidney — Nitrogenous wastes like urea and uric acid are excreted in the form of urine.

Excretion in Plants

  • Carbon dioxide — Product excreted as a result of respiration.
  • Oxygen — Product excreted as a result of photosynthesis.
  • Excess water — Excreted through stomata via the process called transpiration.
  • Excessive salts — Deposited in plants as crystals if present in high levels in soil or water.
  • Gums, resins, and latex — Toxic wastes excreted by older plants, used to manufacture paints, rubber, etc.

IV. Long answer type questions

  1. Explain the main differences between arteries, veins, and capillaries.
    • Answer:
      • Arteries: Carry blood away from the heart; thick-walled tubes; deeply placed under skin; blood moves under high pressure; no valves inside.
      • Veins: Carry blood to the heart; thinner walls; superficially placed; blood does not move under pressure; series of valves present to prevent backflow.
      • Capillaries: Thinnest blood vessels; connect arteries to veins; walls are thin enough for diffusion of gases and chemicals.
  2. Describe how the heart acts as the pumping station of the body.
    • Answer: The heart is a four-chambered muscular organ that pumps blood into the blood vessels. The pressure generated by its rhythmic contractions and relaxations (heart beats) is enough for the blood vessels to carry blood to all parts of the body. A normal heart beats about 60 to 80 times per minute.
  3. What is transpiration? Discuss the role that transpiration plays in a plant and the factors that affect the rate of transpiration.
    • Answer:
      • Definition: Transpiration is the losing of water from the plant in the form of water vapour, mainly occurring through the stomata present on the underside of the leaves.
      • Role:
        • Exchange of gases: Helps in opening of stomata for carbon dioxide absorption.
        • Cooling effect: Evaporation brings down surface temperature, preventing damage due to overheating.
        • Effect on mineral and water transport: Creates a pulling force to pull up water and minerals from roots.
      • Factors affecting rate:
        • Condition of air: Warmer air increases the rate.
        • Amount of moisture in air: Lesser moisture increases the rate.
        • Number of stomata: More stomata increase the rate.
        • Size of the leaves: Larger leaves increase the rate.
  4. With the help of a well-labelled diagram, describe the human excretory system.
    • Answer: The human excretory system comprises:
      • Kidneys: A pair of reddish, bean-shaped organs where urine is formed by filtering waste from blood.
      • Ureters: Two tubes that collect urine from the kidneys.
      • Urinary Bladder: Stores urine.
      • Urethra: Removes urine from the body.
      • Process: Waste substances in blood are removed by kidneys. Useful substances are reabsorbed. Urine is collected via ureters into the bladder and passed out through the urethra.

Section 3: Short Answer Questions

Q14. Why do veins contain valves inside them, whereas arteries generally do not?

Show Answer
✅ **Answer:** Veins have valves to prevent the blood from flowing back towards the organs because the blood pressure in veins is low. Arteries carry blood under high pressure away from the heart, so backflow is less likely, and they do not require valves.

Q15. Differentiate between transpiration and translocation in plants.

Show Answer
✅ **Answer:** Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the leaves through stomata, while translocation is the transport of prepared food from leaves to other parts of the plant through phloem tissue.

Q16. Explain why sweating is considered an excretory process in addition to regulating body temperature.

Show Answer
✅ **Answer:** Sweating helps regulate body temperature by cooling the skin through evaporation. It is also excretory because sweat glands secrete small quantities of urea, salts, and water, removing these waste products from the body.

Q17. What would happen to the rate of transpiration if the amount of moisture in the air increases?

Show Answer
✅ **Answer:** If the amount of moisture in the air increases, the rate of transpiration decreases because there is less difference in water vapor concentration between the leaf and the surrounding air.

Q18. What is the function of the semi-permeable egg membrane in the dialysis demonstration activity?

Show Answer
✅ **Answer:** The egg membrane allows solvent molecules (water) to pass through from higher to lower concentration but blocks solute molecules (sugar), simulating how a dialysis machine filters waste from blood.

Section 4: Descriptive Questions

Q19. Trace the path of deoxygenated blood starting from the body tissues until it returns to the heart after getting oxygenated. Mention the key chambers and vessels involved.

Show Answer
✅ **Answer:** Deoxygenated blood from body tissues enters the **Right Auricle** via veins. It flows into the **Right Ventricle**. The Right Ventricle pumps this blood into the **Pulmonary Artery**, which carries it to the lungs. In the lungs, the blood becomes oxygenated. Oxygen-rich blood returns to the heart through the **Pulmonary Vein** into the **Left Auricle**.

Q20. Describe the role of the ureters and urinary bladder in the human excretory system.

Show Answer
✅ **Answer:** The **ureters** are two tubes that collect urine formed in the kidneys and transport it down to the urinary bladder. The **urinary bladder** is a muscular sac that stores the urine until it is ready to be expelled from the body through the urethra.

TRANSPORT OF SUBSTANCES

The process of transport of substances is very important in a living body. This ensures that the different organ systems of the living organism work efficiently. The following are some of the substances that need to be transported.

Food to all the cells where it will be broken down/oxidized for the release of energy
Oxygen, which is required for the oxidation process
Water and minerals, which are required for proper functioning of the body of an organism
Waste products, generated as a result of life activities, which need to be removed from the cells and the body

TRANSPORT IN ANIMALS

In unicellular animals such as Amoeba and Paramoecium, no special transport system is required. Every part of the organism gets nutrients and oxygen directly through cell diffusion. In Hydra, water transports food and oxygen to all parts of the body and takes body wastes away from the different organs for excretion. In multicellular organisms, the transportation is done by a specialized system known as the circulatory system. It consists of the heart, arteries, veins, and capillaries. It forms a broad network, which carries Blood to all parts of the body. Blood is the medium for transport of all the necessary materials to different parts of the body.

TRANSPORT IN HUMANS

Humans have a well-developed and efficient circulatory system. It consists of (a) blood, (b) blood vessels: they are of three kinds namely, arteries, veins, and capillaries, and (c) a thick muscular organ called the heart.

Blood

Blood is a fluid that flows in the blood vessels. It forms a medium through which nutrients, important gases, water, and waste products are transported inside the organism. It constitutes about 8% of the body weight. Blood consists of several cells floating in a straw-coloured liquid called plasma. These cells (Fig. 10.1) are (a) red blood cells (RBCs), (b) white blood cells (WBCs), and (c) blood platelets.

Know Your Scientist
Karl Landsteiner, an Australian biologist, physician and immunologist, discovered the major blood groups in humans. He discovered these blood types between 1900 and 1901 while he was studying why blood transfusions sometimes saved a patient, while sometimes it was the reason for the patient's death. It was an important milestone achieved at the University of Vienna. For this discovery, he even received a Nobel prize in Physiology (or Medicine) in 1930.

C-6.1

Red blood cells are flat and disc-like in shape with a depression in the centre (Fig. 10.1). They contain the red pigment, haemoglobin. This pigment combines with oxygen to form oxyhaemoglobin that helps in transporting oxygen to all parts of the body. White blood cells are larger than RBCs but do not have haemoglobin. They move actively and protect the body against disease-causing microorganisms by destroying them. Whenever you have a cut on your body, blood comes out. The bleeding is stopped after sometime by the blood platelets present in the blood. Blood platelets are colourless, irregularly shaped, and much smaller than the RBCs.

Blood Vessels

Blood flows through our body in a complex system of tubes called blood vessels. There are three types of blood vessels: arteries, capillaries, and veins. Capillaries are the thinnest of the blood vessels and connect arteries to veins. Differences between arteries and veins are listed in Table 10.1.

Table 10.1 Differences between arteries and veins

Arteries Veins
1. They carry blood away from the heart. 1. They carry blood to the heart.
2. They are thick-walled tubes. 2. They have thinner walls.
3. They are deeply placed under the skin, and blood moves under high pressure. 3. They are superficially placed and blood does not move under pressure.
4. There are no valves inside arteries. 4. There are a series of valves present in the veins. These prevent the blood from flowing back to the organs.

The Heart

The heart (Fig. 10.3) is a four-chambered muscular organ that pumps blood into the blood vessels. The pressure that this pumping generates is enough for the blood vessels to carry this blood to all parts of the body. The heart is able to do so by the rhythmic contractions and relaxations of its muscles. These are known as the heart beats. A normal heart beats about 60 to 80 times per minute. We can hear our heart beat with the help of an instrument called a stethoscope. Blood pressure is measured by using an instrument called a sphygmomanometer.

As the heart beats and forces blood through our body, we feel a throbbing sensation at any point where an artery comes close to the surface of our skin, such as the wrist, neck, or upper arm. This throbbing sensation is called the pulse. Counting your pulse rate is a simple way to estimate how fast your heart is beating.

TRANSPORT IN PLANTS

There are different kinds of plants such as herbs, shrubs, and trees. Plants need to transport water from the roots to the leaves. Then the food manufactured in the leaves is transported to different parts of the plant. Though the complexity may vary, the mode of transport in all types of plants is essentially the same.

A tissue called xylem acts as a type of pipeline, and takes water and minerals from the roots up the stem, and to the leaves. xylem, Parenchyma, fibre, tracheids, vessels.

Food in plants is manufactured by the leaves. Once the food is prepared, it needs to be sent to all parts of the plant. The process by which this takes place is called translocation. Special types of tissues called phloem act as pipelines, and carry the prepared food to all parts of the plant.

Transpiration

Absorption of water and minerals is a continuous process. Water is continuously being lost from the leaves by a process called transpiration (Fig. 10.4). The losing of water from the plant in the form of water vapour is called transpiration. Transpiration mainly occurs through the stomata present on the underside of the leaves. The opening and closing of stomata controls the amount of water lost during transpiration.

A number of factors affect the rate of transpiration in plants:

  • The condition of air The warmer the air, higher is the rate of transpiration.

  • The number of stomata found on the leaves More the number of stomata, higher is the transpiration.
  • The size of the leaves The larger the leaf, faster is the transpiration.

Importance of transpiration

Transpiration is very important for plants, and has several advantages:
change of gases CO2 in & O2 out + transpiration

  • Exchange of gases Transpiration helps in the opening of stomata. This helps in gaseous exchange, and the plant is able to easily absorb carbon dioxide from the air, which is needed for photosynthesis.
  • Cooling effect Evaporation of water vapour given out during transpiration brings down the surface temperature of the plant, thereby cooling it. Transpiration, thus, provides a significant cooling effect that also helps to prevent damage to plant cells due to overheating.
  • Effect on mineral and water transport Transpiration creates a low pressure and a pulling force that pulls up the water and minerals. This is similar to what happens when we drink juice or water through a straw. The water and minerals are then transported to different parts of the plant.

EXCRETORY SYSTEM

There are a large number of chemical reactions that take place inside a living body. As a result, a variety of waste products are constantly being generated. The living body needs to remove these wastes. The process of removal of wastes from the cells of living organisms is called excretion. The organ system responsible for the removal of wastes is called the excretory system.

Excretion in Animals

Animal wastes mainly include carbon dioxide (that is formed during respiration), wastes of the digestive system, and other wastes such as urea and uric acid. Animals such as Amoeba, Paramecium, Hydra, etc., do not have a well-developed excretory system. They get rid of all kinds of body wastes by direct diffusion through the cell membranes. Animals such as earthworms, insects, and leeches have tubular structures that help to get rid of body wastes.

Excretion in Humans

The human body excretes wastes in the following ways:
Through exhaled air Carbon dioxide, formed after respiration, is sent out of the body with the exhaled air.
Through sweat Our skin also acts as an excretory organ. It has small outer openings called sweat pores. Our body gets rid of a small quantity of urea, salts, and water through

sweat secreted by the sweat glands present under the skin. Sweating is important because it helps in regulating body temperature. It helps to keep the body cool by losing body heat, as sweat evaporates off the skin.

Through the kidney A number of nitrogenous wastes such as urea and uric acid are formed inside our body. Urea is a result of the combination of carbon dioxide and ammonia. It is excreted from the body in the form of a pale yellowish-coloured liquid called urine. Urine contains almost 95% water and the remaining 5% consists of urea, uric acid, and minerals. Formation of urine takes place inside the kidneys.

The human excretory system (Fig. 10.5) comprises (a) a pair of reddish, bean-shaped organs called kidneys, (b) two tubes called ureters, (c) a urinary bladder that stores urine, and (d) a urethra that removes the urine. Waste substances present in the blood are removed with the help of the kidney. When blood enters the kidney, the useful substances are absorbed/filtered back into the blood. Waste substances get dissolved in water and are removed as urine. This absorption/filtration is carried out by the functional unit of the kidneys called nephrons. Urine thus formed is collected via the ureters into the urinary bladder. Then, urine is passed out from the body through the opening at the end of the urethra.

Dialysis
Kidneys work in pairs, but they are so efficient that even if one gets damaged, the person can still lead a healthy life. In case both the kidneys are damaged, a person can survive with the help of (a) regular dialysis or (b) a kidney transplant.

Dialysis is a method of removing toxic substances from the blood with the help of a machine when the kidneys are unable to do so. The dialysis machine is also called an artificial kidney. Blood from an artery of the arm is led through the dialysis machine, where urea and excess salts are removed. The blood is then led back to the patient's body through a vein. In a kidney transplant, at least one of the diseased kidneys is replaced with a healthy kidney from a donor.

Excretion in Plants

The main products excreted by plants are:

Carbon dioxide and oxygen Carbon dioxide is formed as a result of respiration. Oxygen is formed as a result of photosynthesis.

Excess water It is excreted through stomata and the process is called transpiration.

Excessive salts If there is a high level of salts present in water or in the soil, these usually get deposited in plants as crystals.

Gums, resins, and latex Many older plants get rid of their toxic wastes in the form of gum, resins, and latex. We use these secretions to manufacture paints, varnishes, rubber, and adhesives.
