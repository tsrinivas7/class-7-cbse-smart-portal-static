Chapter 11

Reproduction in Plants

Chapter Summary

Introduction

Reproduction — The process of producing young ones from their parents is known as reproduction.
Asexual reproduction — The formation of new plants from the cells of a single parent is called asexual reproduction.
Vegetative reproduction — A type of asexual reproduction in which a cell, tissue, or part of an organ of a plant develops into a new organism.
Sexual reproduction — Takes place by the formation of seeds. It involves two parents and the fusion of male and female reproductive cells called gametes to form a single cell called the zygote.
Germination — The process by which a seed becomes a new plant.

  • Most plants reproduce through asexual, vegetative, and sexual means.
  • Flowerless plants use different means to reproduce compared to flowering plants.
  • Asexual reproduction involves simple division of the plant body or formation of spores.
  • Sexual reproduction requires the fusion of male and female gametes.
  • Seeds need air, water, and warmth to become a new plant.

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction — The formation of new plants from the cells of a single parent is called asexual reproduction.
Budding — A small bulb-like cellular outgrowth, called bud, is formed from the cell which keeps on increasing in size and forms an independent organism which separates from the parent.
Fragmentation — A process where the adult organism just breaks up into two or more pieces called fragments, each of which grows up to become a new plant.
Spore formation — Some plants multiply asexually through spores which are microscopic single-celled or several-celled reproductive bodies mostly spherical in shape.
Spore — Microscopic, single-celled, or several-celled reproductive bodies protected by a thick wall.

  • Asexual reproduction is the simplest form of reproduction found in plants.
  • It occurs under favourable conditions and involves only one parent.
  • Common forms include budding, fragmentation, and spore formation.
  • Yeasts reproduce asexually by budding.
  • Algae such as Spirogyra reproduce by fragmentation.
  • Ferns and mosses multiply with the help of spores.

Budding

Budding — In this process, a small bulb-like cellular outgrowth, called bud, is formed from the cell.

  • Microscopic organisms such as yeasts reproduce asexually by budding.
  • The bud increases in size and eventually separates from the parent.
  • This results in two independent yeast cells.

Fragmentation

Fragmentation — In this process, the adult organism just breaks up into two or more pieces called fragments.

  • It is a very common form of asexual reproduction found in algae.
  • Examples include Spirogyra (pond silk) and Fucus.
  • Each fragment grows up to become a new plant.

Spore formation

Spore formation — Some plants such as ferns and mosses multiply asexually through spores.
Spore — Microscopic single-celled or several-celled reproductive bodies that are mostly spherical in shape.

  • Spores are protected by a thick wall when conditions are unfavourable.
  • Once conditions for germination are favourable, spores burst out of the thick wall.
  • They start multiplying and grow into new plants.
  • Ferns and mosses are lower plants that do not have roots, flowers, or seeds.

Vegetative Reproduction

Vegetative reproduction — In some plants, vegetative parts such as root, stem, and leaf can be used to produce new plants.

  • This type of reproduction can be done by natural as well as artificial methods.
  • It allows plants to reproduce without the involvement of human beings in natural methods.
  • New plants resemble the parent plant exactly.

Natural Methods

Natural Methods — Most plants reproduce naturally without the involvement of human beings.

  • Plants do so with the help of stems, roots, and leaves.
  • Tuberous roots of sweet potato, Dahlia, and Asparagus store food for multiplication.
  • Horizontal stems growing parallel to the ground allow new roots to sprout from nodes.
Roots

Roots — Tuberous roots of sweet potato, Dahlia, and Asparagus can be set aside for multiplication.

  • These roots have food stored in them.
  • They are used to produce new plants in the next season.
Stems

Stems — Stems are the most common parts involved in vegetative propagation.

  • Horizontal stems grow parallel to the ground (e.g., Oxalis, grass).
  • New roots sprout from the nodes and new shoots grow upwards.
  • Underground stems are mostly swollen because they have food stored in them.
  • Types of underground stems include rhizomes, bulbs, tubers, and corms.

Rhizomes — They have buds from which outgrowths are produced which give rise to new plants.
Bulbs — Very short underground stems encased in thickened fleshy bulb scales which serve as sites of food storage.
Tubers — They have buds in the eyes, which give rise to new plants.
Corms — Lots of rhizomes joined together which develop into new plants.

  • Rhizomes are seen in ginger, turmeric, and banana.
  • Bulbs are seen in onions.
  • Tubers are seen in potatoes.
  • Corms are seen in Gladiolus and Colocasia.
Leaves

Leaves — Leaves of a number of plants, such as Bryophyllum, develop small buds on their margin.
Adventitious buds — Small buds that develop on the margin of leaves.

  • These buds grow into new plants when the leaf falls from the parent plant.
  • They come in contact with soil to grow.

Artificial Methods

Artificial Methods — Plants can also be produced vegetatively by artificial means.

  • These methods include cutting, grafting, layering, and tissue culture.
  • They are used to multiply plants quickly and preserve characteristics.
Cutting

Cutting — This method involves cutting off part of a stem and placing it in moist soil.

  • After some time, these stems develop roots at the base.
  • They grow into a new plant.
  • Generally used for sugar cane, rose, Bougainvillea, and Hibiscus.
Grafting

Grafting — A method used in ornamental and fruit plants to develop new varieties called hybrid varieties.
Scion — A bud, or a cutting that has several buds called the scion, of one plant is placed over the cut stem.
Stock — The cut stem with roots of another plant.

  • The scion and the stock are firmly tied together.
  • The stock supplies water and minerals to the scion.
  • New cells develop in the area where they are joined.
  • Common in rose, mango, guava, and other fruit plants.
Layering

Layering — A method most commonly used in grapes, jasmine, and Bougainvillea.

  • A young branch is lowered down and bent towards the ground.
  • It is covered by moist soil forming a layer.
  • Roots arise from the branch and grow downwards.
  • The branch is cut off from the parent plant to grow into a new plant.
Tissue Culture

Tissue culture — A method used for large-scale plant multiplication to develop better variety of crops.
Explant — A small piece of tissue (called the explant) is cut off from the growing tips of plants.
Callus — Cell division results in a mass of loosely arranged cells (called callus).

  • Used to conserve endangered and rare breeds of plants.
  • The explant is kept in a medium rich in nutrients.
  • Rooted shoots are shifted to pots to grow into mature plants.
  • Widely used in research work, forestry, and horticulture.

Advantages of Vegetative Reproduction

Advantages of Vegetative Reproduction — The advantages of vegetative reproduction are listed below.

  • It is a faster and more certain method of reproduction.
  • New plants resemble the parent plant exactly.
  • It helps in conserving characteristic features of the parent plant.
  • Plants that do not flower or do not have seeds can reproduce by this method.

Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction — The formation of new plants through the fusion of male and female gametes is called sexual reproduction.
Flowers — Flowers are the reproductive organs of a plant.
Bisexual flowers — Flowers that contain both male and female reproductive cells.
Unisexual flowers — Flowers that produce either male or female cells.
Stamens — Male part of a flower which have anthers that contain pollen grains.
Pistil — Female part of a flower that contains the ovary with the ovules.
Sepals — One of the four parts of a complete flower.
Petals — One of the four parts of a complete flower.
Anther — Part of the stamen that contains pollen grains.
Filament — Part of the stamen.
Stigma — Part of the pistil.
Style — Part of the pistil.
Ovary — Part of the pistil that produces female cells (gametes).
Ovule — Found inside the ovary.
Gametes — Male and female reproductive cells.
Zygote — The result of the fusion of male and female gametes.

  • Sexual reproduction is carried out only in flowering plants.
  • A complete flower has all the four parts: sepals, petals, stamens, and pistil.
  • Pollination is the first step in the process of seed formation.
  • After fertilization, the flower becomes a fruit.
  • Fruits may have one or more seeds inside them.

Pollination

Pollination — The transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma is known as pollination.
Self-pollination — The pollen grains are transferred from the anther to the stigma of the same flower or on another flower of the same plant.
Cross-pollination — The pollen grains are transferred from the anther of one flower to the stigma of another flower of a different plant of the same kind.

  • Pollination needs to happen for fertilization to take place.
  • The male gamete must reach the female gamete in the ovary.
  • There are two types: self-pollination and cross-pollination.

Agents of Pollination

Agents of Pollination — The most common agents of pollination are wind, water, and insects.
Wind — Blows away pollen grains from the anthers of one flower to the stigma of another flower.
Water — Pollination in aquatic plants is usually carried out by water.
Insects — Many insects visit flowers for nectar and carry pollen grains.
Forage — To search for food.

  • Wind-pollinated flowers are small, not brightly coloured, and do not produce nectar.
  • Examples include wheat, rice, and maize.
  • Water-pollinated flowers release pollen grains into the water (e.g., Vallisneria).
  • Insect-pollinated flowers have a sweet smell, brightly coloured petals, and nectar.
  • Examples include rose, sunflower, and gulmohar.

Fertilization

Fertilization — The process of fusion of male and female gametes is called fertilization.
Zygote — A zygote is formed after fertilization takes place.

  • After successful pollination, the stigma secretes nutrients.
  • The pollen grain absorbs nutrients and starts growing a thin tube.
  • The pollen tube reaches the ovule inside the ovary.
  • The male gamete fuses with the female gamete (eggs) present there.

Fruit and Seed Formation

Fruit and Seed Formation — After fertilization, the ovary enlarges to form the fruit.
Fruit — The seed-bearing part or the ripened ovary of the flowering plant.
Seed — The ripened ovule which contains an embryo and is covered by a protective coat.
Germination — The process by which the embryo in the seed becomes active and begins to grow into a new plant.

  • The wall of the ovary becomes the fruit wall.
  • The ovules become the seeds.
  • Petals, sepals, and other parts of the flower usually dry up and fall off.
  • When a seed has matured and suitable conditions of air, water, and temperature are available, it germinates.

Seed Dispersal

Seed Dispersal — The process by which seeds or fruits are scattered is called dispersal.
Dispersal by Explosion — In the simplest way, seeds are automatically set free by opening of the fruit.
Dispersal by Wind — Some seeds have radiating threads or wings that form a parachute.
Dispersal by Water — Some seeds have a spongy outer coat to help them float on water.
Dispersal by Animals — Most fleshy and brightly coloured fruits are eaten by birds and mammals.

  • Scattering of seeds helps in reducing competition for survival.
  • Plants get distributed to distant places and thus enter new habitats.
  • Very often, both fruits and seeds together form the dispersal unit.
  • Pea plants, castor, and balsam disperse by explosion.
  • Dandelion seeds, drumstick, and maple disperse by wind.
  • Coconuts disperse by water.
  • Birds and mammals eat fruits and disperse seeds through digestion or sticking to fur.

Fertilization — The process of fusion of male and female gametes is called fertilization.

  • Reproduction ensures the continuation of plant species.
  • Asexual reproduction involves one parent while sexual involves two.
  • Spores are resistant structures for reproduction in lower plants.

REPRODUCTION IN PLANTS

The process of producing young ones from their parents is known as reproduction. Plants reproduce through asexual, vegetative, and sexual means.

Asexual reproduction involves either the simple division of the plant body into two or more parts or the formation of spores. A new plant body is formed from a single parent. This happens under favourable conditions. Vegetative reproduction is also a type of asexual reproduction in which a cell, tissue, or part of an organ of a plant develops into a new organism.

Sexual reproduction takes place by the formation of seeds. It involves two parents and the fusion of male and female reproductive cells called gametes to form a single cell called the zygote. Let us understand these processes in detail.

ASEXUAL REPRODUCTION

The formation of new plants from the cells of a single parent is called asexual reproduction. Asexual reproduction is the simplest form of reproduction found in plants. It is of three common forms: budding, fragmentation, and spore formation.

Budding Microscopic organisms, such as yeasts, reproduce asexually by budding (Fig. 11.1). In this process, a small bulb-like cellular outgrowth, called bud, is formed from the cell. This bud keeps on increasing in size and forms an independent organism which separates from the parent.

Fragmentation

It is a very common form of asexual reproduction found in algae. Some algae, such as Spirogyra (pond silk), Fucus, etc., are the slimy, green, and small plants seen floating on the surface of ponds or in dirty drains. In this process, the adult organism just breaks up into two or more pieces called fragments (Fig. 11.2). Each of these fragments grows up to become a new plant.

Spore formation

Some plants such as ferns and mosses multiply asexually through spores (Fig. 11.3). Spores are microscopic single-celled or several-celled reproductive bodies that are mostly spherical in shape. They are protected by a thick wall when conditions (such as humidity and temperature) are unfavourable. Once the conditions for germination are favourable, these spores burst out of the thick wall, start multiplying, and grow into new plants.

VEGETATIVE REPRODUCTION

In some plants, vegetative parts such as root, stem, and leaf can be used to produce new plants. This type of reproduction, known as vegetative reproduction, can be done by natural as well as artificial methods.

Natural Methods

Most plants reproduce naturally without the involvement of human beings. They do so with the help of stems, roots, and leaves.

Roots

Tuberous roots of sweet potato [Fig. 11.4 (a)], Dahlia [Fig. 11.4 (b)], and Asparagus can be set aside for multiplication in the next season. These roots have food stored in them.

Stems

Stems are the most common parts involved in vegetative propagation.

Vegetative propagation through stems is of the following types:

Plants such as Oxalis and grass (Fig 11.5) have horizontal stems growing parallel to the ground, almost touching it. New roots sprout from the nodes (slightly swollen areas occurring at regular intervals on a stem or branch). New shoots also grow upwards forming new plants at frequent intervals.

[IMAGE_START] Fig. 11.5 Grass. Illustration of grass plants growing in soil. A label points to a "Horizontal stem" running underground beneath the surface, connecting the root systems. Roots are shown extending downwards. [IMAGE_END]

In plants such as mint and Chrysanthemum, horizontal stems arise from the base of the erect shoot, grow horizontally in the soil, and then come out to form new aerial shoots (Fig. 11.6). These shoots become independent plants when stems break away from the parent plants.

[IMAGE_START] Fig. 11.6 Chrysanthemum. Illustration of a plant with underground stems and roots. Green leafy shoots emerge from the soil. An arrow points to one of these emerging shoots labeled "New aerial shoots". [IMAGE_END]

Some plants have arched stems which cross over small obstacles and develop small plantlets at their nodes, e.g., Vallisneria, wild strawberry.

Some plants have stems that grow underground. They are mostly swollen because they have food stored in them. This food is used by the new plants to grow. The different types of underground stems are: rhizomes, bulbs, tubers, and corms.

Rhizomes They have buds from which outgrowths are produced which give rise to new plants. This is seen in ginger (Fig. 11.7), turmeric, and banana.

[IMAGE_START] Fig. 11.7 Rhizome of ginger. Illustration of a ginger rhizome underground. It is knobby and brownish. A green shoot with leaves grows upwards. An arrow points to a small bump on the rhizome labeled "Bud". [IMAGE_END]

Bulbs They are very short underground stems encased in thickened fleshy bulb scales (which are modified leaves). The scales serve as sites of food storage. This is seen in onions (Fig. 11.8). In the spring, when a new plant shoots up from the centre of the scale cluster, it gets its food from the scales.

[IMAGE_START] Fig. 11.8 Bulb of onion. Illustration of a red onion bulb with roots at the bottom and long green stalks growing out of the top. An arrow points to the bulb layers labeled "Bulb". [IMAGE_END]

Tubers They have buds in the eyes, which give rise to new plants, e.g., potato (Fig. 11.9).

[IMAGE_START] Fig. 11.9 Tuber of potato. Illustration of a potato tuber. Small indentations are pointed out with arrows labeled "Eye". A small sprout is labeled "Bud". [IMAGE_END]

Corms They are lots of rhizomes joined together which develop into new plants, e.g., Gladiolus (Fig. 11.10) and Colocasia.

Artificial Methods

Plants can also be produced vegetatively by artificial means. These methods include cutting, grafting, layering, and tissue culture.

Cutting

Grafting

This is a very common method used in ornamental and fruit plants to develop new varieties called hybrid varieties. It involves the following steps:

A bud, or a cutting that has several buds called the scion, of one plant is placed over the cut stem with roots of another plant called the stock (Fig. 11.13).
The scion and the stock are then firmly tied together.
The stock supplies water and minerals to the scion. In due course, new cells develop in the area where they are joined and a new variety of plant develops. This is common in plants such as the rose, mango, guava, and many other fruit plants.

This is a method most commonly used in grapes, jasmine, and Bougainvillea.

In this process, a young branch is lowered down and bent towards the ground and covered by moist soil forming a layer.

Tissue Culture

This is a method used for large-scale plant multiplication to develop better variety of crops, which are disease-free and have a higher yield, and to conserve endangered and rare breeds of plants. This method is widely used in research work, forestry, and horticulture. Tissue culture involves the following steps (Fig. 11.15).

Advantages of Vegetative Reproduction

The advantages of vegetative reproduction are listed below:

It is a faster and more certain method of reproduction. Sometimes the seed may not germinate due to unfavourable conditions. But in this case, a new plant directly grows from a part of the parent plant.

New plants resemble the parent plant exactly. Thus, it helps in conserving characteristic features of the parent plant.

Plants that do not flower or do not have seeds can reproduce by this method.

SEXUAL REPRODUCTION

The formation of new plants through the fusion of male and female gametes is called sexual reproduction. Sexual reproduction is carried out only in flowering plants. Flowers are the reproductive organs of a plant. Usually a flower contains both male and female reproductive cells. Such flowers are also called complete or bisexual flowers. When flowers produce either male or female cells, they are called incomplete or unisexual flowers. Such types of flowers are found in corn and papaya plants. A complete flower has all the four parts: sepals, petals, stamens, and pistil.

Agents of Pollination

The most common agents of pollination are wind, water, and insects.

Wind Wind blows away pollen grains from the anthers of one flower to the stigma of another flower. Such flowers are small, not brightly coloured, and do not produce nectar (a sweet liquid produced by flowers). Pollen grains are very light and produced in large numbers so they are easily blown away by the wind. This type of pollination occurs in wheat, rice, and maize.

Water Pollination in aquatic plants such as sea grass and Vallisneria (Fig. 11.19) is usually carried out by water. Water-pollinated flowers release their pollen grains into the water, and are slowly carried to other flowers by water currents.

When these insects visit another flower, the pollen grains fall on the stigma. Insect-pollinated flowers usually have a sweet smell, brightly coloured petals, and are rich in nectar, for example, rose, sunflower, and gulmohar. Fertilization

After successful pollination, the stigma secretes nutrients.

The pollen grain absorbs these nutrients and starts growing.
A thin tube grows out of the pollen grain.
The pollen tube keeps growing till it reaches the ovule inside the ovary and enters it.
The male gamete is carried inside the pollen tube. Once the pollen tube reaches the ovule, the male gamete fuses with the female gamete (eggs) present there. The process of fusion of male and female gametes is called fertilization (Fig. 11.21).
A zygote is formed after fertilization takes place.

SEED DISPERSAL

Have you ever wondered how the same kinds of plants are found at different places? If all plants were to grow at the same place, there would be huge competition for survival. They would not be able to get enough space, light, and water to grow. Scattering of seeds or fruits helps in reducing this competition and also plants get distributed to distant places and thus, enter new habitats. The process by which seeds or fruits are scattered is called dispersal.

The seed, enclosed in the fruit, needs to separate from its parent plant before it can develop into a new plant. Fruits are adapted in different ways to help in dispersal. But very often, both fruits and seeds together form the dispersal unit. Dispersal takes place by several mechanisms, including wind, water, and a variety of animals.

Dispersal by Explosion

In the simplest way, seeds are automatically set free by opening of the fruit. An explosive mechanism bursts the fruit open to release the seeds. For example, pea plants, castor, and balsam.

Dispersal by Wind

Some seeds have radiating threads or wings that form a parachute so that they can be carried long distances by even the gentlest breeze. For example, Dandelion seeds, drumstick, and maple. See Figure 11.24.

Dispersal by Water

Some seeds have a spongy outer coat to help them float on water. They are carried great distances by ocean currents and germinate after they wash up on beaches. For example, coconuts.

Dispersal by Animals

Most fleshy and brightly coloured fruits are eaten by birds and mammals. These fruits appear tasty and are highly nutritious for these animals, giving them a reason to forage for the fruits repeatedly. Seeds of these fruits pass through the digestive tract of animals and get dispersed.

Key Words

Term Definition
Reproduction The process of producing young ones from their parents is known as reproduction.
Asexual reproduction The formation of new plants from the cells of a single parent is called asexual reproduction.
Spore Microscopic, single-celled, or several-celled reproductive bodies protected by a thick wall is called spore.
Sexual reproduction The formation of new plants through the fusion of male and female gametes is called sexual reproduction.
Stamen Male part of a flower which have anthers that contain pollen grains is called stamen.
Pistil Female part of a flower that contains the ovary with the ovules is called a pistil.
Self-pollination The process where pollen grains are transferred to the stigma of the same flower is called self-pollination.
Cross-pollination The process where pollen grains are transferred from the anther of one flower to the stigma of another flower of the same kind is called cross-pollination.
Fertilization The process of fusion of male and female gametes is called fertilization.
Zygote The result of the fusion of male and female gametes is called zygote.

C-7.1

Summary

  • Reproduction in plants is of three types: asexual, vegetative, and sexual.
  • Asexual reproduction is the process in which new plants are produced by involving only one parent.
  • Flowerless plants such as algae reproduce by fragmentation; yeasts reproduce by budding, while moulds and mosses reproduce through spores.
  • Vegetatively, plants reproduce with the help of roots, bulbs, rhizomes, tubers, corms, and adventitious buds.
  • There are many artificial methods by which plants multiply. These include cutting, grafting, and layering.
  • In sexual reproduction, the male and female organs produce male and female gametes, whose fusion results in a zygote. This zygote develops into a new individual.
  • Sexual reproduction is carried out only in flowering plants.
  • After fertilization of the egg, the ovary becomes a fruit and ovules become seeds.
  • Seed dispersal is necessary to separate the seed from the parent plant so that the seed can grow into a new plant.
