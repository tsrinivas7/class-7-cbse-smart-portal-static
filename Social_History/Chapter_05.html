# Key Question
Who are the tribals?

## TRIBAL SOCIETIES
Since the ancient times, the tribes have formed a distinct group in Indian society. The tribes lived in clans led by the tribal chiefs. Each tribe had its own laws, rules and traditions and survived by hunting, food gathering and by doing shifting cultivation. Unlike the Hindu society, tribes had no caste system. There was socio-economic equality in the community. A tribal group owned land, cattle and other movable properties collectively. Thus, tribal communities were based on sharing and mutual dependence.

## Interaction between the Tribal Groups and Indian Society
In the medieval period, many of the tribal groups were absorbed into the rest of the Indian society. As the medieval kingdoms expanded, great rulers took over the territories of many tribes. The tribal chiefs were generally given the status of Kshatriyas while the ordinary tribals were given the status of Shudras.

### IMPORTANT TERMS
- distinct: separate, different
- mutual: something held in common
- resistance: use of force or violence to oppose someone

Under the Mughal rule, for example—Khokhars and Gakhars, who were tribes of Punjab, were absorbed into the Indian society. Their chief was given the administrative posts while the rest of the tribesmen became farmers. Khokhars became powerful during 13th and 14th centuries. Afterwards, Gakhars rose to power and their chief Kamal Khan Gakhar was made a mansabdar by Akbar.

But, all the tribal groups did not easily agree to become part of the Hindu society. Often they resisted and fought back. Being independent-minded, many of the tribal groups resisted their absorption into the Hindu society.

One example of such resistance was by the Cheros, the forest tribes of eastern Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand and western Bihar. The Cheros fought with the Mughals and built forts to strengthen their resistance. Finally, the Cheros were defeated by the Mughal general Raja Man Singh. But, the Cheros raised their banner of resistance once again and were again defeated by the forces of Emperor Aurangzeb.

The regions of Multan and Sind were dominated by Langahs and Arghuns before they were suppressed by Mughals. Koragas, Maravars and several others were tribal groups in south. Vast forest areas of Chhattisgarh, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh and Andhra Pradesh were occupied by the Gonds. Nagas and Ahoms dominated the northeastern part of the subcontinent.

Kolis lived in Maharashtra, Karnataka and Gujarat regions. Bhils were an important and large tribal group of western and central India.

**A RELATIONSHIP BASED ON ECONOMIC EXCHANGES**

Most of the tribal groups were pastoral nomads. They moved from one place to another with their cattle. They sold their animal products such as milk, ghee and wool for grains, utensils and textiles, etc. produced by the settled communities—farmers and craftsmen.

**Insight:** Most of them practised 'shifting cultivation' or jhoom cultivation. The tribals moved to new lands when the cultivated land started losing fertility.

One such nomadic tribal group, involved in these commercial exchanges were the Banjaras. They bought grains and vegetables from the farmers and brought them to the cities and towns to be sold in the urban markets. The Banjaras moved along with the caravans, known as tanda, in which they carried their goods. Their animals also moved with them. Thus, the Banjaras provided a very important service to the state and society by transporting food supplies from the villages to the cities. They transported food grains to the large mughal armies during military campaigns.

**Insight:** It is significant that the Khalji Sultan Ala-ud-din Khalji made the Banjaras transport grains from the rural areas to the grain markets of Delhi. This is also mentioned in the memoirs of Jahangir.

**Key Question**

Why did Ala-ud-din Khalji use the Banjaras to transport grains from the rural areas to Delhi markets?

There were several itinerant groups such as craftspersons, pedlars and entertainers who moved from place to place practising their occupations. They often visited the same places year after year. As economy and society became more complex, people with new skills were incorporated into the Hindu society. Many tribes became part of the Hindu caste-based society and were given the status of Jatis.

Within the Kshatriya caste, Rajputs became prominent by the 11th and 12th centuries.

Many Rajputs too, had originally belonged to tribal communities.

Generally, tribal groups belonging to ruling families became Rajputs. Otherwise, a majority of the tribes joined the Jatis which belonged to the lower caste hierarchy.

On the other hand, many tribal groups, especially in the Punjab, Sindh and northwest frontier region, were converted to Islam because there was no caste system in Islam and it was free from caste hierarchy.

CASE STUDY — THE GONDS AND THE AHOMS

The Gond Tribes — Assertion of Tribal Independence

The Gonds were a tribal group which lived in the forests of Central India and Chhattisgarh. The Gonds became rich and powerful because the region in which they lived was rich in precious metals and forest products. The Gonds were especially skilled in capturing and training elephants. As a result, the Gonds set up a large Gond kingdom in the 15th century named Garh-Katanga that had 70,000 villages as per Akbar's biography.

Gond tribe was divided into several clans, headed by its particular rajas or rais. These smaller clans formed new kingdoms which were divided in garhs. Each garh divided into chaurasi (84 villages) and further into barhots (12 villages) each.

The Gonds improved their social status by marrying into Rajput families. Aman Das, a Gond King, assured the title of Sangram Shah. Though the Gonds successfully resisted invasions by the neighbouring kingdoms, they were finally defeated in 1565 CE by the powerful Mughal army. It was a tribal queen, Rani Durgawati who put up a stubborn resistance to the Mughal forces that attacked Garh-Katanga.

She was the daughter of Salbahan, a Rajput King of Mahoba. She got married to Dalpat, son of Sangram Shah. As Dalpat died early, she began ruling on behalf of her five-year-old son, Bir Narain. She led the Gonds in the battle, and when she was defeated, she preferred to die rather than surrender to her enemies. Mughals annexed a part of her kingdom and gave rest to an uncle of Bir Narain named Chandra Shah. The example of Gond kingdom showed that if resources were mobilised properly and efficiently, the tribes too could assert their independence.

The Ahoms — Assertion of Independence in North Eastern India

The Ahoms—a tribal group from North eastern India—controlled large parts of the Brahmaputra Valley. They migrated to the Brahmaputra region

from Burma or Myanmar in the 13th century CE. The Ahoms established the Ahom Kingdom (1228-1826 CE) in parts of present-day Assam and ruled it for nearly 600 years. The Ahoms fought with the rulers of Bengal for the control over the Brahmaputra Valley. They suppressed the old political system of landlords known as bhuiyans. They even resisted the Mughals and their military entered into Mughal territory.

population checks were conducted in villages which made shifting of people from heavily populated to less populated areas. This divided the Ahom society.

The tribal characteristics of the Ahoms can be seen from the way their society was divided into clans or Khels. The clan distributed land among the peasants. All decisions related to the community were taken by the clan.

Ahoms gradually adopted the Vaishnav faith that prevailed in the Brahmaputra Valley. The Ahom kings granted land to the Brahmanas and to the temples. But at the same time, the Ahoms continued to worship their own tribal Gods. Thus, the Ahoms kept a harmonious balance between their newly adopted religious beliefs and their ancestral religion.

They were also great patrons of cultural activities. They awarded grants of land to poets and scholars. Important literary works in Sanskrit were translated into local languages. Ahoms encouraged writing of historical works. One example is buranji's initially written in Ahom language and then in Assamese.

The example of the Ahoms also proved that the tribal power could also become strong enough to assert their identity.
