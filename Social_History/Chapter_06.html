## The medieval period saw the emergence and blending of many cultures. This led to the rise of various new religious ideas. With the arrival of the Turks, the Sufi Movement came to India. Sufi were Muslim mystics and laid down rules of behaviour in Shariat. It influenced both the Hindus and the Muslims. On the other hand, the Bhakti, which spoke of the union of an individual with God also appeared in India and advocated their ideas in a sacred book Bhagavad Gita.

## BHAKTI MOVEMENT IN SOUTH INDIA

The Bhakti Movement was started in South India in the 7th century by the Alvar, saints also called Vaishnavites who worshipped Lord Vishnu and the Nayanar saints, also called Shaivites who worshipped Lord Shiva. These include saints from all castes even those who were considered untouchables such as Panars and Pulaiyar. They believed that God can be reached only through true love, devotion and complete self-surrender. The Bhakti Movement rejected the caste system.

They carried their message of love and personal devotion to God to the various parts of South India in local languages—Tamil and Telugu.

The Alvars and Nayanars, both reached out to people of all castes and classes including artisans, cultivators and even the people who were regarded as untouchables like Panars and Pulaiyar. The Alvars and Nayanars also

There were other great religious teachers of
South India. Among them was Shankara (also
known as Shankaracharya), who was born in
8th century in Kerala. He was a devotee of Lord
Shiva and is considered to be great because he
significantly helped in the revival of Hinduism.
He preached the doctrine of oneness of the
individual and the Supreme God which is the
ultimate reality and was formless. He said that
the world is an illusion or maya and to attain
solution one must renounce the world. His
philosophy came to be known as the Advaita
Philosophy. He taught that only by attaining
complete knowledge, one can understand the
true nature of God and the Universe. Shankara
established four mathas or centres for learning
and worship at Badrinath, Puri, Dwarka and
Shringeri in the four directions in
India. These mathas later grew into
important Hindu pilgrimage centres.

Another famous Bhakti saint of South India
was Ramanuja, born in Tamil Nadu in the 11th
century. He preached the message of intense
devotion to Lord Vishnu. According to his
doctrine of Vishishtadvaita, even though God
and individual soul are united, they remained
distinct. Madhavacharya who lived in the 13th
century in Karnataka popularised the worship
of Lord Krishna.

# Bhakti Movement in North India

## Key Question
What did the Bhakti saints teach about the social division?

### Insight
Namdeva was one of the most famous poet saints of the 13th and 14th century CE. He was the composer of hundreds of devotional songs. Namdeva was a tailor, who later became a saint. His poems written in Marathi breathe a spirit of love and devotion to God.

### Insight
In Kashmir, the Bhakti saints worshipped Shiva. One of the most important Bhakti saints of Kashmir was a woman named Lal Ded. In Gujarat, the spirit of Bhakti was preached by Vallabhacharya and Narsi Mehta.

### Another Assamese saint Shankaradeva
Shankaradeva composed poems and plays dedicated to Lord Vishnu. He also set up houses of recitation known as Namghars for people to recite their prayers in the name of Gods. His teachings were based on Bhagavata Purana and Bhagavad Gita. Kirtana-ghosha is one of his major compositions. His devotion was based on supreme surrender to one God Eka Sarana Nama Dharma.

### Ramananda
Ramananda was a pioneer of Bhakti movement in North India. Among his disciples, were Kabir and Ravidas. In fact, both Hindu and Muslim became his disciples.

### Mirabai
Mirabai, a Rajput princess became a disciple of Ravidas, a Bhakti saint who belonged to the untouchable caste. Through her hymns, Mirabai questioned the domination of the upper castes. She was a devotee of Lord Krishna. She expressed her complete love and surrender to Lord Krishna through her bhajans. She composed her hymns and poems in Brajbhasha and Rajasthani language.

### Insight
Mirabai was married to a prince of Mewar in Rajasthan. She refused to follow the traditional role of a wife. Her in-laws tried to poison her. She left the comfort of the palace to become a wandering saint, composing and singing bhakti songs.

THE SUFI MOVEMENT
The counterpart of Bhakti movement
was the Sufi movement in Islam. The

Sufi saints called pirs also believed in reaching
God through love and devotion and in being
kind and compassionate towards all the human
beings. The Sufis were disgusted by the vulgar
display of wealth and loss of moral values
among the rich Muslims. They, like the Bhakti
saints, spoke of a simple society free from
greed, materialism and advocated the theory
of one God.

Like the Bhakti poets, the Sufis also composed
poems and qawwalis expressing their
unconditional love to God and humanity. Many
Sufi orders evolved in India each following
somewhat different tariqa (method).

Gradually, the Sufis were organised into 12
orders or Silsilahs. The chief among them were
the Suhrawardi and Chishti Silsilahs.

**Insight:** The Suhrawardi Silsilah arose
during the period of Delhi Sultanate. Its
founder in India was Sheikh Bahauddin
Zakariya. Punjab, Sindh and Bengal became
three important centres of this Silsilah.

The silsilah was led by a Sufi saint. These
saints lived in their abodes or hospices, called
Khanqahs, along with their disciples. Devotees
of all classes from the ordinary people to the
upper class nobles and even royal people,
regularly visited these Khanqahs. In these
Khanqahs, there were open kitchens or langars.
Khanqahs were open to all. When the pir or the
Sufi saint, who was the head of the Khanqah
died, his grave would become a dargah and a
centre of pilgrimage.

Sufi saints developed elaborate methods of
training using zikr (chanting of the name or
sacred formula) contemplation, sama (singing),
raqs (dancing), breath control, and discussion
of parables.

## CASE STUDY

### Kabir
According to legends, Kabir was the son of a brahmana widow who abandoned him after his birth. He was later brought up in the house of a Muslim weaver. He too became a weaver but while living in Benaras, he came in contact with Hindu and Muslim saints. He rejected all rituals and ceremonies and preached a religion of love which would unite all the people. Kabir's poems were written in the form of spoken Hindi language of the ordinary people. His followers were both Hindus and Muslims who were attracted by Kabir's message of love and brotherhood.

### Kabir's rejection of the caste system, especially the practice of untouchability and his opposition to all kinds of discrimination between human beings—on the basis of caste, religion and wealth—made him very popular among the common people.

Kabir who lived in the 15th century, said that
there is only one God whom he called by
several names, such as Rama, Hari, Govinda,
Allah, Jesus and Sahib. He rejected idol worship,
rituals, and pilgrimages. The mission of Kabir
was to preach a religion of love which could
unite all castes and creeds. The teachings of
Kabir are in the form of short poems called
dohas. His verses called pads or Sakhis are often
sung by bhajan singers and some are preserved
in Panch Vani and Guru Granth Sahib. After his
death, his teachings were written down as Kabir
Vani. Later, Kabir Granthavali and Bijaka were
also composed which contain the ideas of this
great Bhakti saint. Kabir used many languages
to reach out to the common people. Hindi,
Persian, Arabic and Turkish words are found
in his teachings. The followers of Kabir which
included both Hindus and Muslims came to be
known as Kabirpanthis.

## Guru Nanak Dev

Guru Nanak Dev, from whose teachings
the Sikh religion is derived, was born
in the village of Talwandi (now called
Nankana Sahib, in Pakistan) on the
bank of river Ravi on April 15, 1469 CE.
Guru Nanak, also spoke of 'one God' and
believed that God can be reached through
selfless love and devotion. It is said that Guru
Nanak toured all over India and even beyond
to Sri Lanka in the south and
Mecca and Medina in the
west. He established a sacred
space for all his followers
called Dharmsal or
Gurdwara where his
followers worshipped
and ate together in
langar or common
kitchen. Guru Nanak
laid great emphasis
on purity of character
and conduct as the first
condition for approaching
God. Guru Nanak expressed
his ideas through hymns
called Shabad in Punjabi,
the language of the region.
Guru Nanak sung these
hymns while his attendant
Mardana played the
musical instrument Rabab. Like Kabir, he also
rejected idol worship, pilgrimages, rituals as well
as distinctions based on caste, wealth, creed
and gender. Guru Nanak allowed women to
attend his sermons along with men. After the
death of Guru Nanak, his ideas led to the rise
of a new faith called Sikhism. Guru Nanak Dev
was the first of the ten Gurus of Sikhs. Guru
Nanak passed away in 1539 CE but by that
time a large number of people had become
his followers. The nine descendant Gurus after
Guru Nanak are: Guru Angad, Guru Amar Das,
Guru Ram Das, Guru Arjan Dev, Guru Har
Govind, Guru Har Rai, Guru Har Kishan, Guru
Tegh Bahadur and Guru Gobind Singh.
