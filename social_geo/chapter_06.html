Chapter Summary

Human Environment Interactions: The Tropical and Subtropical Region

Introduction

Nature varies across different parts of the world, influencing plant life, animal life, and human lifestyles. While basic human needs are the same, how we meet them depends on our environment. This chapter explores two major regions:
* Tropical Region: The Amazon Basin.
* Subtropical Region: The Ganga-Brahmaputra Basin.

These regions lie between 35°N and 35°S of the Equator, characterized by warm climates and abundant rainfall.

The Amazon Basin

Location and River

  • Location: Lies between 5°N and 17°S latitudes.
  • Countries: Spans across Brazil, Peru, Ecuador, Colombia, and Venezuela.
  • River: The Amazon is the second-longest river in the world and carries the largest volume of water. It originates from the Andes Mountains in Peru and flows eastwards into the Atlantic Ocean. It has over 1,000 tributaries.

Climate

  • Type: Equatorial climate.
  • Conditions: Hot and wet throughout the year.
  • Temperature: Average around 30°C.
  • Rainfall: More than 200 cm annually. It rains almost daily.
  • Humidity: Very high, though nights are cooler. Day and night lengths are nearly equal.

Vegetation

  • Forest Type: Thick evergreen forests known as 'Selvas' (Tropical Rain Forests).
  • Characteristics: Tall trees with thick foliage block sunlight, making the forest floor dark and damp. This promotes the growth of climbers and ferns.
  • Important Trees: Ebony, Mahogany, Rosewood, Rubber, Cinchona, and Balsa (lightest wood).
  • Products: Rubber, nuts, gum, resins, and Quinine (a drug for malaria).

Wildlife

  • Often called a "Gigantic Zoo" due to its richness.
  • Mammals: Monkeys, jaguars, pumas, tapirs, sloths, anteaters.
  • Reptiles & Amphibians: Pythons, anacondas, crocodiles.
  • Others: Variety of birds, insects, and freshwater fish.

People

  • Tribes: Yanomami and Kayapo live here, leading a primitive life.
  • Housing: Small huts made of branches and leaves, built on platforms (stilts) to avoid floods.
  • Occupations: Hunting, fishing, and slash-and-burn agriculture.
  • Crops: Tapioca, sweet potato, pineapple, bananas. Commercial crops include rubber, coffee, cocoa, and nuts.
  • Resources: Rich in minerals like gold, iron ore, copper, manganese, and bauxite.
  • Changes: The Trans-Amazonian highway has improved access, but development is causing rapid deforestation.

The Ganga-Brahmaputra Basin

Location and Rivers

  • Location: South of the Himalayas, between 20°N and 30°N latitudes. Extends about 2,500 km from the Sutlej to the Brahmaputra.
  • Main Rivers:
    • Ganga: Tributaries include Yamuna, Ghaghra, Gomti, Gandak, Kosi, Son, and Damodar.
    • Brahmaputra: Known as Tsangpo in Tibet, Siang/Dihang in Arunachal Pradesh, and Jamuna in Bangladesh.
  • Delta: Both rivers join before falling into the Bay of Bengal, forming the Sunderban, the world's largest and fastest-growing delta. It gets its name from the Sundari tree found there.

Climate

  • Type: Subtropical monsoon climate.
  • Seasons: Hot and wet summers; cool and dry winters.
  • Rainfall: Heavy rainfall brought by South-West Monsoon winds (June to September).
  • Soil: Rivers carry huge amounts of silt, enriching the floodplains of North India.

Topography and Vegetation

  • Topography: Varied. Mountains are steep and inhospitable; plains are fertile and support dense populations.
  • Lower Mountains: Covered with tropical deciduous forests (Teak, Sal, Bamboo).
  • Delta Region: Mangrove forests containing Sundari trees.

Wildlife

  • Plains: Sparse wildlife due to dense human population.
  • Brahmaputra Plains: Elephants, tigers, deer, monkeys, and the one-horned rhinoceros.
  • Delta: Home to the Royal Bengal Tiger, crocodiles, alligators, and diverse aquatic life.

People

  • Population: Mountainous areas are sparsely populated; plains are very densely populated.
  • Occupation: Agriculture is the main job.
  • Crops:
    • Food: Rice, wheat, maize, sorghum.
    • Cash Crops: Sugarcane, cotton, jute, tea.
    • Others: Fruits, vegetables, pulses, oilseeds.
  • Industry: Modern farming techniques are used. Agro-based industries and small-scale cottage industries have developed. The Brahmaputra valley is rich in petroleum deposits.

Long Answer Type Questions

(a) How does the climatic conditions of the Amazon basin affect the vegetation there?
* Answer: The warm and wet climate results in thick evergreen forests called tropical rain forests ('Selvas'). The high rainfall and heat allow trees to grow tall with thick foliage. This canopy prevents sunlight from reaching the ground, creating a dark and damp interior that promotes the growth of climbers and ferns alongside the tall trees.

(b) Why is the Amazon basin called a 'Gigantic Zoo'?
* Answer: It is called a 'Gigantic Zoo' because it is very rich in wildlife. It includes mammals like monkeys, jaguars, pumas, tapirs, sloths, and anteaters. It is also home to a variety of birds, insects, reptiles (pythons, anacondas, crocodiles), and several species of freshwater fish.

(c) 'Topography of the Ganga and Brahmaputra basin is varied and so is the vegetation.' Discuss.
* Answer: The topography varies from mountainous areas to plains and deltas.
* Mountains: Steep and inhospitable, covered with thick tropical deciduous forests (Teak, Sal, Bamboo).
* Plains: Fertile and support dense populations; used for agriculture.
* Delta: Covered with mangrove forests where Sundari trees are found.
Thus, the vegetation changes according to the landform (deciduous in mountains, mangrove in delta, crops in plains).

(d) Distinguish between the agricultural practices of the Ganga-Brahmaputra basin and the Amazon basin.
* Answer:
* Amazon Basin: Uses primitive methods like slash and burn agriculture. Crops include tapioca, sweet potato, bananas, etc. Farm size is small.
* Ganga-Brahmaputra Basin: Uses modern farming techniques replacing primitive methods. Large areas are cultivated for food crops (rice, wheat) and cash crops (sugarcane, cotton, jute, tea).

(e) The Amazon basin receives heavy rainfall everyday. Why is it so? (HOTS)
* Answer: The Amazon basin lies in the equatorial region. Due to the intense heat and high humidity, warm air rises rapidly, cools down, and condenses to form clouds and rain almost daily. This is known as convectional rainfall.

## TROPICAL AND SUBTROPICAL REGIONS

The tropical and subtropical regions extend between 35°N and 35°S of the Equator. The climate within this belt is generally warm with abundant rainfall. To know more about these regions, we shall look into the details of two regions—the Amazon basin which lies between 5°N and 17°S latitudes in the tropical region and the Ganga-Brahmaputra basin which lies in the subtropical zone between 20°N and 30°N latitudes.

## THE AMAZON BASIN

The river Amazon is the second longest river in the world and largest in terms of volume of water carried by it. It originates from the Andes Mountains in Peru and flows eastwards into the Atlantic Ocean. Its vast river basin covers the parts of Brazil, Peru, Ecuador, Colombia and Venezuela. It has more than 1000 tributaries and has the largest basin in the world.

### Climate and Vegetation
The Amazon basin lies in the equatorial region where the climate remains hot and wet all round the year. The average temperature is around 30°C and the annual rainfall is more than 200 cm. It rains here almost daily and the humidity is very high though the nights are comparatively cooler. There is very little difference between the length of day and night.

The warm wet climate results in thick evergreen forests called tropical rain forests. These are called 'selvas'. The trees here are tall with thick foliage which prevents sunlight from reaching the ground. The interior of these forests is therefore dark and damp which promotes the

thick growth of climbers and ferns along with tall trees. The main trees are ebony, mahogany, rosewood, rubber and cinchona which are hardwood trees. Balsa, the lightest wood is also found in these forests. The forests yield a variety of products ranging from rubber, nuts, gum, resins to quinine which is a drug used to cure malaria.

Wildlife

The evergreen forests of the Amazon basin are like a gigantic zoo—very rich in wildlife which includes mammals like monkeys, jaguars, pumas, tapirs, sloths, anteaters, etc. A variety of birds and insects are also found here. The basin is home to several species of reptiles and snakes like pythons, anacondas and crocodiles as well. Several species of freshwater fish are also found in the rivers.

People

The Amazon basin is home to several tribes who lead a primitive life, collecting food by hunting and fishing. They live in small huts made of branches and leaves. Generally, these are built on platforms in order to avoid flood waters. Yanomami and Kayapo are some of the tribes living in Amazon basin.

[IMAGE_START] Tribal people of the Amazon basin. Three individuals are shown, two of them are holding sticks and one is eating something. They are wearing traditional clothing and appear to be in a forested area. [IMAGE_END]

They grow crops like tapioca, sweet potato, pineapple, bananas, etc. Patches of land are cleared in order to grow these crops. This is known as slash and burn agriculture. Certain cash crops like rubber, coffee, cocoa and nuts are also grown commercially. This region is rich in minerals like gold, iron ore, copper, manganese and bauxite. So, mining is also carried on.

Life in the Amazon basin is gradually changing. The construction of the Trans-Amazonian highway has made all parts of the basin accessible. But the developmental activities are causing destruction of the biologically diverse forests. Forest areas are disappearing very rapidly due to large scale constructional activities.

THE GANGA-BRAHMAPUTRA BASIN

The Ganga-Brahmaputra basin is one of the largest river basins in the world. Lying south of the Himalayas, it extends approximately 2500 km from river Sutlej in the west to River Brahmaputra in the east.

The area is drained by the River Ganga, River Brahmaputra and their tributaries. Together, they have created one of the most fertile plains in the world. ## Climate and Vegetation
The Ganga-Brahmaputra basin lies in the subtropical region and enjoys a monsoon type of climate. Here, the summers are hot and wet, and the winters are cool and dry. The South-West Monsoon winds blowing between June and September bring heavy rainfall. As these rivers flow through the regions of heavy rainfall, they carry a huge amount of silt which helps in enriching the flood plains of North India.

The topography in this basin is varied. While certain areas are mountainous, the majority of the area is plains. The mountains are steep and inhospitable but the plains are fertile and support dense population. The vegetation cover varies according to landforms. The lower mountains are covered with thick tropical deciduous forests where trees like teak, sal and bamboo are found. These have great commercial value. ## Wildlife
The Ganga plain is devoid of much wildlife as the area has dense population. But in the Brahmaputra plains, elephants, tigers, deer, monkeys and even the one-horned rhinoceros are found. The delta area is home to the Royal Bengal Tiger, crocodiles and alligators. The area is also rich in aquatic life.

### People
The mountainous area being inhospitable is sparsely populated but the plains support a very dense population. Agriculture is the main occupation here.

Food crops like rice, wheat, maize, sorghum and nuts are cultivated over large areas. Cash crops like sugarcane, cotton, jute and tea are grown in different areas depending upon the climatic conditions. Fruits like bananas, vegetables, pulses and oilseeds are also grown. Though, the size of the farms is small, the primitive methods of agriculture have been replaced by modern farming techniques. Several agro-based industries have developed in the Ganga plain.
