Chapter 08

Comparing Quantities

Chapter Summary

Learning Objectives

  • Revise the concept of ratio and proportion and use it in solving many mathematical problems.
  • Use unitary method for solving mathematical problems.
  • Explain the concept of percentage and convert fractions, decimals or ratios into percentages and vice versa.
  • Find the given per cent of a given quantity and express one quantity as a percentage of another quantity.
  • Calculate the whole quantity and increase or decrease as per cent.
  • Apply the knowledge of percentages in real-life situations involving profit and loss and simple interest.

Tune Up

  • Comparison of two quantities using subtraction is done to find difference, while comparison using division is called ratio.
  • A wholesaler buys a bag of wheat weighing 90 kg and a bag of rice weighing 45 kg.
  • To compare weight using division, we divide the larger weight by the smaller weight.

Ratio

  • Ratio — A numerical relationship between two quantities of the same kind showing how many times a quantity is of the other.
  • The ratio of two quantities ( x ) and ( y ) is the fraction ( x/y ) where ( y \neq 0 ) and is represented as ( x : y ).
  • The two numbers which form a ratio are called its terms.
  • In the ratio ( x : y ), ( x ) is called the first term or antecedent and ( y ) is called the second term or consequent.
  • A ratio has no unit and is always expressed in its simplest form.

PROPORTION

  • Proportion — An equality of two ratios.
  • The order of the terms in a ratio is very important; for example, ( 5 : 8 \neq 8 : 5 ).
  • The ratio of two quantities can be found only if they are in the same units.
  • If four quantities are in proportion, the product of extremes equals the product of means.

Grasp It

  • The order of terms matters in a ratio.
  • Quantities must be in the same units before finding a ratio.
  • A ratio has no unit.

Simplest Form of a Ratio

  • A ratio is said to be in its simplest form if its terms have no common factor other than 1.
  • To find the simplest form, divide each term of the ratio by their Highest Common Factor (HCF).
  • For example, to simplify ( 16 : 48 ), divide both by 16 to get ( 1 : 3 ).

Comparison of Ratios

  • We can compare ratios to find out whether they are equivalent or not.
  • To compare ratios, write them as fractions in simplest forms.
  • Compare by converting them into like fractions or by cross multiplying them.
  • For example, to compare ( 2 : 3 ) and ( 4 : 5 ), convert to ( 10/15 ) and ( 12/15 ) respectively.

UNITARY METHOD

  • Unitary Method — A simple method of solving many mathematical problems where we first find out the value of a single unit and then use it to find the value of required number of such units.
  • From the provided value of certain number of units, calculate the value of one unit.
  • Multiply the value of one unit by the required number of units.
  • This method is useful for cost calculations, speed-distance-time problems, etc.

PERCENTAGE

  • Percentage — Another way of comparing quantities.
  • The term per cent is derived from the Latin word per centum which means per hundred or out of hundred.
  • Per cent is represented by the symbol '%' and means 1 hundredth.
  • Percentages are the numerators of the fractions whose denominators equal to 100.
  • For example, 2% is 2 out of 100 which is equal to ( 2/100 = 0.02 ).

Converting a Fraction into Percentage

  • Method 1: Convert the denominator of the fraction into 100 by multiplying or dividing it by a suitable factor.
  • Method 2: Multiply the fraction by 100% when the denominator cannot be easily converted into 100.
  • For example, ( 1/4 = 1/4 \times 100\% = 25\% ).
  • Mixed fractions should be converted to improper fractions first.

Converting a Decimal into Percentage

  • To convert a decimal into a percentage, simply multiply the decimal by 100 and put the '%' symbol.
  • For example, ( 0.32 = 0.32 \times 100\% = 32\% ).
  • For example, ( 1.5 = 1.5 \times 100\% = 150\% ).

Converting a Ratio into Percentage

  • To convert a ratio into a percentage, first convert the ratio to a fraction.
  • Then multiply the fraction by 100%.
  • For example, ( 7 : 25 = 7/25 \times 100\% = 28\% ).

Converting a Percentage into Decimal, Fraction or Ratio

  • To convert a percentage to a fraction, decimal or ratio, remove the '%' symbol and multiply by ( 1/100 ).
  • This is equivalent to dividing the given value by 100.
  • For example, 24% as a fraction is ( 24/100 = 6/25 ).
  • As a decimal, 24% is 0.24.
  • As a ratio, 24% is ( 6 : 25 ).

Percentage for Comparison

  • Percentages are used for comparing quantities effectively.
  • To compare literacy rates, calculate the percentage of literate people in each village.
  • To compare mixture concentrations, calculate the percentage of syrup in the total mixture volume.
  • Higher percentage indicates better performance or higher concentration.

APPLICATIONS OF PERCENTAGE

To Find the Given Percentage of a Given Quantity

  • To find the given percentage of a given quantity, first convert the percentage into a fraction.
  • Then multiply it with the given quantity.
  • For example, 12% of ₹500 means ( 12/100 \times 500 = ₹60 ).
  • If a percentage is given for one part, the remaining part is ( 100\% ) minus that percentage.

To Express a Quantity as a Percentage of Another

  • First express both the quantities in the same units.
  • Use the formula: Required % = ( (\text{First quantity} / \text{Second quantity}) \times 100\% ).
  • For example, expressing 6 hours as a percentage of 2 days requires converting 2 days to 48 hours first.

To Find the Whole Using a Given Percentage

  • If a certain percentage of a quantity is known, the whole quantity can be found.
  • Let the whole quantity be ( x ). Set up the equation: ( \text{Percentage} \% \text{ of } x = \text{Given Value} ).
  • Solve for ( x ) by dividing the given value by the percentage fraction.
  • For example, if 30% of price is ₹2,250, then price = ( 2,250 \times 100 / 30 ).

Increase or Decrease as Per Cent

  • Increase or decrease of quantities is conveniently expressed in percentage.
  • Increase % = ( (\text{Increase in the quantity} / \text{Original quantity}) \times 100\% ).
  • Decrease % = ( (\text{Decrease in the quantity} / \text{Original quantity}) \times 100\% ).
  • This helps in getting a clear idea about scenarios like population growth or salary hikes.

Estimation in Percentage

  • Estimation is the method of quickly and efficiently approximating the value of an expression.
  • Round off values to nearest convenient numbers to calculate approximate percentages.
  • For example, 24% of 6,100 can be approximated to 25% of 6,000.

PROFIT AND LOSS

  • Cost Price (CP) — The price at which an article is purchased by the shopkeeper.
  • Selling Price (SP) — The price at which an article is sold by the shopkeeper.
  • Profit — Occurs when SP > CP. Profit = SP - CP.
  • Loss — Occurs when CP > SP. Loss = CP - SP.
  • If CP = SP, there is neither profit nor loss.

Overhead Charges

  • Overhead Charges — Additional expenses made on the commodity before selling it.
  • Includes repairs, maintenance, transportation, etc.
  • Actual Cost Price = Cost at which bought + Extra money spent.
  • Profit or Loss is calculated on the Actual Cost Price including overheads.

Profit or Loss as a Percentage

  • Profit % or Loss % is always calculated on the Cost Price of an article.
  • Profit % = ( (\text{Profit} / \text{Cost price}) \times 100\% ).
  • Loss % = ( (\text{Loss} / \text{Cost price}) \times 100\% ).
  • To find SP when Loss % is given: SP = CP - Loss.
  • To find CP when SP and Profit % are given: Use the unitary method based on assumed CP of 100.

SIMPLE INTEREST

  • Principal (P) — The money borrowed or lent.
  • Time (T) — The period for which the money is borrowed or lent.
  • Interest — The extra money paid for using the borrowed money.
  • Rate of interest (R) — The rate at which the interest is calculated on ₹100 per annum.
  • Simple Interest (SI) — The interest calculated on the original principal only.
  • Amount (A) — The money returned or given back, which includes the principal and the interest. Amount = Principal + Simple Interest.

Simple Interest Formula

  • Simple Interest (SI) = ( (\text{Principal} \times \text{Rate of interest} \times \text{Time}) / 100 ).
  • SI = ( (P \times R \times T) / 100 ).
  • Relationships derived:
    • ( P = (SI \times 100) / (R \times T) )
    • ( R = (SI \times 100) / (P \times T) )
    • ( T = (SI \times 100) / (P \times R) )
  • Time must be in years. If given in months, convert to years by dividing by 12.

Question 1: Express the following as ratios in the simplest form.
a) 655 m to 1 km
b) 35 minutes to 2 hours
c) 845 g to 1 kg
d) 12 hours to 1.5 days

Answer:
a) 655 m to 1 km
Convert units to same measure: 1 km = 1000 m
Ratio = 655 : 1000
Divide both terms by HCF (5): 131 : 200
Simplest form = 131 : 200

b) 35 minutes to 2 hours
Convert units to same measure: 2 hours = 120 minutes
Ratio = 35 : 120
Divide both terms by HCF (5): 7 : 24
Simplest form = 7 : 24

c) 845 g to 1 kg
Convert units to same measure: 1 kg = 1000 g
Ratio = 845 : 1000
Divide both terms by HCF (5): 169 : 200
Simplest form = 169 : 200

d) 12 hours to 1.5 days
Convert units to same measure: 1.5 days = 1.5 * 24 hours = 36 hours
Ratio = 12 : 36
Divide both terms by HCF (12): 1 : 3
Simplest form = 1 : 3

Question 2: Which is greater?
a) 5 : 8 or 7 : 9
b) 49 : 35 or 45 : 25

Answer:
a) 5 : 8 or 7 : 9
Convert to fractions: 5/8 and 7/9
LCM of 8 and 9 is 72.
5/8 = (5 * 9) / 72 = 45/72
7/9 = (7 * 8) / 72 = 56/72
Since 56/72 > 45/72, 7 : 9 is greater.

b) 49 : 35 or 45 : 25
Simplify ratios first.
49 : 35 = (49/7) : (35/7) = 7 : 5
45 : 25 = (45/5) : (25/5) = 9 : 5
Compare 7/5 and 9/5. Since denominators are same, compare numerators.
9 > 7, so 9 : 5 is greater. Thus, 45 : 25 is greater.

Question 3: Are the ratios 7 : 9 and 98 : 126 equivalent?
Answer:
First ratio = 7/9
Second ratio = 98/126
Simplify second ratio: Divide numerator and denominator by 14.
98 / 14 = 7
126 / 14 = 9
So, 98 : 126 = 7 : 9.
Yes, the ratios are equivalent.

Question 4: Are 13, 15, 17 and 23 in proportion? Give reason.
Answer:
For four numbers a, b, c, d to be in proportion, a * d must equal b * c.
Here a = 13, b = 15, c = 17, d = 23.
Product of extremes = 13 * 23 = 299
Product of means = 15 * 17 = 255
Since 299 ≠ 255, the numbers are not in proportion.

Question 5: Find m if 25 : m :: 18 : 36.
Answer:
Given 25 : m :: 18 : 36
This implies 25 / m = 18 / 36
Simplify 18/36 = 1/2
So, 25 / m = 1 / 2
Cross multiply: 1 * m = 25 * 2
m = 50

Question 6: A ribbon, 10 cm 5 mm long, is to be divided in the ratio 1 : 4. Find the length of each part.
Answer:
Total length = 10 cm 5 mm = 10.5 cm
Total parts = 1 + 4 = 5
Length of one part = 10.5 / 5 = 2.1 cm
First part = 1 * 2.1 cm = 2.1 cm
Second part = 4 * 2.1 cm = 8.4 cm
Lengths are 2.1 cm and 8.4 cm.

Question 7: A road map is given with a scale of 1 : 2,00,000. What is the actual distance between the two buildings in kilometres, if the distance on the map is 4.5 cm?
Answer:
Scale 1 : 2,00,000 means 1 cm on map = 2,00,000 cm actual.
Map distance = 4.5 cm
Actual distance = 4.5 * 2,00,000 cm = 9,00,000 cm
Convert to km: 1 km = 1,00,000 cm
Actual distance = 9,00,000 / 1,00,000 = 9 km

Question 8: Sunita comes across a deal in a departmental store: a box of 6 bottles of a drink for ₹24 and a crate of 30 bottles of the same drink for ₹105. Which is a better deal?
Answer:
Cost of 6 bottles = ₹24

Since ₹3.5 < ₹4, the crate of 30 bottles is a better deal.

## Ratio
A ratio is a numerical relationship between two quantities of the same kind. It shows how many times a quantity is of the other.
The ratio of two quantities \( x \) and \( y \) of the same kind is the fraction \(\frac{x}{y}\) (where \( y \neq 0 \)) and is represented as \( x : y \). It is read as ' \( x \) is to \( y \)'.
The two numbers which form a ratio are called its terms. In the ratio \( x : y \), \( x \) is called the first term or antecedent and \( y \) is called the second term or consequent. For example, in the Tune Up section, the ratio of weight of wheat bag to weight of rice bag is \( 90 : 45 = 2 : 1 \).

## Grasp It

- The order of the terms in a ratio is very important. For example, 5 : 8 ≠ 8 : 5

## Simplest Form of a Ratio

A ratio is said to be in its simplest form if its terms (antecedent and consequent) have no common factor other than 1. It is always expressed in the simplest form.

For example, to find the simplest form of 16 : 48, we divide each term of the ratio by 16 (that is, HCF of 16 and 48).

\[
\frac{16}{48} = \frac{16 \div 16}{48 \div 16} = \frac{1}{3}
\]

Thus, the simplest form of 16 : 48 is 1 : 3.

# PERCENTAGE

Percentage is another way of comparing quantities. In our daily lives, we frequently come across the word per cent or percentage. Some examples of percentage are 20 per cent discount on clothes, 90 per cent marks scored by Rohan, 11 per cent interest on car loan, etc.

## Converting a Fraction into Percentage

There are two methods to convert a fraction into a percentage.

### Method 1: Convert the denominator of the fraction into 100 by multiplying or dividing it by a suitable factor. The corresponding numerator obtained will give the required percentage. For example,

\[ \frac{18}{300} = \frac{18 \div 3}{300 \div 3} = \frac{6}{100} = 6\% \]

This method is very useful when the denominator is a multiple of 2 or 5 or 2 and 5 or 100.

# PROFIT AND LOSS

In our day-to-day life, we go to the market many times and buy things from different shops. Shopkeepers buy these things either directly from the manufacturers or from the wholesale market. The price at which an article is purchased by the shopkeeper is called its cost price (CP) and the price at which an article is sold by the shopkeeper is called its selling price (SP).

1. If the selling price is more than the cost price, the shopkeeper makes a profit or gain in the transaction.
   Profit = SP - CP (when SP > CP)
2. If the selling price is less than the cost price, the shopkeeper bears a loss in the transaction.
   Loss = CP - SP (when CP > SP)
3. If the selling price and cost price are the same, there is neither profit nor loss in the transaction.
   In this case, CP = SP = No profit no loss

For example, a man buys a watch for ₹2,500 and sells it for ₹3,000.
Here, SP > CP, so there is a profit.
Profit = ₹(3,000 - 2,500)
= ₹500

## SIMPLE INTEREST

When we need to buy something which is essential, but we do not have enough money to do so, we generally borrow the money from a relative or a friend or a bank. When money is borrowed for a certain period of time, say 2 years or 5 years, and then returned, the borrower pays some extra money to the lender, which is a charge for the use of the money. The extra money paid is known as interest.

The money borrowed or lent is called the principal (P).

The period for which the money is borrowed or lent is called the time (T).

The extra money to be paid for using the borrowed money is called the interest and is denoted by I. The interest which we are referring to here is the simple interest (SI).

The rate at which the interest is calculated on ₹100 is called the rate of interest (R). It is calculated on an yearly basis and agreed upon at the time of borrowing or lending the money.

The money returned or given back, which includes the principal and the interest, is called the amount (A), that is,

Amount = Principal + Simple Interest

For example, Rajat takes a loan of ₹10,000 from a bank for 3 years at a rate of 5% per annum.

Interest on ₹100 for 1 year = ₹5

Interest on ₹1 for 1 year = ₹$\frac{5}{100}$

Interest on ₹1 for 3 years = ₹$\frac{5 \times 3}{100}$

Interest on ₹10,000 for 3 years = ₹$\frac{5 \times 3 \times 10,000}{100}$

From the above calculations, it is observed that the simple interest is given by the following formula.

Simple Interest (SI)

= $\frac{\text{Principal} \times \text{Rate of interest} \times \text{Time}}{100}$

= $\frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$

<!-- Page: 118 -->

We can also derive more relationships from this formula.
P = $\frac{SI \times 100}{R \times T}$; R = $\frac{SI \times 100}{P \times T}$; T = $\frac{SI \times 100}{P \times R}$

## Solved Examples

### Example 20
Find the simple interest and the amount payable on an investment of ₹25,000 at 15% per annum over a period of 4 years.
Solution: Principal = ₹25,000
Rate of interest = 15% p.a.
Time = 4 years
SI = $\frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$
= $\frac{25,000 \times 15 \times 4}{100}$
= ₹15,000
Amount = P + SI = ₹25,000 + ₹15,000
= ₹40,000
Thus, the simple interest is ₹15,000 and the amount is ₹40,000.

### Example 21
Roshni lent ₹7,200 at the rate of 6% per annum for 6 years and Rinki lent ₹8,500 at the rate of 5% per annum for 4 years. Who earned more interest and by how much?

SI = $\frac{7,200 \times 6 \times 6}{100}$
= ₹2,592
Interest earned by Rinki:
P = ₹8,500, R = 5% and T = 4 years

### Example 22
If Ashu pays an interest of ₹1,080 for 9 months on the sum of ₹18,000, find the rate of interest.

R = $\frac{SI \times 100}{P \times T}$
= $\frac{1,080 \times 100 \times 4}{18,000 \times 3}$
= 8%
Thus, the required rate of interest is 8% p.a.

- Ratio is a comparison of two quantities of the same kind using division.
