Chapter 08

Comparing Quantities

Chapter Summary

Ratio and Proportion

  • A ratio compares two quantities of the same kind using division. It is written as x : y and read as "x is to y".
  • The first term (x) is called the antecedent, and the second term (y) is called the consequent.
  • The ratio must have the same units. For example, ₹5 and 200 paisa must be converted to the same unit before comparing.
  • A ratio has no unit.
  • A ratio is in its simplest form when the two terms have no common factor other than 1. For example, 16 : 48 simplifies to 1 : 3.
  • To compare ratios, convert them into fractions and then compare by making them like fractions or by cross-multiplying.

Unitary Method

  • The unitary method finds the value of one unit first and then uses it to find the value of the required number of units.
  • Example: If 24 oranges cost ₹276, then cost of 1 orange is ₹276 ÷ 24 = ₹11.5. So, cost of 20 oranges = 20 × ₹11.5 = ₹230.

Percentage

  • Percentage means "per hundred" and is used to compare quantities.
  • It is represented by the symbol % and means 1/100.
  • To convert a fraction to percentage: multiply by 100%.
    Example: 1/4 = (1 × 100)/4 = 25%
  • To convert a decimal to percentage: multiply by 100 and add the % sign.
    Example: 0.32 = 32%
  • To convert a ratio to percentage: convert to a fraction and multiply by 100%.
    Example: 7 : 25 = (7/25) × 100% = 28%
  • To convert percentage to fraction, decimal, or ratio: divide by 100.
    Example: 24% = 24/100 = 6/25 = 0.24 = 6 : 25

Applications of Percentage

  • Finding a given percentage of a quantity: Multiply the quantity by the percentage (as a fraction).
    Example: 60% of 220 = (60/100) × 220 = 132
  • Expressing one quantity as a percentage of another:
    Formula: (First quantity / Second quantity) × 100%
    Example: 6 hours as a percentage of 2 days = (6 / 48) × 100% = 12.5%
  • Finding the whole quantity when a percentage is given:
    Use: Whole = (Given value × 100) / Percentage
    Example: 30% of a number is 16,500 → Whole = (16,500 × 100) / 30 = 55,000
  • Increase or decrease as a percentage:
    Increase % = (Increase / Original value) × 100%
    Decrease % = (Decrease / Original value) × 100%

Profit and Loss

  • Cost Price (CP): Price at which an item is bought.
  • Selling Price (SP): Price at which an item is sold.
  • Profit = SP – CP (when SP > CP)
  • Loss = CP – SP (when CP > SP)
  • Profit % = (Profit / CP) × 100%
  • Loss % = (Loss / CP) × 100%
  • Overhead charges (like repair, transport) are added to the cost price before calculating profit or loss.

Simple Interest (SI)

  • Simple Interest is the extra money paid for using borrowed money.
  • Formula:
    SI = (P × R × T) / 100
    Where:
    P = Principal (amount borrowed),
    R = Rate of interest per annum,
    T = Time in years
  • Amount (A) = P + SI
  • Other formulas:
    P = (SI × 100) / (R × T)
    R = (SI × 100) / (P × T)
    T = (SI × 100) / (P × R)

Key Concepts Summary

  • Ratio compares two quantities using division.
  • Proportion is an equality of two ratios.
  • Unitary method helps solve problems by finding the value of one unit.
  • Percentage is a way of comparing quantities out of 100.
  • Profit and loss are expressed as percentages based on cost price.
  • Simple interest is calculated using the formula: SI = (P × R × T) / 100.

This chapter helps in solving real-life problems involving comparisons, discounts, profit, loss, and interest using ratios, percentages, and simple interest.

Key Terms

Ratio — a comparison of two quantities of the same kind using division.
Proportion — an equality of two ratios.
Unitary method — a method of finding the value of one unit and then using it to find the value of required number of units.
Percentage — a way of comparing quantities; per cent means per hundred.
Fraction — a part of a whole, expressed as a numerator over a denominator.
Decimal — a number expressed in base 10, using a decimal point.
Ratio in simplest form — a ratio where the terms have no common factor other than 1.
Comparison of ratios — determining whether two ratios are equivalent by converting them to fractions and comparing.
Cost price (CP) — the price at which an article is purchased.
Selling price (SP) — the price at which an article is sold.
Profit — the amount earned when selling price is greater than cost price; profit = SP – CP.
Loss — the amount incurred when selling price is less than cost price; loss = CP – SP.
Profit percentage — profit expressed as a percentage of cost price; calculated as (profit / CP) × 100%.
Loss percentage — loss expressed as a percentage of cost price; calculated as (loss / CP) × 100%.
Simple interest (SI) — the extra money paid for borrowing or lent money; calculated as (P × R × T) / 100.
Principal (P) — the initial amount of money borrowed or lent.
Time (T) — the duration for which money is borrowed or lent, usually in years.
Rate of interest (R) — the percentage of interest charged per year on ₹100.
Amount (A) — the total money returned or given back, including principal and interest; A = P + SI.
Overhead charges — additional expenses incurred before selling a product, such as repair, transportation, or maintenance.
Percentage of a quantity — the value obtained by multiplying the quantity by a given percentage (converted to fraction).
Conversion of fraction to percentage — changing a fraction to a percentage by multiplying by 100%.
Conversion of decimal to percentage — multiplying the decimal by 100 and adding the % symbol.
Conversion of ratio to percentage — converting the ratio to a fraction and then multiplying by 100%.
Conversion of percentage to fraction — removing the % symbol and dividing by 100.
Conversion of percentage to decimal — dividing the percentage by 100.
Conversion of percentage to ratio — dividing the percentage by 100 and expressing as a ratio.
Increase or decrease as a percentage — expressing change in quantity as a percentage of the original value.
Estimation in percentage — approximating a percentage value to quickly get a close result.
Equivalent ratios — ratios that are equal when simplified.
Like fractions — fractions with the same denominator.
LCM — least common multiple, used to convert fractions to have a common denominator.
HCF — highest common factor, used to simplify ratios.
Whole quantity — the original amount from which a given percentage is derived.
Per cent — a fraction with denominator 100, derived from the Latin word "per centum" meaning per hundred.

## Ratio
A ratio is a numerical relationship between two quantities of the same kind. It shows how many times a quantity is of the other.
The ratio of two quantities \( x \) and \( y \) of the same kind is the fraction \(\frac{x}{y}\) (where \( y \neq 0 \)) and is represented as \( x : y \). It is read as ' \( x \) is to \( y \)'.
The two numbers which form a ratio are called its terms. In the ratio \( x : y \), \( x \) is called the first term or antecedent and \( y \) is called the second term or consequent. For example, in the Tune Up section, the ratio of weight of wheat bag to weight of rice bag is \( 90 : 45 = 2 : 1 \).

# PROPORTION

## Grasp It

- The order of the terms in a ratio is very important. For example, 5 : 8 ≠ 8 : 5
- The ratio of two quantities can be found only if they are in the same units. For example, we cannot find the ratio of ₹5 and 200 paisa. We need to change rupees into paisa or vice versa.
- A ratio has no unit.

## Simplest Form of a Ratio

A ratio is said to be in its simplest form if its terms (antecedent and consequent) have no common factor other than 1. It is always expressed in the simplest form.

For example, to find the simplest form of 16 : 48, we divide each term of the ratio by 16 (that is, HCF of 16 and 48).

\[
\frac{16}{48} = \frac{16 \div 16}{48 \div 16} = \frac{1}{3}
\]

Thus, the simplest form of 16 : 48 is 1 : 3.

## Comparison of Ratios

We can also compare ratios to find out whether they are equivalent or not. To compare ratios, we first write them as fractions in simplest forms. Then, compare by converting them into like fractions or by cross multiplying them.

For example, let us find which is greater 2 : 3 or 4 : 5.

\[
2 : 3 = \frac{2}{3} \text{ and } 4 : 5 = \frac{4}{5} \text{ LCM of 3 and 5 = 15}
\]

\[
\frac{2}{3} = \frac{2 \times 5}{3 \times 5} = \frac{10}{15} \text{ and } \frac{4}{5} = \frac{4 \times 3}{5 \times 3} = \frac{12}{15}
\]

\[
\Rightarrow \frac{10}{15} < \frac{12}{15} \text{ or } \frac{2}{3} < \frac{4}{5}
\]

Thus, 2 : 3 < 4 : 5.

# PERCENTAGE

Percentage is another way of comparing quantities. In our daily lives, we frequently come across the word per cent or percentage. Some examples of percentage are 20 per cent discount on clothes, 90 per cent marks scored by Rohan, 11 per cent interest on car loan, etc.

The term per cent is derived from the Latin word *per centum* which means per hundred or out of hundred. Per cent is represented by the symbol '%' and means 1 hundredth. For example, 2% is 2 out of 100 which is equal to $\frac{2}{100} = 0.02$. In other words, percentages are the numerators of the fractions whose denominators equal to 100.

## Converting a Fraction into Percentage

There are two methods to convert a fraction into a percentage.

### Method 1: Convert the denominator of the fraction into 100 by multiplying or dividing it by a suitable factor. The corresponding numerator obtained will give the required percentage. For example,

\[ \frac{6}{23} \times 100\% = \frac{600}{23}\% = 26\frac{2}{23}\% \]

\[ 2\frac{3}{4} = \frac{11}{4} = \frac{11}{4} \times 100\% = \frac{1100}{4}\% = 275\% \]

# PROFIT AND LOSS

In our day-to-day life, we go to the market many times and buy things from different shops. Shopkeepers buy these things either directly from the manufacturers or from the wholesale market. The price at which an article is purchased by the shopkeeper is called its cost price (CP) and the price at which an article is sold by the shopkeeper is called its selling price (SP).

1. If the selling price is more than the cost price, the shopkeeper makes a profit or gain in the transaction.
   Profit = SP - CP (when SP > CP)
2. If the selling price is less than the cost price, the shopkeeper bears a loss in the transaction.
   Loss = CP - SP (when CP > SP)
3. If the selling price and cost price are the same, there is neither profit nor loss in the transaction.
   In this case, CP = SP = No profit no loss

For example, a man buys a watch for ₹2,500 and sells it for ₹3,000.
Here, SP > CP, so there is a profit.
Profit = ₹(3,000 - 2,500)
= ₹500

## Overhead Charges

Overhead charges include the additional expenses that a seller makes on the commodity before selling it. When a person buys a product and spends money on it for repairs, maintenance, transportation, etc. before selling it, the total cost price of the product will be the sum of the cost at which the person bought the product and the extra money spent on it.

For example, Ramesh bought a second-hand mobile phone for ₹1,500 and spent ₹500 on its repair. He sold it for ₹2,500.
Actual cost price of the mobile phone
= ₹(1,500 + 500) = ₹2,000
Selling price of the mobile phone
= ₹2,500

## SIMPLE INTEREST

When we need to buy something which is essential, but we do not have enough money to do so, we generally borrow the money from a relative or a friend or a bank. When money is borrowed for a certain period of time, say 2 years or 5 years, and then returned, the borrower pays some extra money to the lender, which is a charge for the use of the money. The extra money paid is known as interest.

The money borrowed or lent is called the principal (P).

The period for which the money is borrowed or lent is called the time (T).

The extra money to be paid for using the borrowed money is called the interest and is denoted by I. The interest which we are referring to here is the simple interest (SI).

The rate at which the interest is calculated on ₹100 is called the rate of interest (R). It is calculated on an yearly basis and agreed upon at the time of borrowing or lending the money.

The money returned or given back, which includes the principal and the interest, is called the amount (A), that is,

Amount = Principal + Simple Interest

For example, Rajat takes a loan of ₹10,000 from a bank for 3 years at a rate of 5% per annum.

Interest on ₹100 for 1 year = ₹5

Interest on ₹1 for 1 year = ₹$\frac{5}{100}$

Interest on ₹1 for 3 years = ₹$\frac{5 \times 3}{100}$

Interest on ₹10,000 for 3 years = ₹$\frac{5 \times 3 \times 10,000}{100}$

From the above calculations, it is observed that the simple interest is given by the following formula.

Simple Interest (SI)

= $\frac{\text{Principal} \times \text{Rate of interest} \times \text{Time}}{100}$

= $\frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$

<!-- Page: 118 -->

We can also derive more relationships from this formula.
P = $\frac{SI \times 100}{R \times T}$; R = $\frac{SI \times 100}{P \times T}$; T = $\frac{SI \times 100}{P \times R}$

= $\frac{25,000 \times 15 \times 4}{100}$
= ₹15,000

= ₹40,000

## Banking

When you take a loan from a bank or a financial institution, you are required to return the same along with interest over the stipulated tenure of the loan. Simple interest formula is one of the easiest methods of interest computation, which is used for short-term loans, car loans and other consumer loans extended by banks and financial institutions. Also, the interest paid on savings bank accounts and term deposits by banks is also based on simple interest.

Students are divided into groups and provided with different loan scenarios. Each group will compare two loans with different interest rates and tenures.

### Examples:

- **Loan A:** ₹40,000 at 9% per annum for 2 years
- **Loan B:** ₹40,000 at 7% per annum for 3 years

Students will discuss which loan has a lower total repayment amount. How does the interest rate and tenure affect the total repayment amount?
