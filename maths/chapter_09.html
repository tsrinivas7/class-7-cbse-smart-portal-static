Chapter 09

Lines and Angles – Summary

Chapter Summary

Key Concepts

1. Types of Angles

  • Complementary Angles: Two angles are complementary if their sum is 90°.
  • Complement of angle x = 90° – x
  • Supplementary Angles: Two angles are supplementary if their sum is 180°.
  • Supplement of angle x = 180° – x
  • Adjacent Angles: Angles that share a common vertex and a common arm, with non-common arms on opposite sides of the common arm.
  • Linear Pair: A pair of adjacent angles whose non-common arms are opposite rays and sum to 180°.
  • Linear pairs are always supplementary.
  • Vertically Opposite Angles: Formed when two lines intersect. They are opposite each other and are always equal.

2. Transversal

  • A transversal is a line that intersects two or more lines at different points.
  • When a transversal cuts two lines, it forms eight angles:
  • Interior angles: Between the two lines (e.g., ∠3, ∠4, ∠5, ∠6)
  • Exterior angles: Outside the two lines
  • Alternate interior angles: On opposite sides of the transversal, inside the lines (e.g., ∠4 and ∠6)
  • Alternate exterior angles: On opposite sides, outside the lines (e.g., ∠1 and ∠7)
  • Corresponding angles: On the same side of the transversal, one interior and one exterior (e.g., ∠1 and ∠5)
  • Co-interior angles: On the same side of the transversal and both interior (e.g., ∠4 and ∠5)

3. Properties When Lines Are Parallel

When a transversal intersects two parallel lines:
- Corresponding angles are equal (e.g., ∠1 = ∠5)
- Alternate interior angles are equal (e.g., ∠4 = ∠6)
- Alternate exterior angles are equal (e.g., ∠2 = ∠8)
- Co-interior angles are supplementary (sum to 180°) (e.g., ∠4 + ∠5 = 180°)

Converse: If any of these conditions are true (e.g., corresponding angles equal), then the lines are parallel.

4. Shapes to Remember

  • F-shape: Shows corresponding angles
  • Z-shape: Shows alternate interior angles

Solved Examples (Key Ideas)

  • Example 1: If an angle is equal to its complement → x = 90° – x → x = 45°
  • Example 2: Two supplementary angles, greater is 54° more than smaller → x – (180° – x) = 54° → x = 117°, other = 63°
  • Example 5: Vertically opposite angles are equal → if one angle is 70°, the opposite angle is also 70°
  • Example 7: Interior angles on same side of transversal sum to 180° → (6x + 7°) + (3x + 38°) = 180° → x = 15°
  • Example 8: If sum of interior angles on same side is not 180° → lines are not parallel

Important Facts to Remember

  • Two angles are complementary if their sum is 90°.
  • Two angles are supplementary if their sum is 180°.
  • Adjacent angles may or may not form a linear pair.
  • A linear pair is always supplementary.
  • Vertically opposite angles are always equal.
  • When two lines are parallel and cut by a transversal:
  • Corresponding angles are equal
  • Alternate interior angles are equal
  • Co-interior angles sum to 180°

Real-Life Application

  • Solar Panels: The angle at which solar panels are tilted affects how much sunlight they capture. For example, if a panel is tilted at 30° to the ground and the sun’s rays are perpendicular to the ground, the acute angle between the sun’s rays and the panel is 60° (since 90° – 30° = 60°).

  4. Do adjacent right angles form a linear pair? → Yes, because they share a common arm and sum to 180°

Problem Solving Tips

  • Use the fact that vertically opposite angles are equal.
  • Use the sum of angles on a straight line = 180°.
  • When lines are parallel, use corresponding or alternate angles to find unknown values.
  • For supplementary angles, set up equations using the sum = 180°.
  • For complementary angles, use sum = 90°.

Key Terms

Complementary angles — two angles whose sum is 90°.
Supplementary angles — two angles whose sum is 180°.
Adjacent angles — two angles that have a common vertex, a common arm, and their non-common arms lie on opposite sides of the common arm.
Linear pair — a pair of adjacent angles whose non-common arms are opposite rays and whose sum is 180°.
Vertically opposite angles — two angles formed when two lines intersect, which have no common arm and are opposite each other; they are always equal.
Transversal — a line that intersects two or more lines at distinct points.
Interior angles — angles formed between two lines on the same side of a transversal.
Exterior angles — angles formed outside the two lines on the same side of a transversal.
Alternate interior angles — interior angles on opposite sides of a transversal that do not form a linear pair.
Alternate exterior angles — exterior angles on opposite sides of a transversal that do not form a linear pair.
Corresponding angles — angles on the same side of a transversal, one interior and one exterior, that do not form a linear pair.
Co-interior angles — interior angles on the same side of a transversal.
Parallel lines — lines that never intersect and are always at the same distance apart.
Corresponding angles property — when a transversal intersects two parallel lines, corresponding angles are equal.
Alternate interior angles property — when a transversal intersects two parallel lines, alternate interior angles are equal.
Co-interior angles property — when a transversal intersects two parallel lines, the sum of co-interior angles on the same side is 180°.
Converse of parallel lines property — if a transversal intersects two lines such that a pair of alternate interior angles or corresponding angles are equal, then the lines are parallel.
Angle of intersection — the angle formed when two lines meet at a point.
Vertex of an angle — the common point where two arms of an angle meet.
Arm of an angle — one of the two rays that form an angle.
Line segment — a part of a line with two endpoints.
Line — a straight path that extends infinitely in both directions and has no endpoints.
Ray — a part of a line that has one endpoint and extends infinitely in one direction.

# SPECIAL PAIRS OF ANGLES

A combination of two angles is called a pair of angles. Certain pairs of angles have special properties based on their relationship. Let us study about these special pairs of angles.

## Complementary Angles

Two angles are said to be complementary if the sum of their measures is 90°. Each angle is said to be a complement of the other.

[IMAGE_START] Two diagrams showing complementary angles. In diagram a), ∠AOB and ∠BOC are complementary angles with measures 45° and 45° respectively. In diagram b), ∠PQR and ∠XYZ are complementary angles with measures 60° and 30° respectively. [IMAGE_END]

## Supplementary Angles

Two angles are said to be supplementary if the sum of their measures is 180°. Each angle is said to be a supplement of the other.

[IMAGE_START] Two diagrams showing supplementary angles. In diagram a), ∠AOB and ∠BOC are supplementary angles with measures 120° and 60° respectively. In diagram b), ∠PQR and ∠XYZ are supplementary angles with measures 150° and 30° respectively. [IMAGE_END]

## Grasp It

If \( x \) is any angle, then
- complement of \( x = 90° - x \).
- supplement of \( x = 180° - x \).

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

The measure of an angle is equal to its complement. Find the measure of the angle.

Solution: Let the angle be \( x \).

Complement of \( x = 90° - x \)

It is given that \( x \) is equal to its complement.

\[ x = 90° - x \]

\[ \Rightarrow 2x = 90° \]

\[ \Rightarrow x = \frac{90°}{2} = 45° \]

Thus, the required angle is 45°.

### Example 2

In a pair of supplementary angles, the greater angle is 54° more than the smaller angle. Find the measure of the angles.

Solution: Let the greater angle be \( x \) and the smaller angle be \( (180° - x) \).

According to the given condition,

\[ x - (180° - x) = 54° \]

\[ \Rightarrow x - 180° + x = 54° \]

\[ \Rightarrow 2x = 54° + 180° \]

\[ \Rightarrow 2x = 234° \]

\[ \Rightarrow x = 117° \]

Thus, the required angles are 117° and \( (180° - 117°) = 63° \).

## Adjacent Angles

Two angles are said to be adjacent, if they have a common vertex, a common arm and the other two arms lie on opposite sides of the common arm.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two angles with a common vertex and common arm, and the other two arms on opposite sides of the common arm. [IMAGE_END]

In Fig. 9.5 b), ∠POM and ∠PON are adjacent angles.

## Linear Pair

A pair of adjacent angles is said to form a linear pair, if the non-common arms are opposite rays and the angles are supplementary.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two adjacent angles forming a linear pair with the non-common arms as opposite rays. [IMAGE_END]

In Fig. 9.6, ∠PSQ + ∠QSR = 110° + 70° = 180°

Thus, ∠PSQ and ∠QSR form a linear pair.

## Grasp It

- Linear pair of angles are always adjacent.
- Adjacent angles may or may not form a linear pair.
- Linear pair of angles are always supplementary.
- Supplementary angles need not form a linear pair.

In Fig. 9.9, find the measures of ∠AOB, ∠BOC and ∠COD.

### Solution

We know that ∠AOB, ∠BOC and ∠COD are angles on a straight line.

So, ∠AOB + ∠BOC + ∠COD = 180°

y + (y - 29°) + (y + 17°) = 180°

⇒ 3y - 12° = 180°

⇒ 3y = 180° + 12° = 192°

⇒ y = $\frac{192°}{3}$ = 64°

Therefore, ∠AOB = y = 64°, ∠BOC = (y - 29°) = (64° - 29°) = 35° and ∠COD = (y + 17°) = (64° + 17°) = 81°.

## Vertically Opposite Angles

∠AOC and ∠BOD have no common arm, so, they are vertically opposite angles. Similarly, ∠BOC and ∠AOD are vertically opposite angles. Vertically opposite angles are always equal to each other.

This can be verified by measuring the angles. In Fig 9.10, measure the angles using a protractor and confirm that ∠AOC = ∠BOD and ∠BOC = ∠AOD. This can also be proved in the following way.

Proof: ∠AOC + ∠BOC = 180° (Linear pair)

∠BOC + ∠BOD = 180° (Linear pair)

So, ∠BOC + ∠BOD = ∠AOC + ∠BOC

⇒ ∠BOD = ∠AOC

Similarly, we can prove that ∠BOC = ∠AOD.

Hence, it is proved that vertically opposite angles are equal to each other.

# TRANSVERSAL

The word 'transverse' means 'to place across'. A transversal is a line which intersects (goes across) two or more lines at different points.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two lines AB and CD intersected by a transversal EF at points P and Q. Angles are labeled as ∠1, ∠2, ∠3, ∠4, ∠5, ∠6, ∠7, and ∠8. [IMAGE_END]

In Fig. 9.17 a), EF is a transversal to the lines AB and CD because it intersects them at different points P and Q, respectively. But in Fig. 9.17 b), EF is not a transversal because it cuts both the lines AB and CD at the same point P.

## Angles Formed by a Transversal

When a transversal intersects two lines at different points, then eight angles are formed as shown in Fig. 9.18. Let us study about these angles in detail.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two lines AB and CD intersected by a transversal EF at points G and H. Angles are labeled as ∠1, ∠2, ∠3, ∠4, ∠5, ∠6, ∠7, and ∠8. [IMAGE_END]

### 1. Interior angles

All angles which have the line segment GH as one arm are called interior angles; they lie between lines AB and CD. In Fig. 9.18, ∠3, ∠4, ∠5 and ∠6 are interior angles.

### 2. Exterior angles

All angles which do not have the line segment GH as one of their arms are called exterior angles, as they lie outside the lines AB and CD.

### 3. Alternate interior angles

The interior angles which lie on the opposite sides of the transversal and do not form a linear pair are called alternate interior angles. In Fig. 9.18, the two pairs of alternate interior angles are ∠4 and ∠6, and ∠3 and ∠5.

### 4. Alternate exterior angles

The exterior angles which lie on the opposite sides of the transversal and do not form a linear pair are called alternate exterior angles. In Fig. 9.18, the two pairs of alternate exterior angles are ∠1 and ∠7, and ∠2 and ∠8.

### 5. Corresponding angles

A pair of angles on the same side of the transversal, one of which is an interior angle and the other one is an exterior angle, but do not form a linear pair, is known as a pair of corresponding angles. In Fig. 9.18, the pairs of corresponding angles are ∠1 and ∠5, ∠2 and ∠6, ∠4 and ∠8, and ∠3 and ∠7.

### 6. Co-interior angles

A pair of interior angles on the same side of the transversal is called co-interior angles. In Fig. 9.18, ∠4 and ∠5, and ∠3 and ∠6 are pairs of co-interior angles.

## Angles Formed by a Transversal with Parallel Lines

We have already learnt about parallel lines in previous classes. Parallel lines are the lines that do not intersect each other.

When a transversal intersects two parallel lines, the eight angles that are formed have special properties in relation to each other. In Fig. 9.19, two parallel lines AB and CD, intersected by a transversal EF at points G and H, form eight

# Angles

Let us learn the special properties associated with these angles.

1. $\angle 3 = \angle 5$ and $\angle 4 = \angle 6$
   - Alternate interior angles are equal.

2. $\angle 2 = \angle 8$ and $\angle 1 = \angle 7$
   - Alternate exterior angles are equal.

3. $\angle 1 = \angle 5$, $\angle 2 = \angle 6$, $\angle 4 = \angle 8$ and $\angle 3 = \angle 7$
   - Corresponding angles are equal.

4. $\angle 4 + \angle 5 = 180^\circ$ and $\angle 3 + \angle 6 = 180^\circ$
   - Co-interior angles are supplementary.

The above properties can be verified by measuring the angle using a protractor.

Thus, we can conclude that when parallel lines are intersected by a transversal, then
1. the alternate angles are equal.
2. the corresponding angles are equal.
3. the sum of co-interior angles is $180^\circ$.

## Converse of the property

Conversely, if any one of the above three given conditions is satisfied when two lines are intersected by a transversal, then the two lines must be parallel to each other.

#### Example 6: In Fig. 9.22, $p \parallel q$ and $l$ is a transversal. Find the measure of angles $a$, $b$, $c$, $d$, $e$ and $f$.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing parallel lines $p$ and $q$ intersected by transversal $l$. Angles $a$, $b$, $c$, $d$, $e$, and $f$ are labeled. Angle $e$ is given as $125^\circ$. [IMAGE_END]

Solution: It is given that \( p \parallel q \) and \( l \) is a transversal.

125° = ∠d (Corresponding angles)

125° + ∠f = 180° (linear pair)

⇒ ∠f = 180° - 125° = 55°

∠f = ∠a (Alternate interior angles)

⇒ ∠a = 55°

∠a = ∠e (Corresponding angles)

⇒ ∠e = 55°

∠b = ∠d (Vertically opposite angles)

⇒ ∠b = 125°

∠a = ∠c (Vertically opposite angles)

⇒ ∠c = 55°

Thus, ∠e = ∠f = ∠a = ∠c = 55° and ∠b = ∠d = 125°.

Example 7: In Fig. 9.23, \( a \parallel b \) with \( t \) as transversal. Find the value of \( x \).

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two parallel lines \( a \) and \( b \) intersected by a transversal \( t \). Angles labeled as (6x + 7°) and (3x + 38°) are shown. [IMAGE_END]

Solution: Given that \( a \parallel b \) and \( t \) is the transversal.

(6x + 7°) and (3x + 38°) are interior angles on the same side of the transversal \( t \).

(6x + 7°) + (3x + 38°) = 180°

⇒ 9x + 45° = 180°

⇒ 9x = 180° - 45° = 135°

⇒ x = \(\frac{135°}{9}\) = 15°

Thus, the value of \( x \) is 15°.

Example 8: In Fig. 9.24, check whether line \( l \) is parallel to line \( m \) or not.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two lines \( l \) and \( m \) intersected by a transversal \( n \). Angles labeled as 126° and 44° are shown. [IMAGE_END]

Solution: Given that \( l \) and \( m \) are two lines and \( n \) is a transversal.

126° and 44° are angles on the same side of the transversal such that

126° + 44° = 170° ≠ 180°

Since, the sum of interior angles on the same side of the transversal is not 180°, so the lines cannot be parallel.

Thus, lines \( l \) and \( m \) are not parallel.

- Two angles are said to be complementary, if the sum of their measures is 90°.
