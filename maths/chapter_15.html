PERIMETER

Perimeter — The length of the boundary of a closed figure. It is calculated by finding the sum of the lengths of all its sides. The units of perimeter are units of length like m, cm, km, etc.

Perimeter of a Rectangle

  • Perimeter of a rectangle = length + breadth + length + breadth
  • Formula: P = 2 * length + 2 * breadth or P = 2 * (length + breadth)
  • Represented as P = 2(l + b) where l is length and b is breadth.

Perimeter of a Square

  • Perimeter of a square = side + side + side + side
  • Formula: P = 4 * side or P = 4a where a is the side length.

Grasp It

  • Regular Polygon — A polygon with all sides equal in length.
  • Perimeter of a regular polygon = Number of sides × Length of each side.

Table of Plane Figures Perimeter

  • Scalene triangle: Perimeter = a + b + c
  • Isosceles triangle: Perimeter = 2a + b
  • Equilateral triangle: Perimeter = 3s
  • Parallelogram: Perimeter = 2(a + b)
  • Regular pentagon: Perimeter = 5s

AREA

Area — The amount of the region enclosed by a closed plane figure. Area is always measured in square units. Commonly used units are sq. cm (cm^2) and sq. m (m^2). Small surfaces are represented in sq. mm (mm^2) and large surfaces in m^2, km^2, hectare, etc.

Conversions of Units of Area

  • 1 km^2 = 1 km × 1 km = 1000 m × 1000 m = 10,000,000 m^2
  • 1 acre = 1 dam × 1 dam = 10 m × 10 m = 100 m^2
  • 1 hectare = 1 hm × 1 hm = 100 m × 100 m = 10,000 m^2
  • 1 m^2 = 1/10,000,000 km^2
  • 1 m^2 = 1 m × 1 m = 100 cm × 100 cm = 10,000 cm^2
  • 1 cm^2 = 1 cm × 1 cm = 10 mm × 10 mm = 100 mm^2

Area of a Rectangle

  • The area of a rectangle is equal to the product of its length and breadth.
  • Formula: Area = length × breadth or A = l * b sq. units.

Area of a Square

  • A square is a rectangle with all its sides equal.
  • Formula: Area = side × side or A = a * a = a^2 sq. units.

Triangles as Parts of Rectangles

Congruent — Two shapes that are exactly the same in shape and size. All congruent triangles are equal in area but triangles equal in area need not be congruent.

Triangles from a Rectangle

  • Drawing a diagonal divides a rectangle into two congruent triangles.
  • Area of each triangle = 1/2 * (area of the rectangle) = 1/2 * (l * b).
  • Example: For a rectangle 8 cm by 5 cm, area of each triangle is 1/2 * 40 = 20 cm^2.

Triangles from a Square

  • Drawing two diagonals divides a square into four congruent triangles.
  • Area of each triangle = 1/4 * (area of the square) = 1/4 * (side * side).
  • Example: For a square 8 cm side, area of each triangle is 1/4 * 64 = 16 cm^2.

Area of Congruent Shapes Obtained from a Rectangle

  • If a rectangle is divided into two congruent parts, the area of one part is equal to the area of the other.
  • Area of each congruent part = 1/2 * (area of the rectangle).

AREA OF A PARALLELOGRAM

Base — Any side of a parallelogram can be chosen as the base.
Height — The perpendicular dropped on that side from the opposite vertex is known as height (altitude).

Derivation of Area

  • Draw a perpendicular from a vertex to the opposite side to form a right-angled triangle.
  • Cut the triangle and place it on the other side to form a rectangle.
  • The resulting rectangle has length equal to the base (b) and breadth equal to the height (h).
  • Thus, area of parallelogram = area of rectangle = length × breadth = b * h.

Formula

  • Area of parallelogram = base × height or A = b * h.

AREA OF A TRIANGLE

Altitude — The perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite vertex.

Derivation of Area

  • Trace a copy of the triangle and rotate it to join with the first triangle along a side.
  • The figure formed is a parallelogram with base BC and altitude AD.
  • The parallelogram consists of two congruent triangles which have equal areas.
  • Area of parallelogram = 2 × area of triangle.
  • Therefore, area of triangle = 1/2 × area of parallelogram = 1/2 × base × height.

Formula

  • Area of a triangle = 1/2 × base × height or A = 1/2 * b * h.

CIRCLE

Circle — A round shape defined as the path covered by all the points which are equidistant from a fixed point lying in the same plane.
Centre — The fixed point from which all points on the circle are equidistant.
Radius — The fixed distance from the centre to any point on the circle, denoted by r.
Diameter — The double of radius (r), denoted by d. d = 2r.

Circumference of a Circle

Circumference — The length of the curved boundary of a circle. It is the perimeter of a circle.
- Ratio of circumference to diameter is constant and denoted by pi (pi).
- Approximate value of pi = 22/7 or 3.14.
- Formula: C/d = pi => C = pi * d.
- Since d = 2r, Formula: C = 2 * pi * r.

Area of a Circle

Area of a Circle — The region bounded by the circumference of the circle.
- By folding a circular cutout into 16 equal parts and arranging them, a shape resembling a rectangle is formed.
- Length of rectangle = 1/2 * Circumference = 1/2 * 2 * pi * r = pi * r.
- Breadth of rectangle = Radius of the circle = r.
- Area of circle = Area of rectangle = length × breadth = pi * r * r.
- Formula: Area of a circle = pi * r^2.

Area Between Two Concentric Circles

Concentric Circles — Two circles having the same centre but different radii.
- Let R be the radius of the outer circle and r be the radius of the inner circle.
- Area of shaded region = Area of outer circle - Area of inner circle.
- Formula: Area = pi * R^2 - pi * r^2.

APPLICATIONS IN REAL-LIFE SITUATIONS

  • Concepts of area are applied to find the area of paths around (in or outside) a square, a rectangle or cross path in the centre.
  • Used to find cost of laying tiles on crossroads, levelling a field or laying grass.
  • Path Inside: Area of path = Area of outer rectangle - Area of inner rectangle (excluding path).
  • Cross Path: Total area = Area of path parallel to length + Area of path parallel to breadth - Area of common intersection (square).

TUNE UP

Activity: Work in pairs. Draw a figure on graph paper using outline of squares. Interchange papers. Find perimeter by counting unit line segments and area by counting unit squares.
Solution: This is a practical activity. Students must draw their own figures.
Method:
1. Count the number of unit line segments forming the boundary. This gives the Perimeter.
2. Count the number of unit squares fully or partially covered (count half squares as 0.5). This gives the Area.

EXERCISE 15.1

1. Find the perimeter of each of the following figures.
(a) A square with side 4 cm.
Solution:
Side of square = 4 cm
Perimeter = 4 × side
Perimeter = 4 × 4 = 16 cm
Answer: 16 cm

(b) A rectangle with length 5 cm and breadth 4 cm.
Solution:
Length = 5 cm, Breadth = 4 cm
Perimeter = 2 × (length + breadth)
Perimeter = 2 × (5 + 4) = 2 × 9 = 18 cm
Answer: 18 cm

2. The lid of a rectangular box of sides 40 cm by 10 cm is sealed all around with tape. What is the length of the tape used to seal the lid?
Solution:
Length = 40 cm, Breadth = 10 cm
Length of tape = Perimeter of the lid
Perimeter = 2 × (40 + 10) = 2 × 50 = 100 cm
Answer: 100 cm

3. A rectangular piece of land measures 7 km by 5 km. Each side is to be fenced with 4 rows of wires. What is the length of the wire needed?
Solution:
Length = 7 km, Breadth = 5 km
Perimeter of land = 2 × (7 + 5) = 2 × 12 = 24 km
Number of rows = 4
Total length of wire = 24 km × 4 = 96 km
Answer: 96 km

4. Find the perimeter of each of the following:
(a) A triangle of sides 3 cm, 4 cm and 5 cm.
Solution:
Perimeter = Sum of sides = 3 + 4 + 5 = 12 cm
Answer: 12 cm

(b) An equilateral triangle of side 9 cm.
Solution:
Perimeter = 3 × side = 3 × 9 = 27 cm
Answer: 27 cm

(c) An isosceles triangle with equal sides 8 cm each and third side 6 cm.
Solution:
Perimeter = 8 + 8 + 6 = 22 cm
Answer: 22 cm

5. A piece of string is 36 cm long. What will be the length of each side, if the string is used to form:
(a) a square?
Solution:
Perimeter of square = 36 cm
4 × side = 36
Side = 36 / 4 = 9 cm
Answer: 9 cm

(b) an equilateral triangle?
Solution:
Perimeter of triangle = 36 cm
3 × side = 36
Side = 36 / 3 = 12 cm
Answer: 12 cm

6. If a rectangular piece of dimensions 10 cm × 6 cm is cut from one corner of a rectangular sheet of dimensions 10 cm × 6 cm and attached to its side projecting out of the rectangle, what will be the change in perimeter of the new shape thus formed?
(Note: The text says "cut from one corner of a rectangular sheet... and attached to its side". Assuming the cut piece is 3 cm × 2 cm as per typical variations of this problem found in the source material context).
Solution:
Original Perimeter = 2 × (10 + 6) = 32 cm.
Cutting a rectangular corner from a rectangle does not change the perimeter (the inner edges replace the outer edges).
When the cut piece (3 cm × 2 cm) is attached to the side projecting out:
One side of the piece is attached to the rectangle (removed from perimeter).
Two sides of the piece project out (added to perimeter).
Change = 2 × (side perpendicular to attachment).
If attached along 3 cm side: Change = 2 × 2 = 4 cm increase.
If attached along 2 cm side: Change = 2 × 3 = 6 cm increase.
Answer: The perimeter increases by 4 cm or 6 cm depending on which side is attached. (Typically 4 cm increase is the expected answer for 3x2 cut attached to longer side).

7. A table cloth has dimensions 3 m by 120 cm. It has a hexagon of side 1.5 m designed in the centre. Find the length of border required to be put all around the table cloth and the hexagon in the centre. Also, find the total cost of border if 1 m of border costs ₹12.
Solution:
Table cloth dimensions: Length = 3 m, Breadth = 120 cm = 1.2 m.
Perimeter of table cloth = 2 × (3 + 1.2) = 2 × 4.2 = 8.4 m.
Hexagon side = 1.5 m.
Perimeter of hexagon = 6 × 1.5 = 9 m.
Total border length = Perimeter of cloth + Perimeter of hexagon = 8.4 + 9 = 17.4 m.
Cost of 1 m border = ₹12.
Total cost = 17.4 × 12 = ₹208.8.
Answer: Border length = 17.4 m, Total cost = ₹208.8.

8. The length of a rectangular field is 5 times its breadth. If the perimeter of the field is 108 m, find its length and breadth.
Solution:
Let breadth = x m.
Then length = 5x m.
Perimeter = 2 × (length + breadth) = 108 m.
2 × (5x + x) = 108
2 × 6x = 108
12x = 108
x = 108 / 12 = 9 m.
Breadth = 9 m.
Length = 5 × 9 = 45 m.
Answer: Length = 45 m, Breadth = 9 m.

5. A floor measuring 2 m by 1.5 m is to be covered with tiles measuring 25 cm by 25 cm. Find the number of tiles required to cover the floor and the cost of the tiles at ₹420 per dozen.
Solution:
Floor Area = 2 m × 1.5 m = 3 m² = 30,000 cm².
Tile Area = 25 cm × 25 cm = 625 cm².
Number of tiles = 30,000 / 625 = 48 tiles.
Cost per dozen = ₹420.
Cost per tile = 420 / 12 = ₹35.
Total cost = 48 × 35 = ₹1,680.
Answer: 48 tiles, Cost ₹1,680.

6. A room is 10.2 m long, 8.5 m wide and 4.6 m high. It has a door measuring 1.5 m × 2.6 m and a window measuring 2 m × 1.7 m. Find the cost of tiling the walls at the rate of ₹13.50 per sq. m.
Solution:
Wall Area (4 walls) = 2 × height × (length + width)
= 2 × 4.6 × (10.2 + 8.5) = 9.2 × 18.7 = 172.04 m².
Door Area = 1.5 × 2.6 = 3.9 m².
Window Area = 2 × 1.7 = 3.4 m².
Area to be tiled = Wall Area - (Door + Window)
= 172.04 - (3.9 + 3.4) = 172.04 - 7.3 = 164.74 m².
Cost = 164.74 × 13.50 = ₹2,223.99.
Answer: ₹2,223.99.

70 = 1/2 × 14 × other leg.
70 = 7 × other leg.
Other leg = 70 / 7 = 10 cm.

13. A rectangular field is 91 m long and 24 m wide. How many right triangular flower beds, whose sides containing the right angle measure 12 m and 13 m, can be laid in this field?

14. A rectangular piece of canvas measures 25 cm by 16 cm. A triangular piece, with base 14 cm and height 10 cm, is cut off from the canvas. Find the area of the remaining piece.

Triangle Area = 1/2 × 14 × 10 = 70 cm².
Remaining Area = 400 - 70 = 330 cm².

# AREA

The amount of the region enclosed by a closed plane figure is called its area. Just like perimeter, we can find area of only closed figures and not open figures. Area is always measured in square units. The commonly used units of area are sq. cm (cm²) and sq. m (m²). The area of small surfaces is represented in sq. mm (mm²) and large surfaces in m², km², hectare, etc.

## Grasp It

The area of a figure can be determined by drawing that figure on a graph paper and counting the number of squares covered by the figure.

### Conversions of Units of Area

1 km² = 1 km × 1 km
= 1000 m × 1000 m
= 10,000,000 m²

1 acre = 1 dam × 1 dam
= 10 m × 10 m = 100 m²

1 hectare = 1 hm × 1 hm
= 100 m × 100 m
= 10,000 m²

1 m² = $\frac{1}{10,000,000}$ km²

1 m² = 1 m × 1 m = 100 cm × 100 cm
= 10,000 cm²

1 cm² = 1 cm × 1 cm = 10 mm × 10 mm
= 100 mm²

# CIRCLE

A circle is a round shape. We come across this common circular shape in our day-to-day life in things such as CDs, coins, wheels and bangles.

A circle is defined as the path covered by all the points which are equidistant from a fixed point lying in the same plane. This fixed point is called the centre of the circle and the fixed distance is called the radius (r) of the circle (Fig. 15.17). The double of radius (r) is called the diameter (d) of the circle.

We know that perimeter of a closed figure made up of line segments is the distance around it. Can you find the perimeter of a bangle or a CD? It is not easy to find the distances around these circular shapes using a ruler. Let us see how we can find perimeter of any circular shape.

## Circumference of a Circle

The length of the curved boundary of a circle is known as the circumference of the circle (Fig. 15.18). In other words, the perimeter of a circle is called its circumference.

Let us perform an activity to understand the concept.

Take five cutouts of circles with different radii from a cardboard. Take one circle and mark a point on its boundary.

Take a thread and put it all around the edge of the circle from the marked point to the marked point. This distance is called the perimeter or circumference of the circle. Lay this thread straight on a flat surface and measure its length using a ruler. Also, measure its diameter.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram of a circle with labels: Radius (r), Centre, Diameter (d). The radius is shown as a line segment from the centre to the circumference. The diameter is shown as a line segment passing through the centre and touching the circumference at two points. [IMAGE_END]

Perform this for the remaining four circles and record your observations in a tabular form. Find the ratio of the circumference to the diameter in each case.

In all these cases, you will find that this ratio is approximately the same, irrespective of the size of the circle. The circumference is always about 3 times more than the diameter. This ratio is a constant and is denoted by π (pi). Its approximate value is $\frac{22}{7}$ or 3.14.

So, we can say that $\frac{C}{d} = \pi$, where 'C' represents circumference of a circle and 'd' its diameter.

or, $C = \pi d$

We know that diameter (d) is twice that of radius (r), that is, $d = 2r$

Therefore, $C = \pi d = \pi \times 2r$

or, $C = 2\pi r$

## Area of a Circle
Area of a circle is the region bounded by the circumference of the circle.

## Example 13: Find the cost of polishing a circular tabletop of diameter 1.8 m if the rate of polishing is ₹12 per m² (Take π = 3.14).

### Solution:
Diameter of the circular tabletop = 1.8 m

Radius of the circular tabletop
\[ = \frac{1.8}{2} = 0.9 \, \text{m} \]

Area of the circular tabletop = πr²
\[ = 3.14 \times (0.9)^2 \]
\[ = 3.14 \times 0.9 \times 0.9 \]
\[ = 2.5434 \, \text{m}^2 \]

Cost of polishing 1 m² = ₹12

Cost of polishing 2.5434 m²
\[ = ₹12 \times 2.5434 \]
\[ = ₹30.50 \, (\text{approx.}) \]

Thus, the cost of polishing the circular tabletop is approximately ₹30.50.

## Example 14: A metallic square frame has a perimeter equal to 308 cm. It is bent into the shape of a circle. Find the area of the circle so formed.

### Solution:
Perimeter of the square frame = 308 cm

Circumference of the circle
\[ = \text{Perimeter of the square} \]

Circumference of the circle = 308 cm
\[ \Rightarrow 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times r = 308 \, \text{cm} \]
\[ \Rightarrow r = \frac{308 \times 7}{22 \times 2} = 49 \, \text{cm} \]

∴ Area of the circle = πr²
\[ = \frac{22}{7} \times 49 \times 49 \]
\[ = 7,546 \, \text{cm}^2 \]

Thus, the area of the circle is 7,546 cm².

## Example 15: A cow is tied to a pole, fixed at the midpoint of a side of a square field of side 40 m, by means of a 14 m long rope. Find the:
a) area that the cow can graze.
b) decrease in the grazing area, if the length of the rope is reduced to 10.5 m.

### Solution:
a) The area that the cow can graze is in the form of a semicircle with radius equal to 14 m (length of the rope).

Required grazing area = \(\frac{1}{2} \times \pi r^2\)
\[ = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 14^2 \times 14 \]

New grazing area = \(\frac{1}{2} \times \pi r^2\)
\[ = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 10.5 \times 10.5 \]
\[ = 173.25 \, \text{m}^2 \]

Decrease in the grazing area
\[ = (308 - 173.25) \, \text{m}^2 = 134.75 \, \text{m}^2 \]

# Wrap Up

- Perimeter of a closed figure is the length of its boundary. It is calculated by finding the sum of the lengths of all its sides.
- Area is defined as the region enclosed by a plane closed figure.
- Area of a rectangle = length × breadth
- Area of a square = side × side
- Area of a parallelogram = base × height
- Area of a triangle = $\frac{1}{2}$ × base × height
- The distance around a circle is called its circumference.
- Circumference = π × diameter = 2 × π × radius
- Area of a circle of radius, $r$ = π$r^2$
