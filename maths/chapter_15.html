Perimeter

  • The perimeter of a closed figure is the total length of its boundary.
  • It is found by adding the lengths of all its sides.
  • Units: cm, m, km, etc.

Perimeter of Common Shapes

Shape Formula
Rectangle 2 × (length + breadth)
Square 4 × side
Triangle (scalene) a + b + c
Triangle (isosceles) 2a + b
Triangle (equilateral) 3s
Parallelogram 2(a + b)
Regular polygon Number of sides × length of one side

Example: A complex polygon with sides 1, 1, 6, 1, 1, 2, 1, 1, 6, 1, 1, 2 → Perimeter = 24 m

Area

  • The area is the region enclosed by a closed plane figure.
  • Measured in square units: cm², m², mm², km², hectare, acre.
  • 1 hectare = 10,000 m²
    1 acre = 100 m²
    1 m² = 10,000 cm²
    1 km² = 10,000,000 m²

Area of Rectangles and Squares

  • Rectangle: Area = length × breadth
  • Square: Area = side × side = $a^2$

Example: A wall of 18 m × 12 m has a door of 3.5 m × 1.2 m.
Area to be painted = (18 × 12) – (3.5 × 1.2) = 216 – 4.2 = 211.8 m²

Area of a Parallelogram

  • Area = base × height
  • Height is the perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite side.

Example: Base = 4.2 m, height = 2.5 m → Area = 4.2 × 2.5 = 10.5 m²

Area of a Triangle

  • Area = $\frac{1}{2} \times$ base × height
  • Triangles formed by cutting a rectangle or square along a diagonal have equal area.

Example: Base = 8 cm, height = 5 cm → Area = $\frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times 5 = 20$ cm²

Example: Base = 7 cm, height = 6 cm → Area = $\frac{1}{2} \times 7 \times 6 = 21$ cm²
If base = 14 cm, area = 21 cm² → height = $\frac{2 \times 21}{14} = 3$ cm

Circle

  • A circle is a round shape where all points are equidistant from the center.
  • Circumference = distance around the circle
    → $C = \pi d = 2\pi r$
    → $\pi \approx \frac{22}{7}$ or 3.14

Example: Radius = 7 cm → Circumference = $2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 7 = 44$ cm

  • Area of a circle = $\pi r^2$

Example: Diameter = 1.8 m → Radius = 0.9 m → Area = $3.14 \times 0.9^2 = 2.5434$ m²

Area Between Two Concentric Circles

  • Area of shaded region = $\pi R^2 - \pi r^2 = \pi (R^2 - r^2)$

    Example: Outer radius = 20 cm, inner radius = 16 cm
    Area = $3.14 \times (20^2 - 16^2) = 3.14 \times (400 - 256) = 3.14 \times 144 = 452.16$ cm²

Area of Paths Around Rectangles

  • Paths inside or around a rectangle are found by subtracting the area of the inner region from the outer.
  • For cross paths: Total area = (area of path parallel to length) + (area of path parallel to breadth) – (area of intersection)

Example: A 3 m wide path inside a 90 m × 70 m field
Area of path = (90 × 70) – ((90 – 6) × (70 – 6)) = 6300 – 5376 = 924 m²

Real-Life Applications

  • Cost of tiling a floor
  • Cost of fencing a field
  • Cost of laying grass or stones
  • Area of a path around a garden or park
  • Area covered by a cow tied to a pole (semicircle)

Example: A cow tied to a pole with 14 m rope → Grazing area = $\frac{1}{2} \pi r^2 = 308$ m²

Key Terms

Area — the amount of region enclosed by a closed plane figure
Perimeter — the length of the boundary of a closed figure
Rectangle — a four-sided figure with opposite sides equal and all angles right angles
Square — a rectangle with all sides equal
Parallelogram — a four-sided figure with opposite sides parallel and equal
Triangle — a three-sided figure
Circle — a round shape defined as the path of points equidistant from a fixed point (centre)
Radius — the distance from the centre of a circle to any point on its boundary
Diameter — the distance across a circle passing through its centre, equal to twice the radius
Circumference — the length of the curved boundary of a circle, also known as the perimeter of a circle
Height — the perpendicular distance from a base to the opposite vertex or side in a triangle or parallelogram
Base — any side of a figure used as a reference to calculate area or height
Unit of area — square units such as cm², m², mm², km², hectare
Conversion of units — changing one unit of area to another (e.g., 1 m² = 10,000 cm²)
Hectare — a unit of area equal to 10,000 m²
Acre — a unit of area equal to 100 m²
Congruent shapes — shapes that are exactly the same in shape and size
Area of a rectangle — length multiplied by breadth
Area of a square — side multiplied by side
Area of a parallelogram — base multiplied by height
Area of a triangle — half the product of base and height
Area of a circle — π times radius squared (πr²)
Circumference of a circle — π times diameter or 2π times radius
Concentric circles — circles with the same centre but different radii
Path around a rectangle — a strip of land or area running inside or outside a rectangular shape
Cross path — a path crossing through a field or garden, often at right angles
Real-life applications — using perimeter and area in daily situations like fencing, tiling, or construction
Unit conversion — changing between units like km² to m² or cm² to mm²

Exercise 15.1

1. Find the perimeter of each of the following figures.

a) Rectangle of dimensions 4 cm × 4 cm
Perimeter = 2 × (length + breadth) = 2 × (4 + 4) = 2 × 8 = 16 cm

b) Rectangle of dimensions 4 cm × 5 cm
Perimeter = 2 × (4 + 5) = 2 × 9 = 18 cm

2. The lid of a rectangular box of sides 40 cm by 10 cm is sealed all around with tape. What is the length of the tape used to seal the lid?
Perimeter of the lid = 2 × (40 + 10) = 2 × 50 = 100 cm

3. A rectangular piece of land measures 7 km by 5 km. Each side is to be fenced with 4 rows of wires. What is the length of the wire needed?
Perimeter of the land = 2 × (7 + 5) = 2 × 12 = 24 km
Length of wire for 4 rows = 4 × 24 = 96 km

4. Find the perimeter of each of the following:

a) Triangle with sides 3 cm, 4 cm, 5 cm
Perimeter = 3 + 4 + 5 = 12 cm

b) Equilateral triangle of side 9 cm
Perimeter = 3 × 9 = 27 cm

c) Isosceles triangle with equal sides 8 cm and third side 6 cm
Perimeter = 8 + 8 + 6 = 22 cm

5. A piece of string is 36 cm long. What will be the length of each side, if the string is used to form:

a) A square?
Side = 36 ÷ 4 = 9 cm

b) An equilateral triangle?
Side = 36 ÷ 3 = 12 cm

6. If a rectangular piece of dimensions 3 cm × 2 cm is cut from one corner of a rectangular sheet of dimensions 10 cm × 6 cm and attached to its side projecting out of the rectangle, what will be the change in perimeter of the new shape thus formed?

Original perimeter = 2 × (10 + 6) = 2 × 16 = 32 cm
After cutting and attaching:
- Two sides of 3 cm and 2 cm are removed from the original boundary.
- Two new sides of 3 cm and 2 cm are added.
- Net change = 0 cm
So, perimeter remains unchanged → No change

7. A table cloth has dimensions 3 m by 120 cm. It has a hexagon of side 1.5 m designed in the centre. Find the length of border required to be put all around the table cloth and the hexagon in the centre. Also, find the total cost of border if 1 m of border costs ₹12.

Convert all units to meters:
Table cloth = 3 m × 1.2 m
Perimeter of table cloth = 2 × (3 + 1.2) = 2 × 4.2 = 8.4 m
Hexagon side = 1.5 m
Perimeter of hexagon = 6 × 1.5 = 9 m
Border required = Perimeter of table cloth + Perimeter of hexagon = 8.4 + 9 = 17.4 m
Cost = 17.4 × 12 = ₹208.80
Total border length = 17.4 m, Total cost = ₹208.80

8. The length of a rectangular field is 5 times its breadth. If the perimeter of the field is 108 m, find its length and breadth.

Let breadth = x m
Then length = 5x m
Perimeter = 2 × (l + b) = 2 × (5x + x) = 2 × 6x = 12x
12x = 108 → x = 9
Breadth = 9 m, Length = 5 × 9 = 45 m

# AREA

The amount of the region enclosed by a closed plane figure is called its area. Just like perimeter, we can find area of only closed figures and not open figures. Area is always measured in square units. The commonly used units of area are sq. cm (cm²) and sq. m (m²). The area of small surfaces is represented in sq. mm (mm²) and large surfaces in m², km², hectare, etc.

## Grasp It

The area of a figure can be determined by drawing that figure on a graph paper and counting the number of squares covered by the figure.

= 10 m × 10 m = 100 m²

= 100 m × 100 m

1 m² = $\frac{1}{10,000,000}$ km²

1 cm² = 1 cm × 1 cm = 10 mm × 10 mm

# CIRCLE

A circle is a round shape. We come across this common circular shape in our day-to-day life in things such as CDs, coins, wheels and bangles.

A circle is defined as the path covered by all the points which are equidistant from a fixed point lying in the same plane. This fixed point is called the centre of the circle and the fixed distance is called the radius (r) of the circle (Fig. 15.17). The double of radius (r) is called the diameter (d) of the circle.

We know that perimeter of a closed figure made up of line segments is the distance around it. Can you find the perimeter of a bangle or a CD? It is not easy to find the distances around these circular shapes using a ruler. Let us see how we can find perimeter of any circular shape.

## Circumference of a Circle

The length of the curved boundary of a circle is known as the circumference of the circle (Fig. 15.18). In other words, the perimeter of a circle is called its circumference.

Let us perform an activity to understand the concept.

Take five cutouts of circles with different radii from a cardboard. Take one circle and mark a point on its boundary.

Take a thread and put it all around the edge of the circle from the marked point to the marked point. This distance is called the perimeter or circumference of the circle. Lay this thread straight on a flat surface and measure its length using a ruler. Also, measure its diameter.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram of a circle with labels: Radius (r), Centre, Diameter (d). The radius is shown as a line segment from the centre to the circumference. The diameter is shown as a line segment passing through the centre and touching the circumference at two points. [IMAGE_END]

Perform this for the remaining four circles and record your observations in a tabular form. Find the ratio of the circumference to the diameter in each case.

In all these cases, you will find that this ratio is approximately the same, irrespective of the size of the circle. The circumference is always about 3 times more than the diameter. This ratio is a constant and is denoted by π (pi). Its approximate value is $\frac{22}{7}$ or 3.14.

So, we can say that $\frac{C}{d} = \pi$, where 'C' represents circumference of a circle and 'd' its diameter.

or, $C = \pi d$

We know that diameter (d) is twice that of radius (r), that is, $d = 2r$

Therefore, $C = \pi d = \pi \times 2r$

or, $C = 2\pi r$

Cost of constructing 10 m² path = ₹20
Cost of constructing 1 m² path = ₹20 ÷ 10 = ₹2
Cost of constructing 924 m² path = ₹2 × 924 = ₹1,848

# Wrap Up

- Perimeter of a closed figure is the length of its boundary. It is calculated by finding the sum of the lengths of all its sides.
- Area is defined as the region enclosed by a plane closed figure.
- Area of a rectangle = length × breadth
- Area of a square = side × side
- Area of a parallelogram = base × height
- Area of a triangle = $\frac{1}{2}$ × base × height
- The distance around a circle is called its circumference.
- Circumference = π × diameter = 2 × π × radius
- Area of a circle of radius, $r$ = π$r^2$
