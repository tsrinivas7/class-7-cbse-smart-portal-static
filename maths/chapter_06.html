Chapter 06

Algebraic Expressions

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduces the generalised form of mathematics using letters and numbers. It focuses on understanding patterns, forming rules, and performing operations on algebraic expressions.

Learning Objectives

The main goals of this chapter are to:
- Generate algebraic expressions from patterns.
- State the meaning of algebraic expressions.
- Define and identify constants, coefficients, powers, like and unlike terms.
- Explain different types of algebraic expressions.
- State the degree of a polynomial.
- Add and subtract algebraic expressions.
- Find the value of algebraic expressions.

Introduction and Generalisation

Algebra is a generalised form of Mathematics which uses letters along with numbers.
- Variable or Literal — Letters such as ( a, b, c, x, y, z ) that represent unknown quantities.
- Constant — Numbers like 1, 2, 3 whose value remains constant wherever they are placed.
- The most fruitful use of algebra is in generalisation, where patterns are converted into rules. For example, the number of bindis in the nth square follows the rule ( n^2 ).

Generating Algebraic Expressions

While solving arithmetic problems, we come across many patterns and formulae which are the generalised form of many problems.
- Algebraic expressions are generated using variables and constants combined with fundamental operations.
- Patterns help in forming general rules for specific situations, such as finding the cost of pastries or matchsticks required for a pattern.
- Expressions are formed by combining variables with themselves, other variables, or constants.

Algebraic Expressions

A combination of constants and variables connected by the signs of fundamental mathematical operations (+, -, × or ÷) is called an algebraic expression.
- Algebraic Expression — A combination of constants and variables connected by +, -, ×, or ÷. Examples: 4m + 7, 3y - 8, x/2 - 3.
- Expressions can be formed by:
- Combining variables with themselves (e.g., ( x * x = x^2 )).
- Combining variables with other variables (e.g., ( x + y ) or ( x * y * z )).
- Combining constants and variables (e.g., ( 2 * y * z )).

Terms of an Algebraic Expression

Terms are added to form algebraic expressions.
- Term — A part of an algebraic expression separated by + or - signs. For example, in ( 3x - 5 ), ( 3x ) and ( -5 ) are two terms.
- A term can be:
- A constant (e.g., 2, -33).
- A variable (e.g., a, b, x).
- Product of a constant and variables (e.g., 3x, 1/2 ab).
- Product of variables (e.g., xy, ( x^3y^5 )).
- Always include the '-' sign with the negative term when identifying terms.

Factors of a Term

A term is a product of its factors.
- Factor — Numbers or variables multiplied together to form a term. For example, in ( 5x^2 ), the factors are 5, x, and x.
- Factors can be represented conveniently by a tree diagram showing the breakdown of terms into components.
- The term ( -4xy ) is a product of the factors -4, x, and y.

Coefficients

A term generally consists of one numerical factor, the rest being variables (algebraic factors).
- Coefficient — The numerical factor of a term. For example, in ( 7ab^2 ), 7 is the coefficient.
- Numerical Coefficient — Specifically the constant factor of a term.
- Relative Coefficient — Any one of the factors in a term is called the coefficient of the product of the other factors. For example, in ( 7ab^2 ), 'a' is the coefficient of ( 7b^2 ).
- When the coefficient is +1, it is usually omitted (e.g., ( 1x^2y ) is written as ( x^2y )).
- When the coefficient is -1, it is represented only by the minus sign (e.g., ( -x^2y )).

Types of Algebraic Expressions

Algebraic expressions are classified based on the number of terms they have.
- Monomial — An algebraic expression which contains only one term. Examples: 5m, 6pq, -7.
- Binomial — An algebraic expression which contains two unlike terms. Examples: 5a + 3b, 5x - 6y. Note: ( 2t + 6t ) is not a binomial because terms are like.
- Trinomial — An algebraic expression which contains three unlike terms. Examples: a + b + c, 3m - 2n + 4p.
- Polynomial — An algebraic expression with one or more terms. Thus, monomials, binomials, and trinomials are all polynomials.
- A polynomial cannot have terms with negative exponents for any variable (e.g., ( 4x^2 + 3/x + 6 ) is not a polynomial).

Like and Unlike Terms

Terms are categorized based on their algebraic factors.
- Like Terms — Terms with the same algebraic factors. The numerical coefficient may be different. Example: 5xy and 3xy.
- Unlike Terms — Terms with different algebraic factors. Example: ( 2x^2y ) and ( -2xy^2 ).
- Only like terms can be added or subtracted.

Degree of a Polynomial

The highest power of a variable in a polynomial is called its degree.
- Degree — The highest power of a variable in a polynomial. For example, in ( a^2 + 6a + 4 ), the degree is 2.
- For a term containing more than one variable, the degree is obtained by adding the powers of all the variables in a term. Example: In ( -23x^2y ), the degree is 3 (2 + 1).
- The degree of a constant term is 0.
- Standard Form — Writing a polynomial in decreasing order of the powers of its terms.

Addition of Algebraic Expressions

Like numbers, we can also add algebraic expressions.
- To add two or more like terms, we add the numerical coefficients of all the like terms.
- Unlike terms cannot be added.
- Expressions including like and unlike terms are added by collecting different groups of like terms and adding them.
- Addition can be done horizontally or by arranging like terms in columns.
- The sum of two or more like terms is a like term with a numerical coefficient equal to the sum of the numerical coefficients of all the like terms.

Subtraction of Algebraic Expressions

Like addition, we can subtract only like terms.
- Subtracting a term from another term is same as adding its additive inverse to the other term.
- Additive Inverse — The opposite sign of a term used in subtraction (e.g., subtracting 3x is adding -3x).
- Steps for subtraction:
1. Arrange expressions horizontally with a minus sign between them.
2. While removing brackets, reverse the sign of all terms of the expression to be subtracted (- to + and + to -).
3. Rearrange terms to form groups of like terms.
4. Add or subtract the numerical coefficients of the like terms.
- The difference of two like terms is a like term with a numerical coefficient equal to the difference of the numerical coefficients.

Finding the Value of an Algebraic Expression

The value of an algebraic expression depends on the values of the variables forming the expression.
- We find the value by replacing the variables with given numbers.
- It is often helpful to simplify the algebraic expression by combining like terms before substituting values.
- Care must be taken with negative signs and powers when substituting values (e.g., ( (-4)^2 ) is 16, not -16).

Using Algebraic Expressions

In mathematics, formulae and rules can be written in generalised form using algebraic expressions.
- Various arithmetic problems can be compressed into a particular algebraic expression known as its formula.
- Infinite number of problems can be solved using a single algebraic formula.
- Examples of formulae:
- Area of rectangle = length * breadth (Area = l * b).
- Simple Interest (S.I.) = Principal * Rate * Time / 100.
- Conversion formulas (e.g., metres to decimetres: n = 10x).

Wrap Up

Key takeaways from the chapter include:
- An algebraic expression is formed when variables and constants are combined using one or more fundamental operations.
- An algebraic expression is made up of terms. Terms are added to make an expression.
- A term is the product of its factor. Factors containing variables are called algebraic factors.
- The coefficient of a term is the numerical factor of the term.
- Terms with the same algebraic factors are called like terms, while terms with different algebraic factors are called unlike terms.
- Any algebraic expression having one or more terms with variables whose powers are positive integers is called a polynomial.
- Specifically, a one-term expression is a monomial, a two-term expression is a binomial and a three-term expression is a trinomial.

Mental Maths Concepts

Important conceptual checks for students:
- If any variable in an algebraic expression appears in the denominator, then the algebraic expression is not a polynomial.
- All polynomials are algebraic expressions but all algebraic expressions are not polynomials.
- The value of an algebraic expression depends on the values of the variables involved in it.

- Aim — To add algebraic expressions using algebra tiles.
- Materials — Cardboard, two different colour sheets, scissors, glue, pencil.

- Combine similar shapes (like terms) and group additive inverse (zero pairs) together.

Key Terms

Algebraic Expression — A combination of constants and variables connected by fundamental mathematical operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, or division.
Binomial — An algebraic expression which contains exactly two unlike terms.
Coefficient — The numerical factor of a term in an algebraic expression.
Constant — A number whose value remains fixed and does not change in an expression.
Degree of a Polynomial — The highest power of a variable in a polynomial.
Factor — Any quantity that is multiplied to form a term in an algebraic expression.
Formula — A generalised rule written using algebraic expressions to represent arithmetic relationships.
Generalisation — The process of finding a general rule for patterns using algebraic expressions.
Like Terms — Terms that contain the same algebraic factors even if their numerical coefficients differ.
Literal — Letters used to represent unknown quantities or variables in algebra.
Monomial — An algebraic expression which contains only one term.
Polynomial — An algebraic expression with one or more terms where variables have positive integer powers.
Standard Form — Writing a polynomial in decreasing order of the powers of its terms.
Subtraction of Algebraic Expressions — The operation of subtracting like terms by adding their additive inverse.
Trinomial — An algebraic expression which contains exactly three unlike terms.
Unlike Terms — Terms that contain different algebraic factors.
Value of an Algebraic Expression — The result obtained by substituting specific numbers for the variables in an expression.
Variable — A symbol representing an unknown quantity that can take any value.
Zero Pairs — Additive inverse pairs of algebra tiles that combine to equal zero.

Question: Take a sheet of paper and a packet of bindis. Paste 1 bindi on the sheet of paper as shown in Fig. 6.1. Now, paste 4 bindis in such a way that they form a square, that is, 2 bindis each in 2 rows (Fig. 6.1). Similarly, paste 9 bindis in such a way that they form a square, that is, 3 bindis each in 3 rows. Continue the same pattern by pasting the bindis for the next two squares. Can you guess the number of bindis in the 10th squares? Can you form a rule for the number of bindis in the nth squares?

Answer:
1. Pattern Observation:
* 1st square: 1 bindi ($1 \times 1 = 1^2$)
* 2nd square: 4 bindis ($2 \times 2 = 2^2$)
* 3rd square: 9 bindis ($3 \times 3 = 3^2$)
* 4th square: 16 bindis ($4 \times 4 = 4^2$)
* 5th square: 25 bindis ($5 \times 5 = 5^2$)
2. Rule for nth Square:
* The number of bindis in the nth square is given by the rule: $n^2$.
3. Number of Bindis in the 10th Square:
* Substitute $n = 10$ into the rule.
* Number of bindis = $10^2 = 100$.
* So, there are 100 bindis in the 10th square.

Question 1: Write the algebraic expressions for the following statements.
a) Subtract the product of 2 and $x$ from 7.
b) The product of $3x$ and $y$ divided by 5.
c) The sum of squares of $x$ and $y$.
d) 7 times $m$ is added to 5 times $n$.
e) Take away 6 from the sum of $p$ and $q$.

Answer:
a) Product of 2 and $x$ is $2x$. Subtracting this from 7 gives: $7 - 2x$.
b) Product of $3x$ and $y$ is $3xy$. Divided by 5 gives: $\frac{3xy}{5}$.
c) Square of $x$ is $x^2$. Square of $y$ is $y^2$. Sum is: $x^2 + y^2$.
d) 7 times $m$ is $7m$. 5 times $n$ is $5n$. Added together: $7m + 5n$.
e) Sum of $p$ and $q$ is $p + q$. Taking away 6 gives: $p + q - 6$.

Question 2: Add the following expressions.
a) $5xy + 2x, -2xy + 3y$ and $8xy - 3x + 4y$
b) $m + 1$ and $m^2 + m$
c) $4t^4 + 2t + 11, -3t^2 + t$ and $t^4 - 2$
d) $2a + 3b - 4c, 8c - 1$ and $5b + 4$

Answer:
a) Collect like terms:
$xy$: $5xy - 2xy + 8xy = 11xy$
$x$: $2x - 3x = -x$
$y$: $3y + 4y = 7y$
Result: $11xy - x + 7y$
b) Collect like terms:
$m^2$: $m^2$
$m$: $m + m = 2m$
Constants: $1$
Result: $m^2 + 2m + 1$
c) Collect like terms:
$t^4$: $4t^4 + t^4 = 5t^4$
$t^2$: $-3t^2$
$t$: $2t + t = 3t$
Constants: $11 - 2 = 9$
Result: $5t^4 - 3t^2 + 3t + 9$
d) Collect like terms:
$a$: $2a$
$b$: $3b + 5b = 8b$
$c$: $-4c + 8c = 4c$
Constants: $-1 + 4 = 3$
Result: $2a + 8b + 4c + 3$

$x^2$: $7x^2 - 19x^2 = -12x^2$

Result: $-12x^2 + 55x$
d) Group terms:
$m$: $3m - 3m = 0$
$n$: $6n - 2n = 4n$
Result: $4n$

# ALGEBRAIC EXPRESSIONS

A combination of constants (1, 2, 3, ...) and variables (a, m, l, p, ...) connected by the signs of fundamental mathematical operations (+, -, × or ÷) is called an algebraic expression. For example, 4m + 7, 3y - 8, $\frac{x}{2}$ - 3, etc. are all algebraic expressions. Algebraic expressions can be formed by different combinations. Let us discuss these combinations with suitable examples.

1. Combining variables with themselves: The algebraic expression $x^2$ is formed by multiplying the variable $x$ with itself, $x \times x = x^2$.
2. Combining variables with other variables: The algebraic expression $(x + y)$ is formed by adding two variables $x$ and $y$, whereas the algebraic expression $xyz$ is formed by just multiplying the three variables $x$, $y$ and $z$.
3. Combining constants and variables: The algebraic expression $2yz$ is formed by multiplying the constant 2 with the product of the variables $y$ and $z$.

## Terms of an Algebraic Expression

Terms are added to form algebraic expressions. For example, in the algebraic expression $3x - 5$, $3x$ and $-5$ are the two terms. Similarly, $5p^2$, $4pq$ and $-6$ are the three terms in the algebraic expression $5p^2 + 4pq - 6$.

A term of an algebraic expression could be:
- a constant, for example, 2, -33, 45, etc.
- a variable, for example, $a$, $b$, $x$, $y$, etc.
- product of a constant and variables, for example, $3x$, $\frac{1}{2}ab$, etc.
- product of variables, for example, $xy$, $x^3y^5$, etc.

## Grasp It

Always include ‘-’ sign with the negative term.

## Factors of a Term

Consider the expression $5x^2 - 4xy$. This expression consists of two terms, $5x^2$ and $-4xy$. The term $5x^2$ is the product of 5, $x$ and $x$. We say that, 5, $x$ and $x$ are the factors of the term $5x^2$. A term is a product of its factors. The term $-4xy$ is a product of the factors $-4$, $x$ and $y$. The terms and factors of an expression can be conveniently represented by a tree diagram. The tree for the expression $(5x^2 - 4xy)$ is shown in Fig. 6.7.

## Coefficients

We have already learnt to write a term of an algebraic expression as the product of its factors. Factors may be variables or constants. A term generally consists of one numerical factor (or constant), the rest being variables, that is, algebraic factors. The numerical factor of a term is said to be the numerical coefficient or simply the coefficient of a term. For example, in $7ab^2$, 7 is the coefficient of the term. Here, $a$, $b$ and $b$ are called algebraic factors.

<!-- Page: 81 -->

However, coefficients need not only be numerical. Coefficient is a relative term.
In a term, any one of the factors is called the coefficient of the product of the other factors.
For example, in \(7ab^2\), 7 is the coefficient of \(ab^2\), \(a\) is the coefficient of \(7b^2\) and \(b^2\) is the coefficient of \(7a\).

## Grasp It
When the coefficient of a term is +1, it is usually omitted while writing the term.
For example, \(1x^2y\) is written simply as \(x^2y\), \(1xyz\) is written as \(xyz\) and so on. When the coefficient is \((-1)\), it is represented only by the minus sign. Thus, \((-1)x^2y\) is written as \(-x^2y\) and \((-1)xyz\) is written as \(-xyz\).

#### Solution:

b) Coefficient of \(x\) in \((5 + xz + y^2) = z\)
c) Coefficient of \(x\) in \((y - 2x + 2) = -2\)

## Types of Algebraic Expressions
Algebraic expressions can be classified on the basis of the number of terms they have.
- **Monomial**: An algebraic expression which contains only one term is called a monomial. For example, \(5m\), \(6pq\), \(8xyz\), \(-7\), etc. are all monomials.
- **Binomial**: An algebraic expression which contains two unlike terms is called a binomial. For example, \(5a + 3b\), \(5x - 6y\), \(2pq + 5qr\), etc. are all binomials. The expression \(2t + 6t\) is not a binomial as the terms \(2t\) and \(6t\) are like terms.
- **Trinomial**: An algebraic expression which contains three unlike terms is called a trinomial. For example, \(a + b + c\), \(p + 2q + 3r\), \(3m - 2n + 4p\), etc. are all trinomials. The expression \(a + 6b - 2a\) is not a trinomial as the terms \(a\) and \(-2a\) are like terms.
- **Polynomial**: An algebraic expression with one or more terms is called a polynomial. Thus, a monomial, a binomial and a trinomial are all polynomials. For example, \(x^2 + y - 4z + 1\) is a polynomial.

### Get It
- \(4x^2 + 3x + 6\) is a polynomial. \(\checkmark\)
- \(4x^2 + \frac{3}{x} + 6\) is a polynomial. \(\times\) (None of the terms can have a negative exponent for any variable.)

## Like and Unlike Terms
The terms with same algebraic factors are called like terms. In like terms, the numerical coefficient may be different. For example, \(5xy\) and \(3xy\) are like terms. On the other hand, the terms with different algebraic factors are called unlike terms. For example, \(2x^2y\) and \(-2xy^2\) are unlike terms, as the algebraic factors of the two terms are different.

## Degree of a Polynomial
The highest power of a variable in a polynomial is called its degree. For example, in the polynomial \(a^2 + 6a + 4\), the highest power of the variable \(a\) is 2. Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 2.
The degree of a polynomial is decided by the term with the higher power. The degree of a term containing more than one variable is obtained by

adding the powers of all the variables in a term.
For example, the polynomial $-23x^2y + 11xy - 8$ is of degree 3 as the highest power of its term ($x^2y$) is 3. Similarly, the degree of the polynomial $a^5b^2 + a^2b^2 + a^3b + ab$ is 7.

### Grasp It
- The degree of a constant term is 0.
- Writing a polynomial in decreasing order of the powers of its terms is called its standard form.

#### Solved Example
**Example 4:** Write the degree of each of the following polynomials.
a) $a + a^2 + a^4 + 2$
b) $1 + x^3 + x^5 - x^2$

#### Solution:
a) Arranging the terms of the given polynomial in descending order of their powers, we get:
\[ a^4 + a^2 + a + 2 \]
Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 4.
b) Arranging the terms of the given polynomial in descending order of their powers, we get:
\[ x^5 + x^3 - x^2 + 1 \]
Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 5.
c) Arranging the terms of the given polynomial in descending order of their powers, we get:
\[ x^5y^3 - x^4y^2 + x^3y - x^2 + xy \]
Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 8.

## Subtraction of Algebraic Expressions

Like addition, we can subtract only like terms. Subtracting a term from another term is same as adding its additive inverse to the other term. For example, subtracting \(3x\) from \(5x\) is same as adding \(-3x\) to \(5x\).

### Grasp It
- The difference of two like terms is a like term with a numerical coefficient equal to the difference of the numerical coefficients of the two like terms.
- Unlike terms cannot be subtracted.

## Wrap Up

- An algebraic expression is formed when variables and constants are combined using one or more fundamental operations.
- An algebraic expression is made up of terms. Terms are added to make an expression.
- A term is the product of its factor. Factors containing variables are called algebraic factors.
- The coefficient of a term is the numerical factor of the term.
- Terms with the same algebraic factors are called like terms, while terms with different algebraic factors are called unlike terms.
- Any algebraic expression having one or more terms with variables whose powers are positive integers is called a polynomial. Specifically, a one-term expression is a monomial, a two-term expression is a binomial and a three-term expression is a trinomial.
