Chapter 6: Algebraic Expressions – Summary

What is an Algebraic Expression?

An algebraic expression is formed by combining constants (like 1, 2, 3) and variables (like $x$, $y$, $z$) using mathematical operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, or division.

Examples:
- $4m + 7$
- $3y - 8$
- $\frac{x}{2} - 3$

Key Parts of an Algebraic Expression

Terms

Terms are the parts of an expression that are added or subtracted.
- In $3x - 5$, the terms are $3x$ and $-5$.
- In $5p^2 + 4pq - 6$, the terms are $5p^2$, $4pq$, and $-6$.

Factors

A term is a product of its factors.
- In $5x^2$, the factors are 5, $x$, and $x$.
- In $-4xy$, the factors are $-4$, $x$, and $y$.

Coefficients

The numerical factor of a term is called the coefficient.
- In $7ab^2$, the coefficient is 7.
- In $-2x$, the coefficient is $-2$.

Note:
- If the coefficient is +1, it is usually not written (e.g., $x^2$ instead of $1x^2$).
- If the coefficient is $-1$, it is written as a minus sign (e.g., $-x^2$).

Types of Algebraic Expressions

Type Description Example
Monomial One term $5m$, $8xyz$, $-7$
Binomial Two unlike terms $5a + 3b$, $2pq + 5qr$
Trinomial Three unlike terms $a + b + c$, $3m - 2n + 4p$
Polynomial One or more terms (includes monomials, binomials, trinomials) $x^2 + y - 4z + 1$

Important:
- Terms must be unlike to be part of a binomial or trinomial.
- Like terms have the same algebraic factors (e.g., $3x$ and $5x$ are like terms).
- Unlike terms have different algebraic factors (e.g., $2x^2y$ and $-2xy^2$).

Degree of a Polynomial

The degree is the highest power of any variable in the polynomial.
- In $a^2 + 6a + 4$, the degree is 2.
- In $-23x^2y + 11xy - 8$, the degree is 3 (since $x^2y$ has power $2+1=3$).
- In $a^5b^2 + a^2b^2 + a^3b + ab$, the degree is 7 (from $a^5b^2$: $5+2=7$).

Rule:
- Degree of a constant term is 0.
- Polynomials are usually written in standard form, with terms arranged in descending order of powers.

Adding Algebraic Expressions

  • Like terms can be added by adding their coefficients.
  • Unlike terms cannot be combined.

Steps to Add:

  1. Group like terms together.
  2. Add the numerical coefficients of like terms.
  3. Write the result with the same variables.

Example:
Add $72ab + 21a^3b^2 + 53a^3$ and $-23ab + 17a^2b^3 + 5a$
→ $(72ab - 23ab) + 21a^3b^2 + 17a^2b^3 + 53a^3 + 5a$
→ $49ab + 21a^3b^2 + 17a^2b^3 + 53a^3 + 5a$

Subtracting Algebraic Expressions

  • Subtracting a term is the same as adding its additive inverse.
  • Only like terms can be subtracted.

Steps to Subtract:

  1. Write the expressions with a minus sign between them.
  2. Change the sign of each term in the expression being subtracted.
  3. Group like terms and combine them.

Example:
Subtract $x - 2y - 3z$ from $2x + 3y - 5z$
→ $2x + 3y - 5z - x + 2y + 3z$
→ $(2x - x) + (3y + 2y) + (-5z + 3z)$
→ $x + 5y - 2z$

Finding the Value of an Algebraic Expression

Replace variables with given numbers and simplify.

Example:
Find value of $17x + 19x^2 + 21x^3$ when $x = 2$
→ $17(2) + 19(4) + 21(8)$
→ $34 + 76 + 168 = 278$

Real-Life Use of Algebraic Expressions

Algebra helps in writing general formulas for real-life situations.

Situation Formula
Area of a rectangle $A = l \times b$
Simple interest $S.I. = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$
Diameter of a circle $d = 2r$
Number of decimetres in $x$ metres $n = 10x$
Perimeter of a square with side $(x+4)$ $P = 4(x + 4) = 4x + 16$

Wrap-Up

Algebra helps us generalize patterns, solve problems, and create formulas.
By understanding variables, constants, terms, coefficients, and operations, we can work with algebraic expressions confidently and apply them to real-life situations.

Short Revision Notes

  • An algebraic expression is formed by combining constants and variables using operations like +, −, ×, ÷.
  • Terms are added to form an algebraic expression; each term is a product of factors.
  • A constant is a fixed number; a variable represents an unknown quantity.
  • The numerical factor of a term is called the coefficient; coefficients can be numerical or algebraic.
  • Like terms have the same algebraic factors; unlike terms have different algebraic factors.
  • A monomial has one term, a binomial has two unlike terms, a trinomial has three unlike terms.
  • A polynomial is an expression with one or more terms, all of which have positive integer powers.
  • The degree of a polynomial is the highest power of any variable in the expression.
  • For a term with multiple variables, degree is the sum of the exponents of the variables.
  • Like terms can be added or subtracted; unlike terms cannot.
  • To add algebraic expressions, group like terms and add their coefficients.
  • To subtract algebraic expressions, change the sign of each term in the second expression and add.
  • The value of an algebraic expression depends on the values assigned to its variables.
  • Algebraic expressions can represent real-life situations and formulas (e.g., area, interest, perimeter).
  • Expressions with variables in the denominator are not polynomials.
  • A polynomial must have only non-negative integer exponents.
  • In standard form, terms are arranged in descending order of powers.
  • To find a general rule, observe patterns and express them using variables.
  • The coefficient of a term is the numerical part; when it is +1 or −1, it is usually omitted.
  • Zero pairs (additive inverses) cancel each other out in algebra tiles.
  • Algebra tiles help visualize addition and subtraction of algebraic expressions.

Key Terms

Algebraic expression — a combination of constants and variables connected by mathematical operations (+, −, ×, ÷).
Coefficient — the numerical factor of a term in an algebraic expression.
Constant — a number whose value does not change, such as 1, 2, or 5.
Degree of a polynomial — the highest power of any variable in the polynomial.
Factor — a number or variable that divides another term exactly; in a term, factors are multiplied together to form the term.
Like terms — terms that have the same algebraic factors (same variables with the same powers).
Monomial — an algebraic expression with only one term, such as 5x or 7.
Polynomial — an algebraic expression with one or more terms, including monomials, binomials, and trinomials.
Trinomial — an algebraic expression with exactly three unlike terms, such as a + b + c.
Binomial — an algebraic expression with exactly two unlike terms, such as 3x + 2y.
Term — a part of an algebraic expression that is added or subtracted, such as 3x or −5.
Variable — a letter representing an unknown quantity, such as x, y, or z.
Unlike terms — terms that have different algebraic factors (different variables or different powers).
Addition of algebraic expressions — combining like terms by adding their numerical coefficients.
Subtraction of algebraic expressions — subtracting like terms by finding the difference of their coefficients.
Standard form of a polynomial — writing the terms in descending order of their powers.
Numerical coefficient — the number that multiplies a variable or variables in a term.
Product of variables — a term formed by multiplying variables, such as xy or x³y⁵.
Product of a constant and variables — a term formed by multiplying a constant with variables, such as 3xy.
Power — the exponent indicating how many times a variable is multiplied by itself, such as x².

# ALGEBRAIC EXPRESSIONS

A combination of constants (1, 2, 3, ...) and variables (a, m, l, p, ...) connected by the signs of fundamental mathematical operations (+, -, × or ÷) is called an algebraic expression. For example, 4m + 7, 3y - 8, $\frac{x}{2}$ - 3, etc. are all algebraic expressions. Algebraic expressions can be formed by different combinations. Let us discuss these combinations with suitable examples.

1. Combining variables with themselves: The algebraic expression $x^2$ is formed by multiplying the variable $x$ with itself, $x \times x = x^2$.
2. Combining variables with other variables: The algebraic expression $(x + y)$ is formed by adding two variables $x$ and $y$, whereas the algebraic expression $xyz$ is formed by just multiplying the three variables $x$, $y$ and $z$.
3. Combining constants and variables: The algebraic expression $2yz$ is formed by multiplying the constant 2 with the product of the variables $y$ and $z$.

## Terms of an Algebraic Expression

Terms are added to form algebraic expressions. For example, in the algebraic expression $3x - 5$, $3x$ and $-5$ are the two terms. Similarly, $5p^2$, $4pq$ and $-6$ are the three terms in the algebraic expression $5p^2 + 4pq - 6$.

A term of an algebraic expression could be:
- a constant, for example, 2, -33, 45, etc.
- a variable, for example, $a$, $b$, $x$, $y$, etc.
- product of a constant and variables, for example, $3x$, $\frac{1}{2}ab$, etc.
- product of variables, for example, $xy$, $x^3y^5$, etc.

## Grasp It

Always include ‘-’ sign with the negative term.

## Factors of a Term

Consider the expression $5x^2 - 4xy$. This expression consists of two terms, $5x^2$ and $-4xy$. The term $5x^2$ is the product of 5, $x$ and $x$. We say that, 5, $x$ and $x$ are the factors of the term $5x^2$. A term is a product of its factors. The term $-4xy$ is a product of the factors $-4$, $x$ and $y$. ## Coefficients

We have already learnt to write a term of an algebraic expression as the product of its factors. Factors may be variables or constants. A term generally consists of one numerical factor (or constant), the rest being variables, that is, algebraic factors. The numerical factor of a term is said to be the numerical coefficient or simply the coefficient of a term. For example, in $7ab^2$, 7 is the coefficient of the term. Here, $a$, $b$ and $b$ are called algebraic factors.

<!-- Page: 81 -->

However, coefficients need not only be numerical. Coefficient is a relative term.
In a term, any one of the factors is called the coefficient of the product of the other factors.
For example, in \(7ab^2\), 7 is the coefficient of \(ab^2\), \(a\) is the coefficient of \(7b^2\) and \(b^2\) is the coefficient of \(7a\).

## Grasp It
When the coefficient of a term is +1, it is usually omitted while writing the term.
For example, \(1x^2y\) is written simply as \(x^2y\), \(1xyz\) is written as \(xyz\) and so on. When the coefficient is \((-1)\), it is represented only by the minus sign. Thus, \((-1)x^2y\) is written as \(-x^2y\) and \((-1)xyz\) is written as \(-xyz\).

### Solved Example
#### Example 3: Write the coefficient of \(x\) in each of the following.
a) \(y - xy^2\)
b) \(5 + xz + y^2\)
c) \(y - 2x + 2\)

#### Solution:
a) Coefficient of \(x\) in \((y - xy^2) = -y^2\)
b) Coefficient of \(x\) in \((5 + xz + y^2) = z\)
c) Coefficient of \(x\) in \((y - 2x + 2) = -2\)

## Types of Algebraic Expressions
Algebraic expressions can be classified on the basis of the number of terms they have.
- **Monomial**: An algebraic expression which contains only one term is called a monomial. For example, \(5m\), \(6pq\), \(8xyz\), \(-7\), etc. are all monomials.
- **Binomial**: An algebraic expression which contains two unlike terms is called a binomial. For example, \(5a + 3b\), \(5x - 6y\), \(2pq + 5qr\), etc. are all binomials. The expression \(2t + 6t\) is not a binomial as the terms \(2t\) and \(6t\) are like terms.
- **Trinomial**: An algebraic expression which contains three unlike terms is called a trinomial. For example, \(a + b + c\), \(p + 2q + 3r\), \(3m - 2n + 4p\), etc. are all trinomials. The expression \(a + 6b - 2a\) is not a trinomial as the terms \(a\) and \(-2a\) are like terms.
- **Polynomial**: An algebraic expression with one or more terms is called a polynomial. Thus, a monomial, a binomial and a trinomial are all polynomials. For example, \(x^2 + y - 4z + 1\) is a polynomial.

### Get It
- \(4x^2 + 3x + 6\) is a polynomial. \(\checkmark\)
- \(4x^2 + \frac{3}{x} + 6\) is a polynomial. \(\times\) (None of the terms can have a negative exponent for any variable.)

## Like and Unlike Terms
The terms with same algebraic factors are called like terms. In like terms, the numerical coefficient may be different. For example, \(5xy\) and \(3xy\) are like terms. On the other hand, the terms with different algebraic factors are called unlike terms. For example, \(2x^2y\) and \(-2xy^2\) are unlike terms, as the algebraic factors of the two terms are different.

## Degree of a Polynomial
The highest power of a variable in a polynomial is called its degree. For example, in the polynomial \(a^2 + 6a + 4\), the highest power of the variable \(a\) is 2. Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 2.
The degree of a polynomial is decided by the term with the higher power. The degree of a term containing more than one variable is obtained by

adding the powers of all the variables in a term.
For example, the polynomial $-23x^2y + 11xy - 8$ is of degree 3 as the highest power of its term ($x^2y$) is 3. Similarly, the degree of the polynomial $a^5b^2 + a^2b^2 + a^3b + ab$ is 7.

### Grasp It
- The degree of a constant term is 0.
- Writing a polynomial in decreasing order of the powers of its terms is called its standard form.

#### Solved Example
**Example 4:** Write the degree of each of the following polynomials.
a) $a + a^2 + a^4 + 2$
b) $1 + x^3 + x^5 - x^2$

#### Solution:
a) Arranging the terms of the given polynomial in descending order of their powers, we get:
\[ a^4 + a^2 + a + 2 \]
Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 4.
b) Arranging the terms of the given polynomial in descending order of their powers, we get:
\[ x^5 + x^3 - x^2 + 1 \]
Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 5.
c) Arranging the terms of the given polynomial in descending order of their powers, we get:
\[ x^5y^3 - x^4y^2 + x^3y - x^2 + xy \]
Thus, the degree of this polynomial is 8.

## Subtraction of Algebraic Expressions

Like addition, we can subtract only like terms. Subtracting a term from another term is same as adding its additive inverse to the other term. For example, subtracting \(3x\) from \(5x\) is same as adding \(-3x\) to \(5x\).

### Grasp It
- The difference of two like terms is a like term with a numerical coefficient equal to the difference of the numerical coefficients of the two like terms.
- Unlike terms cannot be subtracted.

# FINDING THE VALUE OF AN ALGEBRAIC EXPRESSION

We know that an algebraic expression has variables and a variable can take any value. Thus, the value of an algebraic expression depends on the values of the variables forming the expression. We can find the value of an algebraic expression by replacing the variables with numbers.

**Solution:** When $x = 2$, $17x + 19x^2 + 21x^3 = 17 \times 2 + 19 \times 2^2 + 21 \times 2^3 = 17 \times 2 + 19 \times 4 + 21 \times 8 = 34 + 76 + 168 = 278$

$= -2 \times (-4)^2 - (-4) \times 3 - 3^2 + 3$

$= -2 \times 16 - (-12) - 9 + 3$

$= -32 + 12 - 9 + 3$

$= -41 + 15$

$= -26$

## Wrap Up

- An algebraic expression is formed when variables and constants are combined using one or more fundamental operations.
- An algebraic expression is made up of terms. Terms are added to make an expression.
- A term is the product of its factor. Factors containing variables are called algebraic factors.
- The coefficient of a term is the numerical factor of the term.
- Terms with the same algebraic factors are called like terms, while terms with different algebraic factors are called unlike terms.
- Any algebraic expression having one or more terms with variables whose powers are positive integers is called a polynomial. Specifically, a one-term expression is a monomial, a two-term expression is a binomial and a three-term expression is a trinomial.
