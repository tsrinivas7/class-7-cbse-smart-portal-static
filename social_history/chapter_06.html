Chapter Summary: Reaching God Through Devotion

Introduction

The medieval period in India saw a blending of many cultures, leading to new religious ideas. Two major movements emerged during this time:
* The Bhakti Movement: Focused on the union of an individual with God through devotion.
* The Sufi Movement: Brought to India by the Turks; focused on Muslim mysticism and reaching God through love.

The Bhakti Movement

In South India (7th Century)

The movement began here first with two main groups of saints:
* Alvars (Vaishnavites): Worshipped Lord Vishnu. There were 12 famous Alvar saints. Their songs are compiled in the Divya Prabandham.
* Nayanars (Shaivites): Worshipped Lord Shiva. There were 63 Nayanar saints. Their songs are compiled in Tevaram and Tiruvacakam.

Key Features:
* Saints came from all castes, including those considered untouchables (like Panars and Pulaiyar).
* They rejected the caste system.
* They preached in local languages (Tamil and Telugu).
* Many female devotees joined and challenged rigid social norms.

Other Important Teachers

  • Shankara (8th Century): From Kerala. Preached Advaita Philosophy (oneness of individual soul and Supreme God). Established four mathas (learning centres) across India.
  • Ramanuja (11th Century): From Tamil Nadu. Preached Vishishtadvaita (God and soul are united but distinct).
  • Madhavacharya (13th Century): Popularised the worship of Lord Krishna.
  • Basavanna (12th Century): From Karnataka. Founded the Virashaivas or Lingayats, who worshipped Lord Shiva in the form of a Linga.

In North India

  • Namdeva: A tailor saint who wrote devotional songs in Marathi.
  • Lal Ded: A woman saint from Kashmir who worshipped Shiva.
  • Vallabhacharya & Narsi Mehta: Preached in Gujarat.
  • Shankaradeva: From Assam. Composed plays and set up Namghars (houses of recitation).
  • Ramananda: A pioneer in North India. His disciples included Kabir and Ravidas.
  • Mirabai: A Rajput princess devoted to Lord Krishna. She questioned upper-caste domination and left her palace to become a wandering saint.
  • Chaitanya Mahaprabhu: From Bengal. Devoted to Krishna. Popular among low castes. Known for Kirtan (devotional songs).

The Sufi Movement

The Sufis were Muslim mystics who believed in reaching God through love, devotion, and compassion.

  • Beliefs: They opposed greed, materialism, and vulgar displays of wealth. They advocated for one God and a simple society.
  • Organization: Sufis were organised into 12 Orders called Silsilahs. The most important were the Suhrawardi and Chishti orders.
  • Khanqahs: Hospices where Sufi saints lived with their disciples. These places had open kitchens (langars) and were open to people of all classes.
  • Dargahs: When a Sufi saint died, their grave became a shrine and a centre of pilgrimage.
  • Methods of Training: Included zikr (chanting), contemplation, sama (singing), raqs (dancing), and breath control.

Case Studies: Great Saints

Kabir (15th Century)

  • Background: Son of a Brahmin widow, raised by a Muslim weaver family in Benaras.
  • Teachings: Preached a religion of love to unite all people. Rejected idol worship, rituals, and pilgrimages.
  • Social Stance: Strongly opposed the caste system and untouchability.
  • Literature: Wrote short poems called Dohas in spoken Hindi. He used words from Hindi, Persian, Arabic, and Turkish.
  • Followers: Known as Kabirpanthis.

Guru Nanak Dev (1469–1539 CE)

  • Background: Born in Talwandi (now Pakistan). Founder of the Sikh religion.
  • Teachings: Believed in 'One God' reached through selfless love and devotion.
  • Travel: Toured India, Sri Lanka, Mecca, and Medina.
  • Institutions: Established Gurdwaras (sacred spaces) and Langars (common kitchens) where everyone ate together.
  • Social Stance: Rejected idol worship, caste distinctions, and gender discrimination. Allowed women to attend sermons.
  • Legacy: He was the first of the ten Gurus of Sikhs. His hymns are called Shabad.

Common Themes Across Movements

  • Humanism: All saint-poets emphasised love and camaraderie.
  • Anti-Ritual: They rejected expensive and complicated ceremonies.
  • Language: They wrote in the language of the common man (regional languages).
  • Equality: They totally rejected the caste system and promoted unity.
  • Music: Their songs (bhajans, qawwalis, kirtans) are still sung today.

THE SUFI MOVEMENT
The counterpart of Bhakti movement
was the Sufi movement in Islam. The

Sufi saints called pirs also believed in reaching
God through love and devotion and in being
kind and compassionate towards all the human
beings. The Sufis were disgusted by the vulgar
display of wealth and loss of moral values
among the rich Muslims. They, like the Bhakti
saints, spoke of a simple society free from
greed, materialism and advocated the theory
of one God.

Like the Bhakti poets, the Sufis also composed
poems and qawwalis expressing their
unconditional love to God and humanity. Many
Sufi orders evolved in India each following
somewhat different tariqa (method).

Gradually, the Sufis were organised into 12
orders or Silsilahs. The chief among them were
the Suhrawardi and Chishti Silsilahs.

**Insight:** The Suhrawardi Silsilah arose
during the period of Delhi Sultanate. Its
founder in India was Sheikh Bahauddin
Zakariya. Punjab, Sindh and Bengal became
three important centres of this Silsilah.

The silsilah was led by a Sufi saint. These
saints lived in their abodes or hospices, called
Khanqahs, along with their disciples. Devotees
of all classes from the ordinary people to the
upper class nobles and even royal people,
regularly visited these Khanqahs. In these
Khanqahs, there were open kitchens or langars.
Khanqahs were open to all. When the pir or the
Sufi saint, who was the head of the Khanqah
died, his grave would become a dargah and a
centre of pilgrimage.

Sufi saints developed elaborate methods of
training using zikr (chanting of the name or
sacred formula) contemplation, sama (singing),
raqs (dancing), breath control, and discussion
of parables.

## CASE STUDY

### Kabir
According to legends, Kabir was the son of a brahmana widow who abandoned him after his birth. He was later brought up in the house of a Muslim weaver. He too became a weaver but while living in Benaras, he came in contact with Hindu and Muslim saints. He rejected all rituals and ceremonies and preached a religion of love which would unite all the people. Kabir's poems were written in the form of spoken Hindi language of the ordinary people. His followers were both Hindus and Muslims who were attracted by Kabir's message of love and brotherhood.

### Kabir's rejection of the caste system, especially the practice of untouchability and his opposition to all kinds of discrimination between human beings—on the basis of caste, religion and wealth—made him very popular among the common people.

### Central India
**(13th-17th Century)**
- Believed in formless God as ultimate goal
- Nathpanthis, Siddhacharis, Yogis
- Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram, Jnaneshwar

### Northern India
**(13th Century)**
- Critical and unjust social order
- Rejected caste system
- Promoted unity
- Tulsidas, Surdas, Ravidas, Mirabai, Ramananda, Narsi Mehta

### Sufism
**(12th Century)**
- Counterpart of Bhakti movement
- To reach God through love and devotion
- Organised into Orders called Silsilahs
- Saints lived in Khanqahs
- Organised langars
- Hazrat Nizamuddin Aulia, Khwaja Moin-ud-din Chisti
