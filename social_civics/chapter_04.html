Chapter 04

Chapter Summary

THE STATE GOVERNMENT

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

To enable the learners to understand the structure and functioning of the State Government in India.
* Be familiar with the three levels of government: Central, State, and Local.
* Understand the composition of a State Government including the Governor and Ministers.
* Distinguish between Vidhan Sabha (Lower House) and Vidhan Parishad (Upper House).
* Be aware of the powers and functions of the Governor and the Chief Ministers.

India is a Union of States

India is structured as a Union where power is shared between the Centre and the States.
* There is one Union Government and several State governments across the country.
* The Union (Centre) is more powerful than the individual States.
* At present, there are 28 States in the Indian Union, each having its own Legislature.
* The Parliament of India is the law-making body at the Union level.
* The State Legislature serves as the law-making body at the state level.

Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)

The Legislative Assembly is the primary representative body of the state government.
* It consists of the Governor and the Legislative Assembly led by the Chief Minister.
* It represents the people of the state and its powers apply only within state boundaries.
* Members are directly elected by adult citizens registered as voters in the State.
* Elections are held based on universal adult franchise.
* Some seats are reserved for Scheduled Castes (SCs) and Scheduled Tribes (STs) based on population.

The state legislature consists of either one or two houses

States may have different structures for their legislative bodies depending on their needs.
* If there is only one house, it is called a unicameral legislature.
* The single house is known as the Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha) or the lower house.
* Some states have a second house known as the Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad).
* The Legislative Council is considered the upper house of the state.
* States having two houses in the legislature are called a bicameral legislature.

Composition

The Legislative Assembly has specific rules regarding the number of members and leadership.
* The number of Vidhan Sabha members cannot be more than 500 and not less than 60.
* Very small states have been allowed to have a lesser number of members.
* Members elect their presiding officer who is known as the Speaker and Deputy Speaker.
* The Speaker presides over the meetings of the House and conducts its proceedings.
* The party that wins the majority of seats forms the government; others act as opposition.

Important Terms

  • constituencies — the district and the residents of a district who elect a legislative or an official.
  • presiding — to exercise control.

Tenure

The duration of the Legislative Assembly is fixed but can be ended early under specific conditions.
* The tenure of Vidhan Sabha is five years.
* The Governor can dissolve it before completion on the advice of the Chief Minister.
* It may be dissolved by the President on the advice of the Governor during a constitutional emergency.
* The Assembly can be dissolved if a motion of no confidence is passed against the government.
* To dissolve the house means the assembly is no longer in existence and fresh elections are held.

Insight

  • President's rule — In India, this is the suspension of state government and imposition of direct Union government rule in a state.

Qualifications to be the Members of the Legislative Assembly

Candidates must meet specific criteria to contest Assembly elections.
* Should be a citizen of India.
* Should be a voter in any constituency of that state.
* Must be mentally sound and solvent.
* Must be at least 25 years of age.

IMPORTANT TERMS

  • dissolve — dismiss.
  • mentally sound — one who is able to understand one's actions and distinguish right from wrong.
  • solvent — one who is able to pay one's debts.

Composition of the Legislative Council

The Legislative Council is formed through a mix of election and nomination.
* The Legislative Council is partly elected and partly nominated.
* Most of the members are indirectly elected by various groups.
* One-third members are elected by the members of the Legislative Assembly.
* One-third are elected by electorates consisting of members of municipalities and district boards.
* One-twelfth are elected by graduates and one-twelfth by teachers.

Key Question

  • Creation/Abolition — The power to create or abolish the Vidhan Parishad lies with the Parliament of India based on a resolution by the State Legislature.

Legislative Council, Karnataka

Specific examples illustrate how the council composition works in practice.
* One-twelfth members are elected by graduates in the State with a standing of three years.
* One-twelfth members are elected by teachers of educational institutions.
* About one-sixth members are nominated by the Governor for special knowledge.
* Nominations are made from persons skilled in literature, science, arts, co-operative movement, and social service.

Composition of a Legislative Council

A detailed breakdown shows how seats are distributed among different electorates.
* Graduates: One-Twelfth (1/12th) of members.
* Teachers: One-Twelfth (1/12th) of members.
* Local Authorities: Nearly One-Third (1/3rd) of members.
* Assembly Members: Nearly One-Third (1/3rd) of members.
* Nomination by Governor: 1/6 of members.

Tenure

The Legislative Council operates differently from the Assembly regarding time limits.
* The Legislative Council is a permanent house like the Rajya Sabha at the centre.
* It is never dissolved completely.
* The tenure of its members is six years.
* One-third of its members retire after every two years.
* The presiding officer is known as the Chairman, elected by its members.

Qualifications to be the Members of the Legislative Council

Members of the Council must meet stricter age and financial requirements.
* Needs to be a citizen of India.
* Should have his/her name on the voter's list of the state.
* Should not be below 30 years of age.
* Should be mentally sound and not a bankrupt.

Powers and Functions of the State Legislature

The legislature performs critical roles in governance and law-making.
* Primary function is law-making on subjects mentioned in the State List (police, agriculture, etc.).
* It makes laws on subjects of the Concurrent List unless contradicted by Union law.
* It passes the Money Bill and the Budget of the State.
* It exercises control over the Executive by asking questions and criticizing policies.
* It ratifies amendments made by the Parliament related to state government powers.

Steps followed in the process of making a law

Legislation involves a structured procedure to turn proposals into binding laws.
* Legislation — The process of making and passing laws.
* Bill — A proposal of law that can originate in either house of the State Assembly.
* There are two kinds of bills: Ordinary Bill and Money Bill.
* Ordinary bills can be introduced in either house by any minister.
* Money bills can be introduced only in the Vidhan Sabha with prior consent of the Governor.

Key Question

  • Money Bill Certification — The Speaker of the Legislative Assembly certifies whether a bill is a Money Bill.

Passage of Ordinary Bill

Ordinary bills go through three stages known as Three Readings.
* First Reading: Introduction of the bill and explanation of provisions.
* Second Reading: General discussion; bill may be referred to a Select Committee.
* Third Reading: Voting takes place on the final version of the bill.
* Once passed in both houses, the bill is sent for the Governor's assent.
* The Legislative Council can retain the bill for three months but cannot reject it permanently.

Other functions of the State Legislature

Beyond law-making, the legislature supports administrative oversight.
* Passes the Ordinances issued by the Governor when the Assembly is not in session.
* Ratifies amendments made by the Parliament related to the powers of the state government.
* Exercises control over the Executive by asking questions and giving suggestions.
* Ministers are responsible and accountable to the Legislative Assembly.
* Members of the Legislative Council do not participate in the election of the President.

HEAD OF THE STATE

The Governor serves as the formal head of the state administration.
* The Governor is the constitutional head of the state.
* He/She administers with the help of the Chief Minister and his Council of Ministers.
* Union Territories are administered by the President directly.
* The President appoints a suitable officer for administering Union Territories.

Appointment of the Governor

Specific criteria determine who can be appointed as the Governor.
* Governor is appointed by the President of India in consultation with the Prime Minister.
* Must be a citizen of India.
* Must not be less than 35 years of age.
* Must not be a member of the Parliament or any State Legislature.
* Must not hold any office of profit under the government.

Tenure

The Governor holds office for a fixed period but is subject to removal.
* The Governor is appointed for a period of 5 years.
* He may be removed or shifted to other states during this period.
* He is accountable to the President of India.
* He holds office during the pleasure of the President.
* Therefore, the Governor acts as the Constitutional head and agent of the Central Government.

Powers and responsibilities of the Governor

The Governor wields significant authority across executive, legislative, and judicial domains.
* Executive Powers: All executive powers vested in him/her exercised with aid of Council of Ministers.
* Legislative Powers: A bill becomes an Act only after approval of the Governor; summons sessions.
* Judicial Powers: Appoints High Court judges in consultation with President; grants pardons.
* Discretionary Powers: Can impose President's rule; reserve Bills for President; appoint CM if no majority.
* Financial Powers: Sees annual budget laid in assembly; Money bills need prior permission.

THE CHIEF MINISTER

The Chief Minister leads the executive branch of the state government.
* The leader of the majority Party in the Legislative Assembly is appointed as the Chief Minister.
* Qualifications are the same as those of any other member of the Assembly.
* He appoints other ministers on the advice of the Chief Minister.
* He assigns/allocates the portfolios to the ministers.
* He can ask any minister to resign at any time.

Powers or functions of the Chief Minister

The Chief Minister manages the day-to-day administration and policy direction.
* The Chief Minister is appointed by the Governor.
* He assigns/allocates the portfolios to the ministers.
* He can ask any minister to resign at any time.
* Ministers are responsible and accountable to the Legislative Assembly.
* He heads the Council of Ministers and guides state policy.

Revision Flow Chart

A summary of the administrative structure consolidates key roles and facts.
* The Governor: Constitutional Head, appointed by President for five years, decisions taken in his name.
* The Legislative Assembly: Highest representative body, strength depends on population, members called MLAs.
* The Legislative Council: Permanent House, members called MLCs, tenure six years, 1/6th nominated by governor.
* Chief Minister: Leader of majority party, heads the government, allocates portfolios.
* Speaker: Presides over sittings of the Legislative Assembly, maintains order.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the lesson, learners will be able to demonstrate understanding of state governance.
* Explain the composition of a State Government.
* Compare the working and composition of Vidhan Sabha and Vidhan Parishad.
* List the powers and functions of the Governor and the Chief Ministers.

Part 1: Embedded Key Questions & Discussion Points

Key Question 1: Find out the states that have unicameral and bicameral legislatures.
* Answer: According to the chapter, states with only one house are called unicameral legislatures (most states). States with two houses are called bicameral legislatures. The text provides examples of bicameral states through image captions, such as Bihar and Karnataka, which have a Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad). Other states typically have only the Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha).

Key Question 2: Why is that Goa has only 40 members and Uttar Pradesh has 403 members in the Assembly?
* Answer: The number of members in the Vidhan Sabha depends on the population of the state. The text states that the strength of the assembly depends on the population. While the general rule is 60 to 500 members, very small states like Goa have been allowed to have fewer members (like 40), whereas larger states like Uttar Pradesh have more members (403) to represent their larger population.

Key Question 3: Who is empowered to create or abolish the Vidhan Parishad?
* Answer: Although the text emphasizes that the Union (Centre) is more powerful than States, the specific power to create or abolish a Legislative Council lies with the Parliament of India. This aligns with the chapter's statement that the Union Government is more powerful and handles broader structural arrangements.

Key Question 4: What are the components of Money Bill?
* Answer: The text defines a Money Bill primarily by its introduction process rather than listing specific components. It states that a Money Bill relates to financial matters as it involves the Budget of the State, taxes, and government expenditure. It can only be introduced in the Vidhan Sabha with the prior consent of the Governor.

Key Question 5: Who certifies that any bill is a Money Bill?
* Answer: The Speaker of the Legislative Assembly certifies whether a bill is a Money Bill or not. This ensures that only bills truly related to financial matters follow the special procedure reserved for Money Bills.

Key Question 6: List the subjects mentioned under the Concurrent List.
* Answer: The text mentions that the State Legislature makes laws on 52 subjects (originally 47) given under the Concurrent List. While the text does not list every subject, it notes that in case of contradiction between Union and State law on these subjects, the law made by the Parliament shall prevail.

6. Short Answer Type Questions

(a) How is a member of a Legislative Assembly elected?
* Answer: Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs) are directly elected by all adult citizens registered as voters in the State on the basis of universal adult franchise. The state is divided into constituencies for this purpose.

(b) What are the differences between a Money Bill and an Ordinary Bill?
* Answer:
* Introduction: An Ordinary Bill can be introduced in either House (Assembly or Council). A Money Bill can only be introduced in the Vidhan Sabha with the Governor's consent.
* Council Power: The Legislative Council can retain an Ordinary Bill for up to 3 months and suggest amendments. It can only hold a Money Bill for 14 days; if it fails to return it, the bill is deemed passed.
* Power Balance: The Assembly has more power over Ordinary Bills compared to the Council.

(c) List any two discretionary powers of the Governor.
* Answer:
1. Imposing President's Rule: If the Governor is satisfied that administration is not carried out according to constitutional provisions, they can recommend President's rule.
2. Appointing Chief Minister: When no party has an absolute majority, the Governor uses discretion to appoint the Chief Minister who they think can prove majority in the Assembly.

(d) Who are called the MLAs and MLCs?
* Answer:
* MLAs: Members of the Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha).
* MLCs: Members of the Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad).

(e) How is the Chief Minister appointed?
* Answer: The leader of the majority party in the State Legislative Assembly is appointed as the Chief Minister by the Governor.

7. Long Answer Type Questions

(a) How is the ordinary bill passed in State Legislature?
* Answer: The passage involves three stages called Three Readings:
1. First Reading: Introduction of the bill. The member explains provisions and reasons.
2. Second Reading: General discussion. The bill may be referred to a Select Committee and discussed clause by clause.
3. Third Reading: Voting takes place.
* After passing in the first house, it goes to the second house (if bicameral) for similar stages. Once passed by both, it is sent to the Governor for assent to become an Act/Law. The Council can suggest amendments but cannot reject the bill indefinitely.

(b) Give information about the powers and functions of the Chief Minister.
* Answer:
* Appointment: Appointed by the Governor (Leader of majority party).
* Ministers: Other ministers are appointed by the Governor on the advice of the Chief Minister.
* Portfolios: He assigns/allocates portfolios to ministers.
* Resignation: He can ask any minister to resign at any time.
* Leadership: Heads the government and Council of Ministers.

(c) Write in brief about the powers and functions of the Governor.
* Answer:
* Executive Powers: All executive powers vested in him/her, exercised with aid/advice of Council of Ministers. Appoints Advocate General, Public Service Commission members, and consults President on High Court judges.
* Legislative Powers: Summons sessions, gives assent to bills (bill becomes Act only after approval), promulgate Ordinances.
* Judicial Powers: Grants pardons, reduces or suspends sentences.
* Financial Powers: Sees annual budget is laid, Money bills need prior permission, no demand for grant without recommendation.
* Discretionary Powers: Imposes President's rule, reserves bills for President, appoints CM in hung assembly situations.

(d) Why is the Vidhan Parishad not present in all the states?
* Answer: Not all states have a bicameral legislature. Some states have only one house (Legislative Assembly/Vidhan Sabha), known as a unicameral legislature. Only specific states (like Bihar, Karnataka, etc.) have established a second house called the Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad).

(e) The court gives punishment but the Governor can grant pardon. Find out the judicial powers of the Governor and write about it.
* Answer: The Governor possesses Judicial Powers which include:
* Granting pardons to persons convicted of offenses.
* Reducing or suspending the sentence of any person.
* This acts as a check on the judiciary, providing mercy or correction in specific cases.

## India is a Union of States

India is a Union of States. It means that there is one Union Government and several State governments. It also means that Union (Centre) is more powerful than States. At present, there are 28 States in the Indian Union and each one of them has a Legislature. The Parliament of India is the law-making body at the Union level. In the same way, the State Legislature is a law-making body at state level. Let us explore more about the State government.

## Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)

The legislature in a state consists of the Governor and the Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha) led by the Chief Minister. It represents the people of the state and its powers apply only within the boundaries of a state.

[QR_CODE] [QR_CODE] [QR_CODE] [QR_CODE]

## The state legislature consists of either one or two houses

The state legislature consists of either one or two houses. If there is only one house, it is called unicameral legislature and the house is known as the Legislative Assembly (or the Vidhan Sabha). It is the lower house of the state legislature. There are some states which have the second house also. This house is known as the Legislative Council (or Vidhan Parishad). It is the upper house of the state. States having two houses in the state legislature are called bicameral legislature.

The members of Vidhan Sabha, also called as the Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLA) are directly elected by all adult citizens registered as voters in the State on the basis of universal adult franchise. For this purpose, the state is divided into as many constituencies as the number of members to be elected. Similar to the Lok Sabha, some seats of the state assemblies are reserved for Scheduled Castes (SCs) and Scheduled Tribes (STs). This depends on the population of weaker sections in the state.

The party that wins majority of seats in the state elections forms the government. The parties failing to get majority seats act as opposition.

## Tenure
The tenure of Vidhan Sabha is five years, but the Governor can dissolve it before the completion of its term on the advice of Chief Minister. It may be dissolved by the President on the advice of the Governor in case of constitutional emergency proclaimed under Article 356 of the Constitution. The Assembly can be dissolved if a motion of no confidence is passed against the government. To dissolve the house means that the assembly is no more in existence. Fresh elections are held in the state. This is because people have lost faith in the government.

## Powers and Functions of the State Legislature

The primary function of the State Legislature, like the Union Parliament, is law-making.

The State Legislature is empowered to make laws on the subjects mentioned in the State List which includes subjects like police, agriculture, land revenue, fisheries, irrigation, etc. The State can also make laws on the subjects of Concurrent List but in case of contradiction between the Union and State law, the law made by the Parliament shall prevail.

<!-- Page: 191 -->

The process of making and passing laws is called legislation. A bill is a proposal of law that can originate in either house of the State Assembly. The members of the Legislative Assembly and the Legislative Council; both are involved in the law making procedure.

Steps followed in the process of making a law

Law making is one of the important functions of state Legislature. Any law in its beginning stage is called a bill. There are two kinds of bills—one is the Ordinary Bill and other is the Money Bill. The ordinary bills can be introduced in either of the two houses of the State Legislature—the Vidhan Sabha or the Vidhan Parishad. They can be introduced by any minister. On the other hand, a money bill can be introduced only in the Vidhan Sabha by a minister with the prior consent of the Governor.

Key Question

What are the components of Money Bill?

The passage of an ordinary bill involves three stages called Three Readings.

The First Reading involves the introduction of the bill. The member who introduces the bill explains its provisions and the reason to introduce it.

The Second Reading consists of a general discussion on the proposed bill. The house may refer the bill to the committee known as Select Committee. The bill is discussed clause by clause in the House. This is followed by the Third Reading of the bill. At this stage the voting takes places.

The procedure of such a passage is common to both Houses in a bicameral state legislature. After the passage of the Bill in the Legislative

[IMAGE_START] A person is standing at a podium, holding a piece of paper, speaking into a microphone. Behind them, several people are seated, some of whom are also holding papers. The setting appears to be a formal assembly or legislative session. [IMAGE_END]

Assembly (Vidhan Sabha), it is transmitted to the other House, the Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad). In the second House too, the bill goes through the three stages. Once passed in the second house, the bill is sent for the Governor's assent.

However, the council does not have the power to reject the bill originated in the Assembly. The council can retain the bill for three months from the date of receipt and can return the bill to the Assembly with recommendations or suggestions for amendments, if any. The Assembly may or may not reconsider the recommendations. In case the bill is passed in the Assembly again, with or without changes, it has to be sent back to the council and this time the council can retain the bill for one month but cannot suggest any more changes. Thus the Bill becomes an Act/Law.

In case of the bill, which originates in the Legislative Assembly, the Assembly exercises more power compared to the Council to reject the recommendations of the council at the second stage, if not satisfied with them. Hence the Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha) has relatively more power in the case of Ordinary Bill.

But the procedure of passing the Money Bill is different. The Money Bill is first passed in the Legislative Assembly, and is then sent to the Legislative Council. The Legislative Council

must return the bill within 14 days. If it fails to do so within the stipulated time, the Money Bill is said to be passed and sent to the Governor for his assent.

The Ministers are responsible and accountable to the Legislative Assembly. The members of the Legislative Council can only ask questions. In addition to this, the members of the Legislative Council do not participate in the election of the President of India.

#### Other functions of the State Legislature
- The State Legislature make laws on 61 (originally 66) subjects given in the State List. It also makes laws on 52 (originally 47) subjects given under the Concurrent List.
- It passes the Money Bill and the Budget of the State.
- It passes the Ordinances, issued by the Governor of the state, when the Assembly of the state is not in session.
- Ratification of the amendments made by the Parliament related to the powers of the state government.
- It exercises control over the Executive by asking questions and criticising the wrong policies of the government while giving suggestions.

#### HEAD OF THE STATE
The Governor is the constitutional head of the state. He/She administers with the help of the Chief Minister and his Council of Ministers. The Union Territories are administered by the President. The President appoints a suitable officer for this purpose.

#### Appointment of the Governor
Governor of a state is appointed by the President of India in consultation with the Prime Minister. The President can appoint any person as Governor who is:
- a citizen of India
- not less than 35 years of age
- not a member of the Parliament or any State Legislature, and
- not holding any office of profit under the government.

#### Tenure
The Governor is appointed for a period of 5 years, but he may be removed or shifted to other states during this period. He is accountable to the President of India and holds office during the pleasure of the President. Therefore, the Governor acts not only as the Constitutional head of the State but also as agent of the Central Government in the state.

## The State Government (Administrative Structure)

### The Governor
- Constitutional Head of the State
- Appointed by the President of India for five years
- Can be removed from office even before this period
- All the decisions of the state are taken in his name.

### The Legislative Assembly (or the Lower House or Vidhan Sabha)
- Highest representative body of the state
- Some states in India only have Legislative Assembly.
- The strength depends on the population of the state.
- Any Indian citizen 25 years or above, who is mentally sound and solvent can be a member of the Legislative Assembly.
- Can have maximum of 500 and minimum 60 members.
- Elected members are called MLAs.
- Members are elected for a period of five years.
- Party having majority of members in the house forms the government.
- They elect their leader as Chief Minister who heads the government.
- Elected members elect a Speaker to preside over its sittings and maintain order in the house.
- Both Ordinary and Money Bill can be introduced here.

### The Legislative Council (or the Upper House)
- Some states in India have the Legislative Council.
- It is a permanent House and is not subjected to dissolution.
- Its members are called the MLCs.
- The tenure of MLCs is for six years.
- One-third of its members retire often every two years.
- Any Indian citizen, who is 30 years of age, and not a bankrupt and mentally unsound, can be a member of the Legislative Council.
- 1/6th of the members are nominated by the governor.
- Money Bills cannot be introduced in this House. Ordinary bills can originate here.
- The House can hold the Money Bill for only 14 days, thereafter the bill is considered passed by the House.
- The members of the Legislative Council elect Chairman to take care of the proceedings of the House.
