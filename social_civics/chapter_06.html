SOCIAL ASPECTS OF UNPACKING GENDER ROLE

  • Gender roles develop through internalisation and identification during childhood.
  • Toys and play patterns often reinforce gender norms (e.g., dolls for girls, vehicles for boys).
  • These internalised norms lead to differential treatment between males and females.
  • Distinctions made between sexes result in gender inequality in daily life.

Gender Discrimination in School

  • Schools are integral parts of society but often perpetuate gender discrimination.
  • Girl children are often not allowed to continue education, especially in rural areas.
  • There is an alarming dropout rate of girls in primary education compared to boys.
  • Neglecting the girl child destroys the possibility of raising a mentally strong individual.
  • Schools can establish equality by ensuring safety, encouraging participation, and fair treatment.

The Reinforcement of Male Dominance

  • Male dominance exists at all levels, from interpersonal interactions to cultural institutions.
  • Language perpetuates dominance by using 'he' for unspecified persons and 'mankind' for humanity.
  • Media portrays men as active and aggressive, while women are shown as quiet and submissive.
  • Women are often objectified in films and hired for topics like weather or culture.
  • Occupational segregation assigns jobs based on gender stereotypes rather than capability.

Economic Aspect of Gender Bias

  • Wage discrimination occurs when wages differ between groups due to bias despite equivalent characteristics.
  • Occupational segregation distributes people across jobs according to gender (e.g., nurses vs engineers).
  • Some occupations remain male-dominated (pilots, engineers) while others are female-dominated (teachers, secretaries).
  • Gender inequality persists in different spheres of social and economic life.

Glass Ceiling

  • The term refers to situations where advancement within an organisation is deliberately stopped at a lower level.
  • It is based on discrimination, most commonly sex or racism.
  • 'Glass' refers to the transparent limitation which is unwritten and unofficial.
  • It was coined by Marilyen Loden to refer to invisible barriers in career advancement for women.

Types of Glass Ceiling Barriers

  • Different pay for comparable work.
  • Sexual, ethnic, racial, religious discrimination or harassment at the workplace.
  • Lack of family-friendly workplace policies.

Effects of Glass Ceiling

  • The gender wage gap is a major indicator of inequality between males and females.
  • Women face difficulty acquiring managerial positions even in female-oriented fields.
  • Inequalities are embedded within the social hierarchy regarding leadership roles.
  • Women often have to be highly impressive to be considered eligible for leadership roles compared to men.
  • Women may choose jobs based on factors like childcare duties rather than pay.

Reverse Glass Ceiling

  • A new phenomenon where men start careers in female-dominated industries like nursing or childcare.
  • Men in these fields may face discrimination or rapid advancement known as the "glass escalator".
  • Experts debate whether this concept truly exists as it is still in its infantile growth stage.
  • Biological differences are sometimes used to justify roles allotted to genders unfairly.

Insight: Interesting Facts

  • Worldwide, more than 110 million children are not enrolled in schools, nearly 60% are girls.
  • By age 18, girls have received an average of 4.4 years less education than boys.
  • Pregnancies and childbirth related health problems cause death of nearly 1,46,000 teenage girls each year.
  • At least one in three girls and women worldwide has been physically or sexually abused in her lifetime.

GENDER EQUALITY: THE CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS

  • The Constitution of India states gender equality is a Fundamental Right in its preamble.
  • It empowers the state to adopt measures of positive discrimination in favour of women.
  • India ratified various international conventions, such as the convention on elimination of all forms of discrimination against women in 1993.
  • The Prenatal Diagnostic Test Act (1994) seeks to end sex-determination tests and female foeticide.
  • Women have found places in local governance structures, overcoming some gender bias.

Women Movement for Equality Across the World

  • The Government announced the National Policy for Empowerment of Women in 2001.
  • A draft policy outlines the state's response to problems of gender discrimination.
  • A girl child is vulnerable to human right violations and requires additional protection.
  • Low value placed on girls subjects them to exclusion, exploitation, and violence.
  • Strict laws protect women from sexual harassment at the workplace, home, and educational institutions.

5. Very Short Answer Type Questions

(a) What is gender?
Answer: Gender refers to the socially constructed differences between men and women. Unlike sex, which is biological, gender involves varying attitudes and roles assigned by society.

(b) What is gender equality?
Answer: Gender equality means providing equal opportunity to all school children, whether a boy or a girl. It ensures that girls feel equally safe, are encouraged to participate in activities, and are treated fairly without bias.

(c) What do you mean by patriarchy?
Answer: Patriarchy indicates a system of social structures and practices where men dominate, oppress, and exploit women. It manifests in preferences for sons, discrimination against daughters, and lack of inheritance for women.

(d) Mention any two female dominated fields where glass-ceiling is still present.
Answer: Two female-dominated fields where the glass ceiling is still present are Education and Nursing/Teaching. Women often face difficulty acquiring managerial positions even in these fields.

(e) What does Pre-natal Diagnostic Act prohibit?
Answer: The Pre-natal Diagnostic Test Act prohibits doctors from conducting procedures for the specific purpose of determining the sex of the foetus. It seeks to end sex-determination tests and female foeticide.

6. Short Answer Type Questions

(a) Discuss any three major areas of discrimination.
Answer: Major areas of discrimination include:
1. At Home: Preference given to the son, discrimination against the daughter in food distribution, and burden of household work on girls.
2. Education: Lack of educational opportunities for girls, high dropout rates, and restricted mobility for girls.
3. Workplace: Unequal pay for equal work, limited subjects of study for girls, and discrimination in promotions for women in offices.

(b) Give reasons why boys are given more importance than girls in many societies.
Answer: Boys are often given more importance due to:
1. Patriarchal Systems: A social structure where men dominate and women are expected to be submissive.
2. Economic Beliefs: Belief that sons will support parents in old age, whereas daughters marry out.
3. Social Norms: Deep-rooted traditions valuing males for carrying forward family lineage and inheritance.

(c) How does the Constitution of India ensure gender equality?
Answer: The Constitution ensures gender equality through:
1. Preamble: Stating gender equality is a Fundamental Right.
2. Positive Discrimination: Empowering the state to adopt measures in favour of women via legislation.
3. International Conventions: Ratifying conventions like the elimination of all forms of discrimination against women (1993).
4. Laws: Passing acts like the Prenatal Diagnostic Test Act to prevent female foeticide.

(d) How is a girl child vulnerable to human right violation. State with examples.
Answer: A girl child is vulnerable due to low value placed on her life, subjecting her to exclusion, exploitation, and violence.
Examples:
1. Female Foeticide: Being denied the right to be born.
2. Denial of Education: Being denied access to medical care and education.
3. Violence: Facing abuse and discrimination on grounds of caste, disability, or social origin.

7. Long Answer Type Questions

(a) Highlight the economic aspect of gender bias.
Answer: The economic aspect of gender bias includes:
1. Wage Discrimination: Discrepancy in wages between men and women for equivalent work due to bias.
2. Occupational Segregation: Distribution of people across occupations according to gender (e.g., nurses/teachers are female-dominated; engineers/pilots are male-dominated).
3. Glass Ceiling: Qualified women face invisible barriers preventing upward advancement in organizational hierarchies, limiting their earning potential and promotions.

(b) To what extent is the gender discrimination declining? Give examples.
Answer: Gender discrimination is showing signs of decline but remains prevalent.
Declining Signs:
1. Reservation: Introduction of 33% reservation for women in local and village level bodies has empowered them politically.
2. Laws: Stricter laws against sexual harassment, dowry deaths, and rape have been implemented.
3. Employment: More women are working in diverse professions (doctors, engineers, scientists).
Persistent Issues:
1. Violence: Newspapers still report incidents of rape, eve-teasing, and murder.
2. Bias: Violence against women remains high despite having a woman President, indicating a contradiction in social progress.

(c) Explain the concept of glass-ceiling and reverse glass-ceiling.
Answer:
* Glass Ceiling: Refers to situations where the advancement of a qualified person (usually women) within an organisation's hierarchy is deliberately stopped at a lower level due to discrimination (sex/racism). It is an invisible, unwritten barrier.
* Reverse Glass Ceiling (Glass Escalator): A new phenomenon where men start careers in female-dominated industries (like nursing, childcare) and experience rapid advancement into positions of authority, often facing less discrimination than women in similar roles.

(d) There is great percentage of girls dropouts in the school education at the rural level. Why is it so? (HOTS)
Answer: High dropout rates among girls in rural areas are due to:
1. Parental Attitudes: Parents often do not favour sending girl children to school, especially up to the primary level.
2. Economic Factors: Poverty forces families to prioritise boys' education.
3. Safety and Mobility: Restricted mobility and safety concerns discourage sending girls to school.
4. Household Burden: Girls are often kept home for household work instead of studying.
5. Community: Among tribals and minority communities, this situation is particularly disturbing.

(e) What would be the effect of gender bias on the women if we do not stop it? (HOTS)
Answer: If gender bias continues:
1. Societal Damage: Neglecting the girl child destroys the possibility of creating mentally strong, sensitive, and socially responsible individuals.
2. Leadership Gap: Women will continue to be capped in promotions, lacking representation in leadership roles.
3. Economic Loss: Gender wage gaps and occupational segregation will persist, keeping women economically dependent.
4. Inequality: Inequalities will remain embedded within the social hierarchy, reflecting negative perceptions of women in leadership.

8. Assertion and Reason Based Questions

Assertion (A): Discrimination may entail behaviour either to benefit an individual or harm the individual concerned.
Reason (R): Discrimination is associated with prejudice and regarded as objectionable.

Answer: (a) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A.
Explanation: Both statements are factually correct according to the chapter. Discrimination stems from prejudice (R), which leads to behaviours that can either benefit or harm individuals (A). The prejudice is the underlying cause of the discriminatory behaviour.

### EQUAL RIGHTS ADVOCATES
[IMAGE_START] A group of women in uniform holding a banner with the text "EQUAL RIGHTS ADVOCATES". [IMAGE_END]

Gender as a term refers to the differentiation or varying attitudes between persons on the basis of their sex. There are two kinds of differences between men and women, i.e., sex and gender. Sex signifies the physical and biological differences between males and females which is a natural phenomenon. On the other hand, gender refers to the socially constructed differences between men and women. These ideas further give way to the exploitation and discrimination against women.

Discrimination is associated with prejudice and regarded as objectionable. It may entail behaviour either to benefit an individual or harm the individual concerned. Gender discrimination refers to a belief in relation to gender of a person which may or may not carry legal consequences but is of social nature.

When we talk of gender discrimination, we talk about patriarchy which indicates a system of social structures and practices where men dominate, oppress and exploit women. It manifests itself in preference for a son, discrimination against daughter, in food distribution, burden of household work, lack of opportunities for girls, lack of educational opportunities, restricted mobility, acts of domestic violence, lack of inheritance to women, etc.

## Glass Ceiling

The term 'glass ceiling' refers to situations where the advancement of a qualified person within the hierarchy of an organisation is deliberately stopped at a lower level because of some form of discrimination, most commonly based on sex or racism.

This situation is referred to as a 'ceiling' as there is a limitation, blocking upward advancement, 'Glass' (transparent) refers to the limitation which is not immediately apparent and is normally an unwritten and unofficial policy. The term 'glass ceiling' has been thought to have first been used to refer to invisible barriers that create obstacles in the career advancement of women in the workforce of developed countries.

The term glass ceiling was originally specifically applied to discrimination against women.

### GENDER EQUALITY: THE CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS

The Constitution of India states in its preamble that gender equality is a Fundamental Right. It also empowers the state to adopt measures of positive discrimination in favour of women by way of legislation and policies.

India has also ratified various international conventions and human rights to secure equal rights for women, such as ratification of convention on elimination of all forms of discrimination against women in 1993. Women have been finding a place in local governance structures, overcoming gender bias.

The passing of Prenatal Diagnostic Test Act in 1994 is a step in removing gender discrimination. This Act seeks to end sex-determination tests and female foeticide and prohibits doctors from conducting such procedures for the specific purpose of determining the sex of the foetus.
