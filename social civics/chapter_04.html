Chapter 04

Chapter Summary

Key Learning Objectives

  • Understand the three levels of government: Central, State, and Local
  • Learn about the composition of a state government
  • Differentiate between the Vidhan Sabha and Vidhan Parishad
  • Know the powers and functions of the Governor and Chief Minister

Structure of State Government

India is a union of states, meaning there is one Union Government and several State Governments, with the Union being more powerful than the states.

Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)

  • The lower house of the state legislature
  • Represents the people of the state
  • Members (MLAs) are directly elected by adult citizens through universal adult franchise
  • Number of members ranges from 60 to 500; small states may have fewer
  • Each member is elected from a constituency
  • Seats reserved for Scheduled Castes (SCs) and Scheduled Tribes (STs)
  • Party with majority of seats forms the government; others become opposition
  • The Speaker is elected by MLAs to preside over meetings

Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad)

  • The upper house in bicameral state legislatures
  • Not present in all states
  • Members (MLCs) are partly elected and partly nominated
  • One-third elected by members of the Vidhan Sabha
  • One-third elected by local authorities (municipalities, district boards)
  • One-twelfth elected by graduates and teachers
  • One-sixth nominated by the Governor
  • Tenure is six years, with one-third retiring every two years
  • The Chairman is elected by members to preside over proceedings

Bicameral vs Unicameral Legislatures

  • Bicameral: States with two houses (e.g., Karnataka, Maharashtra)
  • Unicameral: States with only one house (e.g., Tamil Nadu, West Bengal)

Powers and Functions of the State Legislature

  • Primary function: Law-making
  • Can make laws on subjects in the State List (e.g., police, agriculture, land revenue, fisheries, irrigation)
  • Can make laws on subjects in the Concurrent List (e.g., health, education) but if there is conflict with Union law, Union law prevails
  • Passes the Money Bill and the State Budget
  • Passes Ordinances when the Assembly is not in session
  • Exercises control over the executive by asking questions and criticizing policies
  • Ratifies amendments made by Parliament related to state powers

Role of the Governor

  • Constitutional head of the state
  • Appointed by the President of India for 5 years (can be removed earlier)
  • Tenure is during the pleasure of the President
  • Powers include:
  • Summoning and proroguing sessions of the legislature
  • Dissolving the Vidhan Sabha on advice of the Chief Minister
  • Appointing the Chief Minister (leader of majority party) and other ministers
  • Appointing the Advocate General, Chairman, and members of the State Public Service Commission
  • Granting pardons and reducing sentences
  • Promulgating ordinances when the Assembly is not in session
  • Reserving bills for the President's consideration in case of constitutional emergency

Role of the Chief Minister

  • Appointed by the Governor, based on the party with majority in the Vidhan Sabha
  • Responsible to the Legislative Assembly
  • Assigns portfolios to ministers and can ask them to resign
  • Leads the Council of Ministers and is responsible for government policy and administration

Process of Making a Law

  1. A bill is introduced in either house of the legislature.
  2. Ordinary Bill:
  3. Passes through three readings:
    • First Reading: Introduction and explanation
    • Second Reading: General discussion and committee review
    • Third Reading: Voting
  4. In bicameral states, it must pass both houses. The Council can suggest amendments, but the Assembly can reject them.
  5. Money Bill:
  6. Introduced only in the Vidhan Sabha by a minister with prior consent of the Governor
  7. Sent to the Vidhan Parishad, which has 14 days to respond
  8. If not responded to, it is considered passed and sent to the Governor for assent

Key Terms

  • Constituencies: Areas where voters elect representatives
  • Presiding: To conduct or manage proceedings
  • Majority: More than half of the seats
  • Constitutional Emergency: Situation that allows the Union Government to take control
  • President’s Rule: Suspension of the state government and imposition of Union rule

Special Features

  • The concept of live telecasting, web casting, and digitalization of legislative proceedings was first initiated by the Andhra Pradesh Legislative Assembly.
  • In the absence of a clear majority, the Governor has discretionary powers to appoint a Chief Minister who can prove majority in the Assembly.

This chapter provides a clear understanding of how state governments function, including the roles of elected representatives, legislative processes, and key constitutional figures.

Short Revision Notes

  • India is a Union of States with one Union Government and several State governments
  • The Union Government is more powerful than State governments
  • There are 28 states in the Indian Union, each with a legislature
  • The State Legislature consists of either one (unicameral) or two (bicameral) houses
  • The Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha) is the lower house; the Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad) is the upper house
  • Members of Vidhan Sabha (MLAs) are directly elected by adult citizens through universal adult franchise
  • The number of MLAs ranges from 60 to 500, depending on state population
  • The party with majority of seats in the Legislative Assembly forms the government
  • The head of the state government is the Chief Minister, appointed by the Governor
  • The presiding officer of the Vidhan Sabha is the Speaker
  • The tenure of the Vidhan Sabha is five years, but the Governor can dissolve it earlier
  • A bill becomes an Act after being passed by both houses and receiving the Governor's assent
  • Ordinary bills can be introduced in either house; Money Bills can only be introduced in the Vidhan Sabha
  • Money Bills must be passed by the Legislative Assembly and may be returned by the Legislative Council within 14 days
  • The Governor is the constitutional head of the state, appointed by the President for five years
  • The Governor can dissolve the Assembly, promulgate ordinances, and reserve bills for the President's consideration
  • The Governor has discretionary powers, including imposing President’s rule in emergencies
  • The Chief Minister appoints other ministers on advice, allocates portfolios, and can remove ministers
  • The Legislative Council is a permanent house with a six-year tenure; one-third of its members retire every two years
  • MLCs are elected or nominated; 1/6th are nominated by the Governor
  • The State Legislature makes laws on subjects in the State List and Concurrent List
  • The State Legislature passes the state budget and exercises control over the executive through questioning
  • Constitutionally, the real head of a democratic country is the people, through elected representatives

Key Terms

Bicameral legislature — States having two houses in the state legislature.
Chief Minister — The leader of the majority party in the State Legislative Assembly appointed by the Governor.
Concurrent List — Subjects on which both Union and State governments can make laws.
Constituencies — The district and the residents of a district who elect a legislative or an official.
Dissolve — To dismiss; when the assembly is no more in existence.
Executive Powers — Powers vested in the Governor exercised with the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers.
Legislative Assembly — The lower house of the state legislature representing the people of the state.
Legislative Council — The upper house of the state legislature in states having two houses.
Legislation — The process of making and passing laws.
Mentally Sound — One who is able to understand one's actions and distinguish right from wrong.
Money Bill — A bill that can be introduced only in the Vidhan Sabha by a minister with the prior consent of the Governor.
Ordinary Bill — A bill that can be introduced in either of the two houses of the State Legislature.
President's Rule — The suspension of state government and imposition of direct Union government rule in a state.
Presiding — To exercise control over meetings and conduct proceedings.
Solvent — One who is able to pay one's debts.
Speaker — The presiding officer of the House elected by its members.
State List — Subjects like police, agriculture, and land revenue on which the State Legislature makes laws.
Tenure — The fixed period for which an office or body is held, such as five years for the Vidhan Sabha.
Unicameral legislature — A legislature consisting of only one house known as the Legislative Assembly.
Universal Adult Franchise — The basis on which all adult citizens registered as voters elect representatives.
Vidhan Parishad — Another name for the Legislative Council, the upper house of the state legislature.
Vidhan Sabha — Another name for the Legislative Assembly, the lower house of the state legislature.

5. Very Short Answer Type Questions

(a) What percentage of the members of the Legislative Council are nominated by the Governor?
One-sixth (1/6) of the members of the Legislative Council are nominated by the Governor.

(b) Who is the real head in a democratic country?
The people (or the people through their elected representatives). In a democratic system, the people are the real head as they elect the leaders.

(c) Who is the presiding officer of Legislative Assembly?
The Speaker of the Legislative Assembly.

(d) What do you mean by majority?
Majority means more than half of the total seats. A party or individual has a majority if it holds more than 50% of the seats.

(e) What is a Legislative Assembly?
A Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha) is the lower house of the state legislature that represents the people of the state and is responsible for making laws on state subjects.

6. Short Answer Type Questions

(a) How is a member of a Legislative Assembly elected?
Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs) are elected by the people of the state through direct elections based on the universal adult franchise. The state is divided into constituencies, and each constituency elects one MLA.

(b) What are the differences between a Money Bill and an Ordinary Bill?
| Feature | Money Bill | Ordinary Bill |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Origin | Can only be introduced in the Vidhan Sabha | Can be introduced in either house |
| Purpose | Relates to the state’s finances | Covers various subjects including law, agriculture, education, etc. |
| Approval | Must be passed by the Legislative Council within 14 days; if not, it is deemed passed | Can be discussed and passed in both houses |
| Power | Cannot be rejected by the Legislative Council | Can be rejected or modified by the Council |

(c) List any two discretionary powers of the Governor.
1. The power to dissolve the State Legislative Assembly on the advice of the Chief Minister.
2. The power to reserve a bill for the President’s consideration in case of disputes or emergencies.

(d) Who are called the MLAs and MLCs?
- MLA stands for Member of Legislative Assembly – elected representative of a constituency in the Vidhan Sabha.
- MLC stands for Member of Legislative Council – representative of the upper house (Vidhan Parishad).

(e) How is the Chief Minister appointed?
The Chief Minister is appointed by the Governor, typically the leader of the majority party in the Legislative Assembly. The Governor considers the party that has the majority and appoints its leader as the Chief Minister.

7. Long Answer Type Questions

(a) How is the ordinary bill passed in State Legislature?
An ordinary bill follows three stages known as Three Readings:
1. First Reading: The bill is introduced in the Assembly. The member who introduces it explains its provisions and justification.
2. Second Reading: The bill is discussed in general terms. A Select Committee may be formed to examine the bill clause by clause.
3. Third Reading: The final discussion and voting take place. If passed by the house, it is sent to the other house (in bicameral states). After passing in the second house, the bill goes to the Governor for assent and becomes an Act.

(b) Give information about the powers and functions of the Chief Minister.
- Appointed by the Governor, the Chief Minister is the head of the state government.
- Appoints other ministers on the advice of the Chief Minister.
- Allocates portfolios to ministers.
- Can remove any minister at any time.
- Responsible to the Legislative Assembly and must face a confidence motion if the Assembly loses confidence.
- Leads the government and represents the state in official functions.

(c) Write in brief about the powers and functions of the Governor.
- The Governor is the constitutional head of the state.
- Summon, prorogue, and dissolve the state legislature.
- Signs bills into law after they are passed by the legislature.
- Can promulgate ordinances when the legislature is not in session.
- Can grant pardons and reduce sentences.
- Exercises discretionary powers, such as dissolving the assembly or reserving bills for the President.
- Acts as a link between the Centre and State government.

(d) Why is the Vidhan Parishad not present in all the states?
- Some states have only one house (unicameral) because they are smaller in population or size.
- The presence of a Legislative Council adds complexity and cost to governance.
- States like Rajasthan, Gujarat, and Tamil Nadu have bicameral legislatures, but others like Maharashtra, Karnataka, and West Bengal have unicameral systems.
- The decision is based on historical, political, and administrative factors.

(e) The court gives punishment but the Governor can grant pardon. Find out the judicial powers of the Governor and write about it.
- The Governor has judicial powers, including the power to grant pardons, reduce sentences, or suspend punishments.
- This power is granted under Article 161 of the Constitution.
- The Governor can pardon a person convicted by a court, reduce the punishment, or suspend the sentence.
- This power is a check on the judicial system and ensures mercy and fairness.
- It is discretionary and exercised only in cases of public interest or individual hardship.
