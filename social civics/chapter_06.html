Chapter 6: Gender Discrimination – Summary

What is Gender?

  • Sex refers to biological differences between males and females (e.g., chromosomes, reproductive organs).
  • Gender refers to the social and cultural roles, behaviours, and expectations associated with being male or female.
  • Gender is not just biological—it is shaped by society and culture.

What is Gender Discrimination?

  • It is unequal treatment based on gender, often rooted in prejudice and patriarchy.
  • Gender discrimination includes:
  • Preference for sons over daughters
  • Unequal access to education and healthcare
  • Domestic violence
  • Lack of inheritance rights
  • Limited job opportunities and lower wages

Patriarchy

  • A social system where men dominate, oppress, and exploit women.
  • Manifestations:
  • Discrimination in food distribution
  • Burden of household work on women
  • Lack of mobility for girls
  • Child marriage
  • Female foeticide and infanticide
  • Dowry system and acid attacks

Gender Inequality in Schools

  • Girls face higher dropout rates (about 30% more than boys).
  • Parents in rural and tribal areas often do not send girls to school.
  • Girls are not equally safe, encouraged, or valued in school activities.
  • Solution: Schools must promote gender equality by:
  • Ensuring safety for girls
  • Encouraging equal participation in extracurriculars
  • Providing unbiased treatment and building confidence

Gender Roles and Media

  • Children internalize gender roles through toys and media:
  • Girls given dolls and kitchen sets
  • Boys given construction and vehicles
  • Media often portrays:
  • Men as active, aggressive, and powerful
  • Women as quiet, passive, and decorative
  • Women are often shown in passive roles (e.g., weather reports) or objectified in films and soaps

Economic Aspects of Gender Bias

  • Gender wage gap: Women earn less than men for similar work.
  • Occupational segregation:
  • Women dominate teaching, nursing, secretarial work
  • Men dominate engineering, aviation, and leadership roles
  • Women face barriers to promotions and leadership roles due to:
  • Societal stereotypes
  • Lack of family-friendly policies
  • Higher performance standards expected of them

Glass Ceiling

  • A hidden barrier that blocks women’s career advancement.
  • It is not written in law but is unofficial and deeply embedded in workplace culture.
  • Types of glass ceiling barriers:
  • Different pay for equal work
  • Discrimination based on gender, race, or religion
  • Lack of family-friendly policies

Reverse Glass Ceiling

  • A growing phenomenon where men enter female-dominated fields (like nursing or childcare).
  • These men face discrimination and are often not accepted due to gender stereotypes.
  • Some experts call it a "glass escalator"—a rise in men’s positions in traditionally women’s roles.
  • Still debated—some say it is not fully established.

Constitutional Provisions for Gender Equality

  • The Preamble of the Indian Constitution declares gender equality as a fundamental right.
  • The government has:
  • Ratified the Convention on Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) in 1993
  • Passed the Prenatal Diagnostic Test (PDT) Act, 1994 to stop sex determination and female foeticide
  • Introduced 33% reservation for women in local and village bodies (a major step toward empowerment)

Women’s Movements in India

  • Women’s movements have fought for:
  • Protection from violence (e.g., rape, dowry deaths)
  • Equal rights in education, employment, and healthcare
  • Legal reforms (e.g., stricter laws on rape and dowry)
  • Key outcomes:
  • Stronger laws against gender-based violence
  • Women now hold leadership positions in local governance
  • Increased awareness and participation in social change

Key Facts and Statistics

  • Over 110 million children are out of school, with 60% being girls.
  • By age 18, girls receive 4.4 years less education than boys.
  • Nearly 146,000 teenage girls die each year due to pregnancy and childbirth complications.
  • At least one in three girls and women worldwide have faced physical or sexual abuse.

Important Terms

  • Patriarchy: A system where men dominate and control women.
  • Discrimination: Unequal treatment based on gender.
  • Prejudice: Unfair or unreasonable beliefs about a group.
  • Glass ceiling: An invisible barrier blocking women’s career growth.
  • Reverse glass ceiling: When men enter traditionally female-dominated jobs and face discrimination.
  • Reservation: Reserved seats for women in local bodies.
  • Vulnerable: Easily harmed or exploited due to gender, caste, or social status.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:
- Define gender and gender discrimination
- Identify gender inequalities in social and economic spheres
- Explain the concept of glass ceiling and its types
- List constitutional provisions promoting gender equality
- Name key women’s movements and their outcomes

Key Terms

prejudice — unreasonable or unfair attitude toward a group or individual based on gender, race, or other characteristics

vulnerability — being at risk of harm, abuse, or exploitation due to gender, social status, or other factors
violations — acts of breaking laws or rights, such as abuse, harassment, or discrimination against women

Exercise 5: Very Short Answer Type Questions

(a) What is gender?
Gender refers to the socially constructed differences between men and women, based on cultural, social, and psychological norms, not just biological sex.

(b) What is gender equality?
Gender equality means equal rights, opportunities, and treatment for all individuals regardless of gender in all spheres of life — education, employment, health, and decision-making.

(c) What do you mean by patriarchy?
Patriarchy is a social system where men hold primary power and dominate in family, work, and societal institutions, often leading to the oppression and exploitation of women.

(d) Mention any two female-dominated fields where glass-ceiling is still present.
1. Teaching
2. Nursing

Explanation: Despite being female-dominated, women face barriers in acquiring managerial or leadership positions.

(e) What does Pre-natal Diagnostic Act prohibit?
The Pre-natal Diagnostic Test (PNDT) Act prohibits doctors from conducting sex determination tests on fetuses and bans female foeticide.

Exercise 6: Short Answer Type Questions

(a) Discuss any three major areas of discrimination.
1. At home: Preference for sons, discrimination in food distribution, lack of educational opportunities for girls.
2. In education: High dropout rate of girls, especially in rural areas, due to poverty, early marriage, or family preference for boys.
3. In employment: Gender-based wage gap, lack of promotions, occupational segregation, and limited access to leadership roles.

(b) Give reasons why boys are given more importance than girls in many societies.
- Cultural beliefs that sons will carry the family name and continue the lineage.
- Sons are seen as contributors to household income and property.
- Girls are often considered less capable or less valuable in traditional roles.
- Patriarchal systems prioritize male dominance in family and society.

(c) How does the Constitution of India ensure gender equality?
- The Preamble declares gender equality as a fundamental right.
- The Constitution empowers the state to adopt positive discrimination policies in favour of women.
- It mandates the prohibition of sex determination and female foeticide through the PNDT Act.
- It provides for 33% reservation of seats for women in local bodies.
- It protects women from sexual harassment through the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013.

(d) How is a girl child vulnerable to human right violation? State with examples.
A girl child is vulnerable due to:
- Early marriage: Girls are married off at young ages, violating their right to education and personal development.
- Child marriage and dowry deaths: Girls are killed or abused due to dowry demands.
- Female foeticide: Parents may abort female fetuses due to gender bias.
- Lack of access to education: Girls are often denied schooling.
- Physical and sexual abuse: Girls face abuse in homes, schools, or during migration.
- Neglect of health: Lack of access to maternal healthcare during pregnancy and childbirth.

Exercise 7: Long Answer Type Questions

(a) Highlight the economic aspect of gender bias.
Gender bias in the economy manifests in several ways:
- Wage disparity: Women earn less than men for the same work.
- Occupational segregation: Women are concentrated in low-paid, low-status jobs like teaching, nursing, and secretarial work, while men dominate high-paying fields like engineering, defence, and management.
- Glass ceiling: Women face barriers in promotions and leadership roles, even in female-dominated fields.
- Work-life imbalance: Women often take more time off due to childcare responsibilities, leading to lower workforce participation.
- Limited access to financial independence: Women have less access to property, loans, and financial decision-making.
- Underrepresentation in entrepreneurship and high-level decision-making.

These economic disparities reinforce gender inequality and limit women’s financial autonomy and social mobility.

(b) To what extent is the gender discrimination declining? Give examples.
Gender discrimination is declining in several areas:
- Education: More girls are attending school, especially in urban and rural areas. The dropout rate has decreased due to government schemes like the Right to Education Act.
- Legal protections: Laws like the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act (2005), Sexual Harassment (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act (2013), and PNDT Act have strengthened legal safeguards.
- Political representation: Women now hold key positions — including the President of India (Dr. Droupadi Murmu) and many state-level leaders.
- 33% reservation: Women’s participation in local governance has increased significantly.
- Media representation: Women are increasingly portrayed in diverse roles in films, news, and public life.

However, challenges remain — especially in rural areas, in family decisions, and in workplace discrimination.

(c) Explain the concept of glass-ceiling and reverse glass-ceiling.
- Glass Ceiling: A metaphorical barrier that prevents women from advancing to higher leadership positions in organizations, despite their qualifications. It is often due to unconscious bias, lack of mentorship, or societal expectations. For example, women in teaching or nursing may not be promoted to head positions.
- Reverse Glass Ceiling: A phenomenon where men enter traditionally female-dominated fields (like nursing, childcare, or teaching) and face discrimination or exclusion. For instance, men in nursing may be seen as "out of place" or not "qualified" due to gender stereotypes. Some experts call this the "glass escalator" — a rise in men's advancement in female-dominated sectors, but still facing resistance.

While the glass ceiling is widely recognized, the reverse glass ceiling is a newer, less acknowledged form of gender bias.

(d) There is a great percentage of girls dropouts in school education at the rural level. Why is it so? (HOTS)
- Poverty: Families cannot afford school fees, uniforms, or transport.
- Early marriage: Girls are married off at young ages, leading to school dropout.
- Lack of female teachers or school infrastructure: Rural schools often lack qualified female teachers or safe learning environments.
- Cultural beliefs: Sons are considered more valuable; parents prefer to send boys to school.
- Gender bias in household responsibilities: Girls are expected to help with domestic work and care for younger siblings.
- Lack of access to sanitation and safe toilets in schools, especially for girls.
- Fear of harassment or violence in schools or at home.

These factors combine to create a hostile environment for girls' education in rural areas.

(e) What would be the effect of gender bias on women if we do not stop it? (HOTS)
If gender bias continues unchecked, the following consequences will occur:
- Economic stagnation: Women will remain underemployed, underpaid, and excluded from decision-making.
- Social instability: Gender-based violence, child marriage, and female foeticide will continue, leading to high rates of poverty and illiteracy.
- Health risks: Women will face higher risks of maternal mortality, mental health issues, and reproductive health problems.
- Weakened democracy: Women’s lack of political participation will limit inclusive governance.
- Intergenerational cycle of inequality: Girls will grow up in environments where gender discrimination is normal, perpetuating the cycle.
- Reduced national development: A society that fails to empower women cannot achieve sustainable economic or social progress.

Thus, stopping gender bias is essential for building a just, equitable, and prosperous society.

Assertion and Reason Based Questions

Assertion (A): Discrimination may entail behaviour either to benefit an individual or harm the individual concerned.
Reason (R): Discrimination is associated with prejudice and regarded as objectionable.

✅ Answer: (a) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A.
Explanation: Discrimination can be harmful (e.g., denying jobs) or sometimes beneficial (e.g., preferential treatment based on bias), but it is always rooted in prejudice and is considered socially unacceptable.

Analytical Skills

Question: Do women still consider inferior to men? Has there been change in women’s status in India?

✅ Answer:
- Yes, gender bias still exists, especially in rural areas, family structures, and traditional beliefs. Women are still often seen as secondary in decision-making.
- But significant progress has been made:
- Women hold key government positions (e.g., President, Union Ministers).
- Legal protections against harassment and violence are strong.
- Education and employment opportunities have improved.
- Women are increasingly active in politics, business, and media.
- Latest data (as of 2024):
- Female literacy rate: ~70% (up from ~50% in 1990).
- Women in workforce: ~35% of total workforce.
- Women in parliament: ~15% (increasing).
- Gender wage gap: ~16% (still significant).
- Child marriage rate: Declining (from 30% in 2000 to ~10% in 2023).

👉 Conclusion: While progress is evident, deep-rooted gender bias remains. Ongoing efforts are needed to achieve true gender equality.
